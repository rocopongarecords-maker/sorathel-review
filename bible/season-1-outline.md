## THE SHAPE

Season 1 is Vaelen's story — the brilliant, magnetic man whose warmth is a survival mechanism and whose gift shows him things he doesn't trust himself to act on. By the end of the season, the reader has watched him lose the person he loved most, see the flaw in the most powerful system in the world, and choose silence over truth because he doesn't believe his own eyes.

But Vaelen's story lives inside a WORLD that is breaking — and the world has three layers of breaking that the reader experiences through three threat layers, each concealing the next.

**Three acts. Two peaks. One devastating choice. Three threats — only one real.**

### THE PHILOSOPHICAL ENGINE

Every episode is downstream of one question: **"When the world stops telling you who to be, can you survive becoming yourself?"**

The Amarwen network is the most benevolent system imaginable — and it made self-development unnecessary. It gave everyone an identity, a purpose, a place. When it withdraws, the question arrives like a flood. The four responses (destroy, rebuild, freeze, re-perform) are not character types — they're contextual outcomes determined by timing, community, and what options are available to reach for. **Community is a mechanical survival factor, not just a theme.**

The season shows this through escalation: Episode 1 plants the seeds (wrongness beneath normalcy). Episodes 2-3 show the world as alive, beautiful, and beginning to crack — while a visible threat rises from the margins. Episodes 4-5 break the world open (the Dread as the question made literal). Episodes 6-9 show the institutional response — mobilization against the fightable enemy, triumph over the wrong threat, and willful blindness to the real one.

### THE THREAT HIERARCHY

Modeled on GRRM's layered structure: the Lannisters (visible, fightable) vs. the White Walkers (the real thing, barely present in Book 1).

| Layer | Function | Sorathel | When Fully Encountered |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Visible threat** | Fightable. Season arc. | The outer cultures — raiders from beyond the network | Season 1 main fight |
| **Personal antagonists** | Faces the heroes measure against | Amarith Commander (Drenna's Snape) + Amarith Practitioner (Vaelen's Malfoy) | Season 1 onward |
| **The real thing** | What the story is actually about | The Ethura / The Dread | Season 3 full confrontation |

**Season 1 ends with a "victory."** The outer culture threat is contained. The frontier is "secured." The institutions celebrate. The reader knows it's hollow — because the Dread (Episode 5) showed them something the institutions are choosing to ignore. But the characters believe the crisis is over.

**The misattribution:** After Episode 5, institutions attribute the Dread's effects to the outer cultures — "some kind of dead-zone weapon." This prevents anyone from investigating the Dread as its own phenomenon. The Dread zone is perimetered, classified, and forgotten. Season 2: more zones appear. Can't all be contained. Season 3: full confrontation.

## THE OUTER CULTURES — Season 1's War

### Who they are
People from beyond the Amarwen network's reach. Cultures pushed to the margins by the network-sustained civilization — into the dead lands, the spaces the Amarwen didn't cover. They adapted. They survived without the network. They're tougher, harder, and they have capabilities the network-dependent people don't.

The Amarwen's presence was a BOUNDARY. Not a wall — a pressure. Her signal was hostile to these outer cultures. They couldn't enter network territory because the network's presence was physically uncomfortable, disorienting, painful for people not attuned to it. Now the network is retreating. The pressure drops. The boundary moves inward. The outer cultures follow.

### They have WEAPONS
This is terrifying because Sorathel has NO weapons. Peace and order have been sustained by the Amarwen for so long that the concept of organized violence has been FORGOTTEN. Nobody carries weapons. Disputes are resolved through mediation, arbitration, Solvarei intervention. The practitioners are problem-solvers, not fighters. The gray foxes are negotiators, not generals.

The outer cultures carry physical, brutal, practical weapons — blades, projectiles, tools designed exclusively to harm. The people of Sorathel haven't seen anything like this in living memory. Early frontier dispatches describe attacks as "armed with sharpened implements" because the vocabulary of war doesn't exist.

### Not villains
From their perspective, this is long-overdue justice. The network-dependent civilization expanded across the continent on the Amarwen's power, pushing everyone else to the margins. Now the margins are pushing back. "Your tree is dying. We've been waiting."

### The "War on Drugs" framing
Nobody calls it a war. Institutional language minimizes: "frontier incursions," "unauthorized territory occupation," "resource displacement events." Acknowledging the outer cultures as an organized civilization with territorial claims would mean admitting the network-sustained world isn't the only world. A law-enforcement framing applied to a military-scale threat.

### The Outer Culture Leader
Not seen until Episode 6 or 7. Before that: effects only. Raided communities. Strange camps. When the leader appears — a brief encounter — the reader sees a person, not a monster. Calm. A grievance that's real. One line that reframes: "Your tree is dying. We've been waiting." Not revenge. SCHEDULE. They've known this was coming.

## A SOCIETY WITHOUT AN ARMY

Sorathel has no military. No defense force. No organized capacity for violence. When frontier communities are attacked by armed raiders, the institutions have no response mechanism.

### The Solvarei response: operations, not combat
The Solvarei is mobilized to help OPERATIONS — logistics, communication, evacuation, network stabilization. They're consultants, not soldiers. A Solvarei practitioner picking up a weapon would be like an Accenture consultant picking up a rifle.

### The Amarith response: Commander vs. Practitioner
The Amarith Practitioner dismisses raid reports — "a security matter, beneath our scope." He genuinely doesn't comprehend organized violence as a threat. The Commander understands immediately (he grew up in dirt, remembers what the world looks like without guaranteed order) and forces the Practitioner to engage. The Practitioner resents this bitterly — field work, dirty, dangerous, beneath his training.

### The Gray Fox Entrepreneur
A gray fox who's different from the old guard — self-made, restless intelligence, sees problems as opportunities. When the raids become undeniable (after Episode 5), he sees the gap and improvises a protection force:

- Transport creature handlers become scouts and rapid-response teams
- Practitioner tools and construction equipment repurposed as defensive implements
- Frontier people recruited — already hardened by declining conditions
- Called a "protection force" or "frontier response" — NOT an army (the word doesn't exist)

He's solving a real problem AND positioning himself as indispensable. Whoever builds the protection force CONTROLS it. Defense contractor energy — genuinely believes in security AND is getting very rich.

Major figure in Seasons 2-3: the man who gave Sorathel its first army and now controls it.

**This mobilization happens AFTER Episode 5.** The Dread and the escalating raids create enough crisis to justify the unprecedented step of organized defense.

## THE ANTAGONISTS

### Amarith Commander — Drenna's Snape

**Who he is:** Born in dirt. Not a gray fox by birth, not Amarith by heritage. He climbed — through calculation, sacrifice, becoming someone his younger self would barely recognize. He cut people loose. He swallowed humiliation from people he was smarter than. And he ARRIVED. He runs the Amarith. The most powerful private organization in Sorathel.

**His relationship with power:** He's not ABOVE the gray foxes. He's among them the way the CEO of McKinsey is at Davos — a different species of power. The foxes have money, land, heritage, political cunning. He has institutional brilliance, the Amarith's reputation, and systematic intelligence. They're peers. Different kinds of smart at the same table.

**How he enters (Episode 2, Drenna's dinner chapter):** He's at the gray fox gathering. Not there for Drenna — there because the Amarith has business that intersects with the upper class. One drink. Maybe two.

Drenna's mentor introduces her proudly. The Commander cuts him off: "The Amarith doesn't take rotations. We take practitioners who've earned their place through demonstrated excellence at tier. When she's ready, she'll be evaluated. Not before." Meritocracy as brand. The ladder he built, insisted everyone climb the hard way, because that's how HE climbed.

But he looked at HER when he said "when she's ready." Not at the mentor. At Drenna. A flicker of recognition — the look of someone who sees the calculation behind the performance because they INVENTED that calculation.

He doesn't say anything else to her. She carries that look for the rest of the season.

**His role in the outer culture response:** One of the few who takes the threat seriously immediately. Forces the Amarith Practitioner to engage. Uses honest language — "organized armed groups with territorial objectives" — while everyone else minimizes.

**Seasons 2-3:** Becomes Drenna's mentor when she "succeeds" and discovers success is a prison. "You got everything. Now what?" He knows that question. The answer nearly destroyed him.

### Amarith Practitioner — Vaelen's Malfoy

**Who he is:** Born into the Amarith. Parents were Amarith practitioners. Grandparents were Amarith practitioners. Trained at the elite Solcarin. Never had to prove himself. His last name proved it for him.

Infuriatingly, also TALENTED. Genuinely gifted. Network readings that are precise, elegant, powerful. Does at trunk level what Vaelen does at branch level, with the ease of inherited certainty.

**His antagonism is INDIFFERENCE.** Not hostility. Not resentment. He barely registers Vaelen. To him, Vaelen is a competent branch-level practitioner — impressive for his tier. The compliment and the condescension are the same sentence.

This destroys Vaelen because Vaelen's entire self is built on being seen, valued, recognized. The Practitioner's indifference is a mirror showing Vaelen how small his world is.

**The Amarith calls their work "engagements."** The word carries prestige and self-regard. Jorath hears this and deploys it mercilessly: "I'm on engagement with this buckle." "Critical engagement with the dried fruit vendor." Running joke.

**How he enters (Episode 3, Velanthra):** He's on an Amarith engagement in Velanthra — the city's network hub is strategically important. Vaelen is told to deliver his preliminary readings to the Amarith's person in the city. He goes. Watches the Practitioner work for thirty seconds — trunk-level reading at a depth Vaelen has only theorized about. The Practitioner takes Vaelen's data, scans it, gives a professionally neutral nod. One question that reveals mild curiosity. Then back to work. Vaelen walks out carrying that nod for days.

**Why they stay connected:** The rescue mission changes everything. Vaelen returns from the dead zone with data nobody else has. The Practitioner needs it. They're assigned to the same operation. The Practitioner brings institutional resources and trunk-level capabilities. Vaelen brings field data and unreplicable readings. They need each other. Neither likes it.

The Practitioner is miserable in the field. Brilliant in controlled settings — useless in degraded conditions. A lab scientist in a war zone. Resents every minute.

## VAELEN'S FLAW — The Jungian Core

Vaelen figured it out at sixteen. Not consciously — like a body learning a reflex. When he gave people what they wanted, they gave him warmth, attention, safety. He got very good at reading what people wanted. Not manipulatively — instinctively. The way a plant turns toward light.

By thirty, a star. And every step: read the room, find the signal, give them what they need, receive the warmth.

**The problem isn't that this is fake.** The warmth is REAL. He genuinely cares. The performance isn't a lie — it's a FILTER. Everything authentic passes through "how will this land?" before it reaches the world. The caring is real, but shaped for impact. The generosity is real, but calibrated for reception.

**The filter ate the source.**

Vaelen can't make a decision from his gut. He doesn't HAVE a gut anymore. Just a sophisticated antenna that picks up what the people around him think the right decision is. When Commander Essren says "trust your readings," he hears the instruction but doesn't know HOW. What does it feel like to trust yourself? He's never done it. He's trusted what other people valued about him and performed more of that.

**His gift IS his flaw.** Pattern-reading (reading external signals) is the literal, magical expression of his psychological wound. He can read EVERYTHING except himself. A mirror that needs something to reflect.

**The catastrophe pattern:**
- **Ep 1:** Works perfectly. Familiar room, clear signals. Star.
- **Ep 3 — First cost:** In the dead zone, no network signal. His antenna locks onto "what would the impressive call look like?" He makes the call that SOUNDS like what a talented leader would do. The call is wrong. Someone gets hurt. He was performing competence instead of BEING competent.
- **Ep 5 — Caeleth:** His antenna produces the "impressive" call: deploy Caeleth's sensitivity. Use the asset. Something in his chest whispers DON'T. But the whisper has no practice being heard. The antenna is louder, clearer, more reliable. He follows competence. Caeleth dies.
- **Ep 8 — The silence:** Working alongside the Amarith, his antenna locked onto the most powerful signal he's ever encountered. His gift shows him the Amarith's approach is wrong. But he genuinely doesn't know if what he's seeing is REAL or his own noise. He's never trusted his own perception without validation. He can't locate the self that would speak.

**Vaelen IS Sorathel in miniature.** Sorathel never developed independent identity because the Amarwen made it unnecessary. Vaelen never developed a Self because the Persona made it unnecessary. When the Amarwen withdraws, Sorathel collapses. When situations demand authentic selfhood, Vaelen collapses.

## THE EARLY SIGNS vs. THE DREAD — Distinct Categories

**Early signs (Episodes 1-4):** Behavioral. Social. Infrastructure. The network weakening causes:
- People behaving differently (father acting strangely, professional losing precision)
- Creatures becoming wilder, more instinctive (loss of "civilizatory" domestication)
- Communities fracturing under stress
- Infrastructure failing (routes unreliable, bonds weakening)

These are attributed to the external threat, to stress, to the frontier crisis. They're concerning but comprehensible.

**The Dread (Episode 5):** A completely DIFFERENT category of experience. Not behavioral — REALITY BREAKING. Space folds. Time layers. Interior becomes exterior. The Ohn hum. Nothing in the early signs prepares the characters (or the reader) for this. The Dread is unprecedented, incomprehensible, and terrifying in a way that the outer culture raids and the behavioral signs simply are not.

The gap between the early signs and the Dread is the gap between "concerning situation" and "the rules of reality have changed." That gap is the cliffhanger. The reader who has experienced Episode 5 knows that the dead zones — currently small, currently contained — contain THAT. And they're growing.

## THE DREAD — What It Is

### What it is NOT
- Not "people going still" or "sitting down"
- Not "people acting out of character"
- Not behavioral changes (those are the EARLY SIGNS, Episodes 1-4)

### What it IS
Full reality distortion. The moment the team enters a full dead zone, the rules of reality change.

**SPACE FOLDS.** You walk toward a building and it's farther than it was. Turn around and the path you came from is gone — not hidden, GONE. The landscape rearranges when you're not looking. There's a LOGIC you can almost grasp and then CAN'T. Trying to understand it makes your teeth ache.

**TIME LAYERS.** You see the same place at different times simultaneously. A woman setting a table — alive, warm, calling her children. And the table is decayed. The food is ash. The children are gone. She's still calling. Both states coexist. Not flickering — LAYERED. Double exposure. You hear conversations from weeks ago. Laughter from a dinner that already happened. You can't tell NOW from THEN. Time isn't a line here. It's a place.

**INTERIOR BECOMES EXTERIOR.** You think about a meal with someone you loved. The meal is THERE. On a table that wasn't there before. Food correct, dishes correct, smell correct. But the person has the expression from your FEAR of what they think of you, not their actual face. The landscape reads your mind — not telepathically, ARCHITECTURALLY. Memories become geography. Fears become weather. Multiple people = interiors COLLIDE. Vaelen's memory of a conversation overlaps with Sorvyn's memory of the same conversation, and both versions are PHYSICALLY PRESENT. The team experiences each other's interior landscapes.

**THE OHN HUM.** Beneath everything — the substrate. A vibration felt in bones, in teeth. The frequency of reality without interpretation. Without meaning. It makes the chaos feel COHERENT. The hum says: stop resisting. The linear time was a simplification. The private interior was a convenience. The stable geography was a story the Amarwen told. This is what's real. And if you listen long enough, the chaos starts to make sense. The alien logic clicks. And that's when you're lost — because understanding the Dread's logic means your mind has switched to a mode that can't switch back.

### The Dread is NOT the outer cultures' weapon
But the institutions classify it as one. This misattribution is critical — it keeps everyone focused on the fightable enemy and prevents investigation of what the Dread actually is.

### Each character's Dread experience
- **Vaelen:** Mirror with nothing to reflect. The Persona with no audience. Emptiness where a self should be.
- **Caeleth:** Coherence. He can read the logic. He guides the team. And the reading pulls him deeper until his body can't contain the signal.
- **Sorvyn:** His faith in Vaelen exposed as dependency. Not faith — need.
- **Jorath:** Almost nothing. Self-constructed identity. The Dread has nothing to dissolve. Terrified watching others dissolve. "Kid. KID." — the voice cracking.
- **Drenna:** NOT PRESENT. She's in a committee room. Reads Caeleth's name on a list.

## CAELETH'S DEATH — The First Violence in Sorathel

No violence has been used in the story until this point. Not the outer cultures' weapons — not yet aimed at anyone the reader loves. The first violence in Sorathel is reality rewriting a boy's body.

### The sequence

Caeleth's sensitivity translates the Dread's logic. He guides the team through the folded space. He's calm — calmer than he should be. The gift that terrified him around large creatures is now the only thing keeping the team alive.

He stops walking. Tilts his head — the student's version of the mentor's gesture. Something is pulling his perception DOWNWARD. Into the substrate. Into the Ohn.

"There's something underneath."

He opens his gift wider. The Ohn responds. **And his body begins to answer.**

**The veins in his wrists darken** — visibly, through the skin. The blood vessels becoming PRONOUNCED. The network pattern — the branching, fractal structure of the Amarvel root system — surfaces under his skin. Tracing up his forearms. Branching. Spreading. His body is mapping the thing he's perceiving. Interior becoming exterior.

**The lines begin to GLOW.** Not the warm bioluminescence of the Amarvel. Something colder. Bluer. The Ohn's light. His body is becoming a conductor. A tuning fork. The Ohn's frequency resonating through his physical form.

**His body begins to VIBRATE** — not shaking, VIBRATING. At a frequency the others feel in their teeth. The air distorts. His skin, traced with luminous branching lines, begins to FRACTURE. Not cuts. Not wounds. Fracture lines — like cracks in porcelain. Light pouring through the cracks — not blood, LIGHT.

**Caeleth's hand comes up.** That gesture — the tender one, the goodbye. His fingers find Vaelen's face. And where the cracked, luminous skin touches Vaelen's cheek — Vaelen SEES. One second. The full Ohn signal. Everything. The substrate laid bare. Every root, every bond, every identity — a single unified structure so enormous the human mind recoils. Beautiful the way an ocean is beautiful from inside a drowning. True the way death is true.

**In that second, Vaelen understands why Caeleth can't come back.** The boy hasn't been destroyed. He's been OPENED. What poured through is too vast to close.

The contact breaks. The fractures stop spreading. The glow dims — doesn't disappear, dims. The branching lines remain, faintly luminous. His eyes are open. He breathes. He stands.

The boy is gone. What remains is a body WRITTEN ON by the Ohn. A human form that became a page for something that needed to be expressed and didn't care what the expression cost. The fracture lines will not heal. They are not wounds. They are a map of something that used to be a person.

**Jorath grabs him.** "Kid. KID." The word cracking in a voice that has never cracked. Caeleth's face turns toward Jorath. Something in those eyes that might be recognition. Might be goodbye. A look of such complete peace that Jorath lets go and steps back, because peace that complete on a face that young is the most terrifying thing he has ever seen.

**The mark on Vaelen's cheek.** A faint branching line where Caeleth's fingers touched. It fades over minutes. But Vaelen felt it — the truth, the full signal — and will carry that second for the rest of the series.

**Caeleth after:** Not dead. Not alive. A wound that walks. His body remains — breathing, luminous fracture lines, eyes open seeing something else. The team carries him out. He walks if they lead him. He stands where they put him. He's not dead enough to mourn. Not alive enough to save.

## ACT 1: WORLD DISCOVERY (Episodes 1-3)

The reader enters Sorathel. Falls in love with the world, the magic, the team. The wrongness creeps in — not as invasion, but as absence. The outer cultures are a distant rumor that gets louder. By the end of Act 1, the reader has seen beauty, adventure, weapons that shouldn't exist, and the first taste of reality folding. The old stories don't apply.

**v2 FOUNDATION IN ACT 1:** The beauty IS the fragility. Every gorgeous detail about the Amarwen network — the bonds, the Mirathyn, the creature connections, the warm humming infrastructure — is also the dependency. The reader should fall in love with a world that is, structurally, a system that made its people unable to survive without it. Show the beauty. Let the reader love it. The tragedy is that the beauty was real AND it was a cage.

### EPISODE 1 — "The Deployment"
*The reader's entry point. World, team, magic, and the first signs of wrongness. Outer culture rumors in the background.*

**WHAT THIS EPISODE DOES:**

The reader meets Sorathel through a routine Solvarei deployment to a frontier community. The team is introduced: Vaelen (the star), Drenna (the strategist), Caeleth (the sensitive junior), Sorvyn (the faith), Jorath (the anchor). The Amarwen network is alive, gorgeous, humming — and something beneath it is wrong. Subtle wrongness: a child's smile slightly muted, flowers losing their scent, readings that fluctuate when they shouldn't. The instruments say fine. The children know better.

**OUTER CULTURE THREAD:** Route refusals in the background. Merchants who won't travel certain paths. Handler conversations overheard — rumors of strangers on the frontier. Nothing confirmed. The language is vague: "frontier trouble." Nobody takes it seriously yet.

**EARLY SIGNS:** Behavioral strangeness that can be explained away. Torven's grandmother myth. The gap in the western fields. The second rhythm Vaelen discovers — something in the silence between pulses. Every fix feeds the problem.

**CHARACTER SEEDS:**
- Vaelen works perfectly — familiar room, clear signals. His gift reads beautifully. Star performance. The catastrophe pattern begins with success.
- Drenna shows both: compassion AND ambition, simultaneously. Father visit (Mirathyn abuse). "Nobody is coming." Her dual nature established from page one.
- Caeleth: wonder, sensitivity, "bees behind a wall." Build this boy to be loved.

- Jorath: physical competence, Mirathyn joke, resourcefulness — NOT money-comedy-bit.

**DRENNA DEPARTURE:** Drenna departs to the gray fox world at the end of Episode 1. The separation begins.

The Amarith Practitioner enters and devastates Vaelen with indifference.

**EARLY SIGNS → FIRST TASTE:** Terrain distortion in the dead zone — not full Dread, but reality folding at the edges. Enough to show the reader something categorically different from the behavioral signs of Eps 1-2. The gap between "concerning" and "impossible" opens here.

**KEY BEATS:**

- **Velanthra arrival:** Urban hub. The Amarith Practitioner is on engagement — the city's network hub is strategically important. One question revealing mild curiosity. Back to work. Vaelen walks out carrying that nod for days. Indifference that devastates.

## THE REVELATION ENGINE ACROSS THE SEASON

**Episode 1:** The wrongness beneath normalcy. Subtle signs. Team introduced. Outer culture rumors (route refusals, merchant silence). Drenna's dual nature established. Zethael at the wayhouse. Vaelen's star performance. Ohn seed. Every fix feeds the problem.
**Episode 2:** Life and society. Solvarei compound as workplace. Commercial transport. Caeleth beloved-building through witnesses. Commander at gray fox dinner — the LOOK. Amarith seeded. Outer culture thread: merchant reports, creature reversion, handler fears. Drenna's visible fork at Velanthra. "Engagement" joke launched.
**Episode 3:** Adventure — the "troll moment." Dead zone crossing. Outer cultures encountered — WEAPONS seen. Amarith Practitioner enters (indifference). Vaelen's first cost (wrong call). Zethael's two innovations revealed. Three worlds diverging.
**Episode 4:** Cascade. Raids escalate. Behavioral breakdown spreads. Both attributed to the same crisis — the reader suspects they're not. Drenna creates her political opening. Zethael leaves the Solvarei.
**Episode 5:** THE DREAD. Full reality distortion. Caeleth consumed. Raid coincides with Dread zone — misattribution locked in. Jorath as anchor. "Kid. KID." Drenna reads both names in a committee room. Zethael takes innovations to commercial institution — hope.
**Episode 6:** Mobilization. Outer culture leader glimpsed. Protection force assembled. Commander forces Amarith engagement. Drenna crosses to the other side of the table. Zethael breaks from commercial institution, finds partner, goes solo. Three responses to institutional failure.
**Episode 7:** The Elite. Vaelen alongside the Amarith (awe and doubt). Protection force's first defense. Drenna on the political side. Zethael building on the frontier — adventure, flaws visible, but it works. Commander vs. Practitioner dynamic.
**Episode 8:** Vaelen's silence. Outer culture "victory." Sorvyn's resentment breaks open. Drenna's Tabata moment. Zethael's frontier network growing.
**Episode 9:** Three friends, three paths, three answers. Hollow victory. Outer cultures "secured" — leader retreated, not defeated. Dread zone classified. Vaelen through the ceiling door. Drenna in the rooms. Zethael on the frontier. The protection force exists — and its creator controls it. None fully right. None fully wrong. The crisis is "over." The reader knows it's not.

## STRUCTURAL NOTES

- **Peak 1 (Ep 5):** Midpoint. Spectacle. Horror. Loss. The Dread. The question made literal. The first violence in Sorathel.
- **Peak 2 (Ep 8):** 8/9 mark. Character. Moral failure. Vaelen's silence. Drenna's choice. Outer culture "victory." The question answered badly.
- **Bridge (Eps 6-7):** Mobilization + The Elite. Protection force assembled. Amarith deployed. Each episode tightens the vise.
- **Three-episode Act 1:** World entry. Life and society. Fall in love — then the adventure, weapons, and first taste of the impossible.
- **Three-episode Act 2:** Cascade → Dread → Mobilization. The world breaks, someone is lost, the response begins — aimed at the wrong threat.
- **Three-episode Act 3:** The Elite → The Silence → The Door. The fightable war is "won." The real threat is classified. Vaelen fails to speak. Everyone departs.
