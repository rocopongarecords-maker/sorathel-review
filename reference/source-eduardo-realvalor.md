## THE TIMELINE

### Pre-founding (2014-2016)
- Eduardo started investing in 2014, influenced by his sister and a mentor
- Worked in management consulting in São Paulo (2015-2016) — hated it
- Created his own Excel spreadsheet to track investments — worked great but maintaining it was unsustainable
- **The injustice moment:** Created a tool that secured a million-real project for his company. Reward: "two pats on the back." No raise, no bonus, no promotion. "This dynamic destroyed me. If I did something extraordinary, I'd get 2 pats on the back, but if I did something disastrous, I'd be fired. Entrepreneurship seemed more fair."
- Read "4 Hour Work Week" by Tim Ferriss — taught him the cost of a life of regret would be greater than the financial cost of trying

### The Idea & Launch (Late 2016 - Early 2017)
- Friend Gabriel Farias called asking about investment tracking tools
- Gabriel's suggestion: "If there isn't one, why don't we make one?" 
- Eduardo's obvious responses would have been: we can't code, Excel works fine, if brokerages haven't done it why would we succeed
- **His actual answer: "BORA" (LET'S GO)**
- Neither knew how to code. Neither had finance or tech background.
- Agreement: willing to invest 100% of personal savings (~R$100k each)
- Eduardo stayed at his job initially; Gabriel had just quit after burnout at consulting firm
- Read Lean Startup, took Steve Blank's course on Udacity
- Interviewed potential users with a wireframe made in Excel
- Validated the pain point: people either didn't track investments or had a Herculean task maintaining spreadsheets

### Building the MVP (Jan-Apr 2017)
- Chose Android (Java) over iOS because more online resources to learn from
- Bought a Java course, watched 2 hours, ADHD wouldn't let him continue: "I need to get my hands dirty"
- Strategy: start building, use the course as reference when stuck
- Everything a developer would do in 2 minutes took them 10 hours
- "I coded like the meme of the dog in the laboratory"
- Worked from PUC university library (their only "office")
- Arrived at 7am to get parking, library opened at 8:30 — napped in the gardens between
- Named it "Real Valor" (Gabriel's idea) — chose name somewhat randomly then invented justifications after
- Logo: tried hiring a designer, got garbage, Eduardo drew it himself after hours studying Illustrator
- Business model: planned Spotify-style freemium, but had NO IDEA what premium would include

### First Users (Mid 2017)
- Launched alpha to friends, then friends of friends
- Marketed exclusively through Facebook investment groups — ZERO marketing budget
- Strategy: announce updates in groups, respond to EVERY comment, EVERY email, EVERY review
- Had users' WhatsApp numbers, deeply personal relationships with users
- 100 daily active users — seemed small but was growing weekly with zero spend
- Community-led growth before they knew the term

### Accelerator & São Paulo (Oct 2017 - 2018)
- Applied to ACE accelerator — rejected the first time
- Applied again — accepted into ACE Start program
- Moved to São Paulo. Eduardo crashed on friend's sofa for ~2 months (one-bedroom apartment — Eduardo occupied the living room more than his friend)
- Ate at "Dozão" — R$12 lunch plates near Paulista
- Flew to Rio every weekend (advance tickets were cheaper than bus)
- Eventually found a shared apartment for R$1,200/month
- **The accelerator mentor ("Suli")** was a mix of mentor and torturer. Would give harsh responses: "A good founder doesn't ask me that kind of question. Figure it out yourself." Eduardo was FURIOUS but later recognized it built resilience.
- Passed from ACE Start to ACE Growth — received R$150k investment

### The Monetization Struggle (2018)
- Premium launch: few features behind paywall, limited free users to 10 assets
- Almost nobody subscribed
- WORSE: store ratings plummeted. Users who loved the free product now hated the restrictions
- "No matter how cheap a product is... if the user didn't see value, they won't pay"
- Had to rethink monetization entirely — removed the premium paywall
- Eduardo admits: fear of selling was a bigger barrier than actual product issues. "Unconsciously, I was fooling myself saying it wasn't time to launch premium. In reality, it was fear of selling."

### The XP Meeting Disaster (2018)
- Partnership with Monte Bravo (major XP brokerage office) was developing
- Got a meeting with Gabriel Leal, XP's #2 executive
- Multiple people warned: "Don't show all your cards. Those people are foxes."
- Eduardo's ego: dismissed the warning
- **First mistake:** Went without their sponsor (Edgar from Monte Bravo) 
- Day of meeting: told Leal couldn't attend, replaced by tech person
- **Second mistake:** Should have said "our meeting is with Leal" and walked out. Didn't have the experience to know this.
- The tech person destroyed every idea, questioned everything, killed the project
- Eduardo realized: this person had personal interest in blocking them. "Big company, right? Personal interests sometimes speak louder than the company's."
- **Third mistake:** Had revealed their entire strategy to a potential competitor
- Left devastated. The meeting remained a painful memory for years.

### Hiring & Technical Debt (2018)
- App had become a mess of workarounds — "gambiarra em cima de gambiarra"
- Adding new features created mysterious bugs in unrelated areas ("seemed like black magic")
- Hired Ferolla (freelancer who'd done payment processing) as first employee
- Ferolla accepted ~R$1,200/month + equity, vs R$6,000+ he could earn elsewhere — "He bought the idea"
- Plan: rebuild the app in React Native (solving both technical debt AND getting iOS version)
- Needed a Mac for iOS — borrowed one from his girlfriend's sister's boyfriend
- Also started a blog to drive user acquisition beyond Facebook groups

### Monte Bravo Partnership (Late 2018 - Early 2019)
- Finally launched partnership with Monte Bravo brokerage
- Model: Real Valor would help advisors see clients' full portfolio, enabling "share of wallet" capture
- Revenue came from conversion — sending users to open accounts at Monte Bravo
- Revenue was tiny. Process wasn't optimized. But it was the first real business model.

### The Gabriel Breakdown & Return to Rio (Late 2018)
- Acceleration ending — had to start paying for office space
- Gabriel called Eduardo into a meeting room. Started crying.
- "I need to go back to Rio. I'm in the shit. I can't stay in São Paulo anymore."
- Far from girlfriend, family. Company wasn't making money. Struggling emotionally.
- **Key detail:** Gabriel felt he was "weakening" as an entrepreneur. His internal battle was: "We built this from zero, solved a MOUNTAIN of problems, and now I'm being weak about something simple."
- Eduardo's instant response: "If you need to go to Rio, we go to Rio. Fuck it."
- "It doesn't make sense for us to separate. Our advantage is being together every day."
- Decision made in 1 minute. Moved back to Rio within a week.
- "It doesn't make sense for the company to prosper and sanity to go to hell."
- **From outside, they looked like rockstars.** PUC friends thought they were flying high and getting rich. Reality: no salary, spending savings, working from a library.

### Growth & Web Version (Early 2019)
- Back at PUC library, then found proper office
- 150k+ users on platform
- Brilliant email from user requesting web version: "I like checking investments at work, but if I pick up my phone my boss notices I'm not working. If you had a desktop version, my boss would never suspect."
- Eduardo: "For someone to have the courage to send an email like that, the web version must be VERY important to them." This was the final push to build web.

### Monte Bravo Acquisition Talks (2019)
- Monte Bravo started hinting at acquisition: "Let's do something together." "Why don't you come work with us?"
- Never explicitly said "we want to buy you" — just heavy insinuation
- Eduardo and Gabriel prepared with ACE's guidance
- Learned about cash out vs. earn out structures
- Valued Real Valor at R$7 million
- Eduardo would receive ~R$3 million. Got caught up in the fantasy.
- **The emotional peak:** "I built a business from zero, without knowing how to code, without knowing the market. 2 years later, invested by ACE, and one of the biggest XP offices wants to buy me? HOLY SHIT."
- Walked into negotiation and said: "You want us to join? The price is 7 million."
- The room went awkward immediately.
- [Article 12 ends mid-scene — the deal apparently fell through, leading eventually to the Empiricus acquisition in 2020]

## KEY THEMES FOR VARDO

### 1. The Injustice Trigger
Not philosophical. Not existential. A specific moment where institutional reward was absurdly disconnected from value created. "Two pats on the back" for a million-real contribution.

### 2. Learning by Doing (Total Ignorance + Willpower)
Never coded before. Never worked in finance. Never worked in tech. Every task took 10x longer than it should. But velocity of learning matters more than starting knowledge.

### 3. The Emotional Rollercoaster
Not a steady climb. Extreme highs followed by devastating lows, with amplitude INCREASING over time. The higher the peak, the deeper the valley.

### 4. The Partnership
Eduardo and Gabriel's bond is the spine. Gabriel's breakdown in SP and Eduardo's instant "then we go to Rio, fuck it" is the defining moment. They chose each other over the plan.

### 5. The Outside Perception Gap
From outside: two guys flying high, getting invested, building something amazing. From inside: no salary, spending savings, eating R$12 lunches, sleeping on friend's sofa, terrified about monetization.

### 6. Fear of Selling / Fear of Charging
Eduardo admits: the delay in monetizing was partially fear of selling. Engineering background gave zero sales instinct. The premium launch failed partly because they feared the act of asking for money.

### 7. The Frugality Ethic
Extreme cost-consciousness as survival strategy. R$12 lunches. Friend's sofa. Weekend flights to save on rent. Every real spent extended the company's runway.

### 8. Community as Strategy
Responding to every comment, every email, every review. Having users' WhatsApp. Building genuine relationships. This created organic growth that marketing budgets couldn't match.

### 9. The Mentor-Torturer
The ACE accelerator who pushed them to the limit. Harsh, seemingly cruel, but building resilience. "A good founder doesn't ask me that question."

### 10. Institutional Sabotage
The XP meeting where a tech person killed the project to protect their own position. Big company politics vs. startup innovation. "Personal interests sometimes speak louder than the company's interests."

### 11. The Failed First Exit
Monte Bravo acquisition that fell through (implied from article 12 cliffhanger). The emotional devastation of thinking you're about to be rich and having it collapse.

### 12. Financial Education as Freedom
Eduardo could only pursue entrepreneurship because he'd been extremely frugal and invested aggressively. His savings bought him runway. "Financial education allowed me to accumulate money to pursue my dream."
