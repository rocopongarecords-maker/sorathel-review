# Episode Two — Chapter Six
## The Garage
*Jorath*

*Version 3 — Land-skiff, Diagon Alley market, Amarwen scene*
*Created: 2026-03-17*

---

The bearing sleeve was wrong.

Jorath turned it in his hands, feeling the bore with his thumb. Too wide by a quarter-mark at least — maybe more. The bronze was good, cast from a northern foundry that still used sand molds instead of the clay slurry everyone had switched to because it was cheaper and worse, and the result was a clean outer surface with a bore that was oval where a circle should be. The foundryman had poured freehand instead of using a jig. Jorath could feel the asymmetry without measuring it. His thumb knew.

He set it on the workbench beside its predecessor — the first bearing sleeve he'd bought, three months ago, from a different vendor in the same district. That one had been too narrow. He'd paid four and a half marks for it. This one had cost six, because the vendor on Wheelwright Row had seen Jorath's face when he examined the grain of the bronze and known he was selling to someone who could tell quality from shine. The markup for competence. Jorath respected it, in principle. In practice, six marks for an oval bore was robbery.

The workshop occupied the ground floor of a storage building on Cartway Street, in the lower city. Jorath rented it from a retired wheelwright named Hassin who lived on the floor above and charged two marks a month for the space and another half-mark for the use of the forge, which Jorath hadn't used yet but kept paying for because the moment he stopped, Hassin would rent the forge rights to someone else and Jorath would never get them back at that rate. The economics of holding an option. You paid for what you might need because paying later always cost more.

The space was narrow — ten paces deep, eight wide — with a workbench along the back wall, tools hung on pegs in order of frequency of use, and a set of shelving Jorath had built himself from salvaged timber because Hassin's shelving had been crooked enough to slide a level off the surface. The front opened onto Cartway Street through a wide wooden door that Jorath kept propped with a stone when the weather was decent, which let in light and street noise and the smell of the tannery two buildings east, which was not decent but was included in the rent.

The land-skiff sat in the center of the space on blocks.

Six months of work and it was still mostly a frame — ironwood beams fitted with forged metal brackets at the joints, each bracket hand-shaped because nobody manufactured brackets for a craft this size and Jorath refused to use rope lashings that would stretch under load. The rear wheels were salvaged from a decommissioned municipal hauler, hardwood rims banded in iron, sound enough for another decade if he trued the spokes. The front wheels he was building from scratch — smaller, lighter, mounted on a tiller arm that connected to the frame through metal rods so the whole front assembly could pivot. A collapsible sail mast rose from a socket behind the forward seat, folded down now, its canvas bundled and tied. For windless days and low speeds: a pedal assembly driving a chain-and-gear mechanism to the rear axle. For open road with weather at his back: the sail.

In a world where everything that moved on land moved because a creature carried it, the thing was borderline insane. People said so. Hassin said so every time he came down to collect rent and stood in the doorway shaking his head at the ironwood frame with the expression of a man watching someone build a boat in his living room. "Why not just hire a runner?" Hassin would ask, and Jorath would say something about commercial fares and long-term economics and the math of twelve years of deployment travel, and Hassin would nod and not believe a word of it, and Jorath would go back to work.

The truth — the one Jorath wouldn't say, the one his hands said every time they found the grain of the ironwood and followed it with a thumb — was simpler than economics. The truth was that the bearing sleeve in his hand was a problem with edges. A thing that could be measured, tested, and either solved or not, without ambiguity, without anyone's feelings about it mattering at all. The truth was that a chain-and-gear mechanism either transferred force cleanly or it didn't, and when it did, the satisfaction was so specific and so complete that nothing else existed for a moment. Just the mechanism. Just the fit.

He'd never call it joy. He'd call it not being bored.

The door was propped open. The morning was overcast and warm, the kind of weather that made the tannery smell travel. Jorath could hear Cartway Street doing what it always did — hauler wheels on cobblestone, vendors arguing about delivery schedules, a dog with opinions about something in an alley. The lower city was loud — constantly circulating, stopping only when something broke.

He had the bearing sleeve clamped in a vise and was working the bore with a reaming tool — slow, careful, coaxing the oval toward round — when the boy appeared.

---

Caeleth stood in the doorway with the particular posture of someone who wanted to be invited in but wouldn't ask. His shoulders were drawn up slightly, his weight on his back foot, his hands holding a paper sack of something from the market against his chest — either fragile or embarrassing, from the grip. His field jacket was open over a civilian shirt that was too clean and too new, and his boots — the same boots from the deployment — still had frontier soil in the seams. He hadn't cleaned them. Whether that was sentiment or laziness, Jorath couldn't tell.

"Are you lost?"

"No." Caeleth looked past him at the land-skiff on its blocks. His eyes went wide and stayed that way. "What is that?"

"A land-skiff."

"Is it — are you building this?"

"No, it grew here. The lower city has fertile soil." Jorath turned back to the bearing sleeve and checked the bore with his thumb. Closer. Another few passes. "What do you want?"

"Sorvyn said you were going to the market today. For the deployment." He produced a piece of paper from his jacket pocket. Not the field journal — a separate sheet, folded twice, covered in handwriting that was too large and too hopeful for the things it was listing. Jorath glanced at it. Dried rations, replacement water skins, a whetstone, field tape, a new strap for his equipment satchel.

Caeleth looked at his list with quiet betrayal.

"The Solvarei provides team equipment — probes, field kits, anything heavy," Jorath said. "The stores have standard gear — you just have to ask Theryn before midday or he closes the requisition list. Everything else is yours to source. You don't need the whetstone — I carry three. You don't need the water skins — Theryn has them in inventory. The rations, yes. The strap, yes. Your satchel strap is going to snap in the first week — I've been watching it fray since Torven. Should have replaced it there but you won't find frontier leather in a Darachet market, so we'll settle for commercial grade. Which will cost more and last less. Don't get me started."

"Oh." Caeleth folded the list and put it back in his pocket. Then looked at the land-skiff again. "But how does it — it moves? Without a creature?"

"On wheels, yes. Gearing and pedals for low speed. Sail for open road." Jorath nodded at the folded mast. "Wind's free. My legs are free. The only cost is the frame, and I built the frame."

"But why not just —"

"Hire a runner? Pay commercial rates every time I need to move equipment between sites? I've calculated the fares I've paid over twelve years of deployment travel. The number would make you physically unwell." He paused. "Also, have you ever built something that worked exactly the way you designed it?"

"No."

"It hasn't happened to me yet either. But I'm getting closer."

Caeleth was smiling. The whole face — mouth wide, eyes crinkled at the corners, head tilted slightly forward as if the smile had weight and was pulling him toward whatever had caused it.

"You can hold this," Jorath said, lifting the bearing sleeve from the vise. "Don't rotate it. The reaming marks need to set."

Caeleth held it with both hands, carefully, fingers cupped underneath, thumbs steadying the top — too much care for a piece of bronze. Jorath watched his fingers settle around the cylinder, finding the balance point by instinct — which was interesting, because the boy had no mechanical instinct at all. He just held things gently. Everything. Always.

Jorath fitted a copper shim inside the bore, tapping it into place with a small hammer. The sound was sharp and regular in the quiet workshop. Caeleth held the sleeve steady without being told, his eyes moving between Jorath's hands and the land-skiff behind them with the particular attention of someone who was seeing a thing he didn't understand and not pretending otherwise.

"How long before it's finished?"

"Two months on the frame. Six months of planning before that. I'll need another year at least, and that's optimistic, because the suspension is —" He stopped. He'd been about to explain the spring tempering problem, which would lead to the metallurgy problem, which would lead to the alloy sourcing problem, and the boy didn't need to hear all that. "A while longer."

"It's going to be beautiful," Caeleth said.

Jorath looked at him. The boy wasn't looking at the unfinished frame. He was looking at the thing it would become. The finished craft. The open road with wind in the canvas. Jorath recognized the expression because he'd felt it on his own face, alone in the workshop, and never expected to see it on someone else's.

"It's going to move," Jorath said. "Beautiful is optional."

He turned back to the shim. It was a good fit. He tapped twice more, tested the play — minimal, even. The kind of fit that would hold for years.

---

The Darachet market occupied three connected squares in the lower city, and walking into it was walking into a different country.

The first square — the creature market — hit them before they'd passed the entrance arch. Sound first: a layered cacophony of calls, warbles, low rumbles that traveled through the cobblestones into the soles of Jorath's boots. Then the smell — warm animal musk, bonding oil (sweet, resinous, applied to creature hides to ease new partnerships), rootwood shavings scattered on the ground to absorb waste, and underneath it all the faintly mineral tang of creatures who had been near healthy root junctions. The square was wide, open to the sky, and full of life.

Creature vendors lined the perimeter in stone-walled stalls with ironwood gates, each stall different — some holding a single large creature bedded on rootgrass, others with a half-dozen small ones clustered on warm perch-stones. A handler near the entrance was demonstrating a diagnostic creature to a pair of farmers: the thing was small enough to sit in a cupped hand, iridescent, its scales shifting between copper and deep green as the handler moved it near a node marker set into the ground. Where the marker pulsed healthy, the creature brightened — scales flaring to gold-green. The handler tilted it toward a cracked section of cobblestone where the marker had gone dark and the scales dulled to grey-brown. The farmers leaned in, murmuring.

Farther in, the companion creatures. These were the ones looking for bonds — young animals, unbonded, moving in their stalls with the restless energy of things that hadn't found their person yet. A broad-headed furred creature pressed itself against the gate of its enclosure, amber eyes tracking every person who walked past. A winged thing the size of a cat perched on a rootwood stand and called — a sound between a whistle and a hum, persistent, aimed at no one and everyone. In the far corner, a bonding was happening: a young woman sat cross-legged on the cobblestones, very still, while a creature the size of a large dog approached her hand. It touched its nose to her palm. Her breathing changed — visible from across the square, the sudden depth of it, shoulders dropping, posture opening. The handler standing behind her folded his arms and watched with the practiced patience of someone who'd seen this a thousand times and still found it worth seeing.

Caeleth had stopped walking.

Jorath looked back. The boy was watching the bonding with an expression that wasn't wonder — it was closer to recognition. His hands hung at his sides, palms open, fingers slightly spread, and his breathing had gone deep and slow, matching — Jorath realized — the rhythm of the woman's.

"Caeleth. Market. Shopping. Today."

The boy blinked. Followed.

Jorath walked the creature square with the economy of a man who had no business there and resented the distraction, though he noticed — because he noticed everything that had a price — that the diagnostic creatures were going for forty marks, which was fifteen more than last season, and the companion creatures weren't moving. Three of the stalls that had been full on his last visit had their gates open and their bedding fresh but empty. Fewer creatures to sell. That was new.

The second square — provisions — opened through a covered walkway where vendors who couldn't afford stall rent sold from blankets and overturned crates. The air changed: animal musk replaced by spice, dried fruit, smoked grain, and something sweet-mineral underneath that Jorath recognized as Mirathyn. A vendor at the walkway entrance had arranged glass vessels on a cloth-draped table — a dozen bottles of varying size, each holding liquid that ranged from pale gold to deep amber. He was explaining the differences to a woman in merchant's clothes with the patient specificity of someone who took his product seriously.

"Ashara root-country," he said, lifting a bottle of dark amber. The glass caught the light and the liquid seemed to shift — not glowing, but aware of the attention. "Mineral. Smoky. You'll taste the volcanic soil. Good for evening — it sits heavy." He set it down and lifted a paler bottle, nearly colorless gold. "Northern plain. Lighter. Sweeter. Morning Mirathyn — farmers drink it with breakfast and swear it helps the fields." He smiled at his own salesmanship. "Whether the fields agree, I can't say."

Caeleth had drifted toward the table. Jorath caught his arm.

"Window shopping. We have a list."

He bought the field rations in three minutes from the dried-goods vendor near the east wall — a leathery man with white eyebrows and a son who'd been a practitioner once, which meant he didn't mark up for Solvarei clients. Jorath negotiated the price down by fifteen percent because one of the sealed packets had a stress mark on the wax and he pointed it out and the vendor, who was honest and therefore respected the observation, adjusted without argument. A clean transaction. Jorath's preferred kind.

Past the rations: an ashroot vendor with a clay brazier, roasting dried roots on a flat stone. The smell drifted — smoky, deep, slightly bitter, familiar from every morning at Sorvyn's apartment. "Three-copper a portion," the vendor called. "Ashara stock, ground fresh." Jorath filed the price. Cheaper than what Sorvyn paid at the spice merchant, but Sorvyn would say the grind was wrong, and Sorvyn would be right.

He turned to check on Caeleth and found the boy three stalls away, buying dried fruit.

No — not buying. Being robbed. The vendor — an older woman with cropped gray hair and a face that had been in the sun for every one of its years, her hands dark and steady as she weighed the fruit on a scale that Jorath could see, even from this distance, was at least two percent heavy on the vendor's side — was measuring out fruit into a cloth bag while Caeleth counted out coins.

Jorath walked over.

"How much?" he said to Caeleth.

"Seven marks for a half-measure."

"That's three marks over market rate."

Caeleth looked at the fruit. Looked at the vendor. Looked at the fruit again. "It's good fruit."

"It's fruit. It costs four marks at the stall on the west side. Three and a half if you buy the second-pick grade, which tastes the same and just doesn't look as pretty in the bag."

The vendor was watching Jorath with the flat expression of someone who had been selling fruit in this market for decades and didn't need a man in a practitioner's jacket telling her what fruit cost. She didn't argue. She didn't need to. She had the boy's money already.

But she didn't. Caeleth hadn't paid yet — he was still counting. He looked up at Jorath, then at the vendor, then at the fruit, and something moved in his face that was not calculation and not embarrassment and not the sensible response to being told he was overpaying. Something softer. His eyes stayed on the woman's hands — the dark knuckles, the cracked skin at the joints, the way she held the bag open with one hand and weighed with the other with the practiced steadiness of someone who had been doing this since before Caeleth was born.

He paid seven marks.

Jorath said nothing. He watched the coins change hands, watched Caeleth take the bag, watched the vendor fold the cloth around the fruit with the care of someone who took the work seriously regardless of the price, and he felt in his chest the specific irritation of witnessing a financial crime that the victim had chosen to commit with full knowledge and apparent contentment.

"She reminded me of someone," Caeleth said, as they walked.

"She reminded you of someone who charges three marks over market rate?"

"My mother's neighbor. Maret. She has hands like that — the knuckles, the way she holds things."

Jorath looked at the bag of fruit. Looked at the boy. Made a decision that was not about the fruit.

"Eat one," he said. "If it's worth seven marks, I'll stop talking about it."

Caeleth ate one. His face did something complicated — his eyes closed, his jaw loosened, and then his mouth pulled to one side and his brow tightened for half a breath before it all smoothed out again.

"It's good," Caeleth said.

"Is it seven-mark good?"

"I don't know what seven-mark good tastes like."

"It tastes like four-mark good with three marks of guilt attached."

The third square was hardware, and walking into it was walking into a forge's dream. Metalwork on stone counters — springs, brackets, tiller fittings, wheel rims stacked against walls. Leather goods on wooden racks. But between the practical stalls, stranger things. A woodworker's table covered in rootwood carvings — bowls and boxes and ornamental pieces whose grain didn't follow the logic of any tree Jorath had seen. The wood swirled, spiraled, doubled back on itself in organic patterns that traced the flow of the root network beneath the city. Jorath picked up a small box. The grain under his thumb pulsed with a warmth that had nothing to do with the weather.

Farther in: a table of practitioner instruments laid out on dark felt, some of them humming faintly. Not broken — resonating. The root network ran deep under Darachet, and the instruments could feel it through the stone. A young apprentice sat behind the table with a cloth, polishing a calibration probe, its tip bright. A rack of quarry stone tiles — pale, honey-colored, warm to the touch even in the morning air. A fabric vendor whose bolts of dyed cloth shifted color when you moved — not dramatically, not magically, but subtly — a trick of proximity to the Amarvel that the weaver had figured out how to fix in the fiber.

"They teach you about node stability indices at the Solcarin," Jorath said. Caeleth had stopped to watch the apprentice polish the probe. "Out here, you look at the Mirathyn color and you know. If the vendor's Mirathyn is pale, the local nodes are healthy. If it's dark, something's pulling. Academy-standard probes are calibrated for textbook readings. Every practitioner worth their salt modifies theirs in the first month."

Caeleth looked at him. "You're not a pattern reader."

"I don't have to read patterns to know what things cost when they break."

---

They bought the satchel strap from a leather vendor named Hathelle — a tall woman with close-cropped hair and hands that smelled of neatsfoot oil. Good work, clean stitching, proper edge treatment. Five marks, market rate, acceptable. Caeleth paid without looking at the price. Jorath watched him do it and said nothing because the price was fair and there was nothing to say, which somehow felt worse than when there was.

The transport hub occupied the northern edge of the market district, where the lower city flattened out and the streets widened to accommodate the creatures. Jorath had walked past it a hundred times — the staging yard, the loading platforms, the covered pen-bays where the commercial carriers waited between routes. The creatures were massive up close. Thirty feet at the shoulder, some of them, with the blunt wide heads and deep-set eyes of animals bred for endurance rather than speed. Their skin was dark and ridged, thick enough to carry deck structures on their backs, and they stood with the patient stillness of things that knew they were large and had learned, over generations of breeding, not to be careless about it.

Jorath noticed them as infrastructure — components, costs, systems. A carrier in its pen-bay, eating from a feed trough the size of a bathtub. Two handlers checking harness tension on a creature that was being prepped for a midday departure. The smell of them — warm, loamy, faintly sweet, an animal smell so large it became weather.

He'd walked ten paces past the hub before he realized Caeleth had stopped.

The boy was standing at the edge of the staging yard, his body wrong. Not the posture of someone looking at something — the posture of someone being looked at. His shoulders had dropped but his arms were rigid at his sides, his hands open and pressing against nothing, fingers spread. His head was tilted back slightly, his mouth open, his eyes fixed on the nearest carrier — a massive dark-plated creature standing in its pen-bay with one foot lifted, its jaw working slowly on a mouthful of feed.

Caeleth wasn't moving. The bag of overpriced fruit was clenched in his left hand but he didn't seem to know he was holding it. His chest was rising and falling too fast, short sharp breaths through his mouth, and there was a fine tremor in his hands that hadn't been there a minute ago.

Jorath walked back.

"Kid."

Caeleth's eyes didn't move from the creature.

"Caeleth."

The boy blinked. Looked at Jorath. His pupils were wide and his face had gone the color of cold ash, and his weight kept shifting — forward onto the balls of his feet, then back onto his heels, forward and back, his body unable to commit to either direction.

"They're calmer after they've eaten," Jorath said. He nodded at the creature's working jaw. "That one's on its second trough. Practically asleep."

Caeleth swallowed. His breathing was still too fast but it was slowing. He looked at the creature again, and something passed through his body — a shudder, starting in his shoulders and running through his spine and ending in his hands, which curled closed and then opened again, pressing against the air.

"It's not —" He stopped. Tried again. "I'm not afraid of it."

"I know."

"It's just — they're very —"

"Big. Yes."

"Not big." Caeleth's voice was quiet and strange, and his eyes were still on the creature. "Present."

Jorath didn't know what that meant. He looked at the carrier in its pen-bay — a big animal eating feed, worth somewhere between eight and twelve thousand marks depending on age and route certification, a depreciating asset over a twenty-year working life but a sound investment if you factored in the lack of fuel costs and the self-maintenance of a living transport system — and tried to see what Caeleth was seeing and couldn't.

"Come on," he said. "I need to talk to Orvel about springs, and if I wait until afternoon he'll pretend to be closed."

Caeleth followed him away from the transport hub, and as they walked, Jorath noticed that the boy's hands were still shaking. Not dramatically — just a fine tremor, visible when he held the fruit bag against his jacket. He tucked his hands into his pockets. Whether that was because he noticed Jorath noticing, or because the pockets were warm, or because he needed something to press his fingers against, Jorath didn't ask.

---

Orvel's stall was in the back of the hardware square, a permanent stone counter piled with salvaged metalwork — springs, brackets, tiller fittings, wheel components, and hardware of various sizes and various degrees of quality, which was Orvel's way of saying various degrees of uselessness mixed with the occasional gem you had to dig for. Orvel himself was a tall man with a beard that had once been red and was now the color of rust left out in the weather, and he sat behind his counter on a stool he'd modified — Jorath could see the welds — to accommodate his bad hip.

"Jorath." Orvel's voice was the sound of gravel being poured into a bucket. "You're early."

"I'm on schedule. You're slow."

"Still building that thing?" Orvel leaned forward, elbows on the counter. "People are going to think you've lost your mind."

"People already think that."

"I have your springs."

"You said that last month."

"Last month I had springs. Not your springs. These are your springs. Quarter-inch coil, hardened, tempered at high heat for twelve hours." Orvel reached under the counter and produced a cloth-wrapped bundle. He set it on the stone and unwrapped it with the care of someone presenting an argument.

Jorath looked at the springs. Four of them, steel, coiled tight. He picked one up and pressed the ends together, feeling the resistance. Firm. Even. Not too stiff, not too soft. He bounced it in his palm, listened to the ring. Clean. No harmonic wobble. He'd tested springs from three other smiths over eight months — springs that were too soft, too brittle, springs whose coils had gaps you could feel with your fingernail, springs that rang dull because the temper was wrong. These rang true.

"What's the tempering alloy?"

"Vaethrin base with a carbon addition. The foundry man in the northern quarter does them — you don't know him. He's new."

"How much?"

"Twelve marks per spring."

"That's forty-eight marks for a set."

"You can count. I'm impressed."

"Forty-eight marks is double what I paid for the frame."

"The frame doesn't keep you alive when you hit a rut at speed. The frame is the body. The springs are the knees."

Jorath set the spring down. He looked at the other three. He looked at Orvel. He calculated. Twelve marks per spring was too high — eight would be fair, ten would be acceptable, twelve was Orvel's opening position and they both knew it. But the quality was right. The temper was right. And these were the first springs in eight months that didn't fail the bounce test. A cheaper spring might last a season. These would last ten years.

"Ten per spring. Forty for the set."

"Eleven."

"Ten and a half. Forty-two total. And you source me the rear tiller bushings I need — hardened bronze, not brass — at cost."

Orvel looked at him with the expression of a man who had been doing this long enough to recognize when the negotiation was over and the terms were fair. He rubbed his beard. He looked at the springs. He looked at Jorath.

"The bushings will cost you six marks at my cost."

"Then the total is forty-eight, which is what you asked for in the first place. But I get better springs AND bushings. You get the sale AND a sourcing commission on the bronze." Jorath held out his hand. "Everybody loses a little. Everybody wins a little. That's a deal."

Orvel shook his hand. His grip was iron, which was fitting.

"Your boy is touching things," Orvel said.

Jorath turned. Caeleth was at the next stall, examining a set of hand tools laid out on a leather roll — chisels, files, a small precision hammer — with the delicacy of someone handling artifacts. The stall owner, a young woman with ink-stained hands and a measuring cord around her neck, was watching Caeleth with an expression that Jorath recognized: the vendor who has identified a customer who will pay whatever is asked.

"Caeleth," Jorath called. "Don't buy anything."

The boy looked up. "I'm just looking."

"Nobody's ever 'just looking.' Walk away."

Caeleth set the chisel down — carefully, in exactly the position he'd found it, the kind of precision that was courtesy rather than compulsion — and walked back. As he passed the vendor, she held out a small wooden-handled file with a ground edge that caught the light.

"Field file," she said. "Self-sharpening grain. Good for instrument work."

Caeleth looked at the file. Looked at her. His hand moved, and Jorath knew — in the way you know a rock is about to fall because the ground has shifted under it — that the boy was going to buy a field file he didn't need because the woman selling it had ink on her hands and was trying.

"He has three," Jorath said. "I carry spares."

The vendor shrugged and withdrew the file. No hard feelings. Caeleth walked back to Jorath with the expression of someone who'd been pulled back from a ledge he hadn't noticed he was standing on.

"You don't carry spare files," he said quietly.

"I carry spare everything. That's the point of being me."

---

On the walk back up through the lower city, they passed a loading platform where a team of haulers were transferring grain sacks from a ground cart to a storage building. One of the haulers — a broad man with a shaved head and a sun-darkened face — was struggling with a sack that had torn at the seam, grain spilling in a golden stream across the cobblestones. The man was trying to hold the sack closed with one hand and lift it with the other, and the geometry was wrong — it wasn't going to work, and the sack was going to rip fully, and the grain was going to become the property of the cobblestones and the pigeons and whoever had a broom.

Caeleth moved.

Not fast — he didn't run. He just walked over, knelt, and put his hands under the sack at the torn seam, pressing the grain back in and holding the fabric closed while the hauler shifted his grip to get under the weight. Together they lifted the sack to the platform edge, where a second hauler took the weight from above and swung it inside.

The first hauler nodded at Caeleth. A single nod, brief, the acknowledgment of help received. Caeleth nodded back. The hauler's hands had left grain dust on Caeleth's jacket and the boy didn't brush it off, either because he hadn't noticed or because he didn't care or because the grain dust on his jacket was, to him, the exact cost of what he'd done and the price was reasonable.

Jorath had watched the whole thing from the street. He had calculated, automatically and without choosing to: the time cost (forty seconds), the effort cost (minimal — the boy was young and the sack was manageable), the opportunity cost (none — they weren't in a hurry), and the return (a nod from a stranger). The numbers didn't balance. Forty seconds for a nod. No future value. No leverage gained. No relationship established that could be drawn on later. A pure expenditure with no return.

But the hauler had needed help. And the boy had helped. And the grain was in the building instead of on the cobblestones. And Caeleth was walking back toward Jorath now with grain dust on his jacket and no particular expression on his face — not pride, not self-consciousness, not the look of someone who expected to be praised for what he'd done. Just the face of a person who had seen a problem and solved it in the simplest way available.

Jorath said nothing. He turned and walked, and Caeleth fell into step beside him.

---

They were climbing the cobblestone streets through the lower city, the compound hill above them, when Caeleth said: "Have you ever seen her? Amarwen?"

Jorath looked at him. The boy's face was open, serious — something underneath had surfaced. Not an idle question.

"She's in the ground," Jorath said. "She's in the food. She's in every creature in that market. Every practitioner who reads the network is touching her. The question isn't whether she exists."

"Then what's the question?"

Jorath walked a few paces. The springs knocked gently against each other inside the cloth under his arm.

"My grandmother farmed a settlement in the eastern valleys. Small — twelve families, one node. She told me that on certain nights, late in the season when the fields were resting, you could feel her in the ground. Not see. Feel. A presence underneath everything." He shifted the springs to his other arm. "She said it was the way you know someone is in the next room even though the door is closed. You don't see them. You don't hear them. You just know. The house feels different when someone's in it."

He didn't look at Caeleth. He didn't need to. He could feel the boy going still beside him — not the frozen stillness from the transport hub but a different kind, a listening kind, the body getting quiet so something else could pay attention.

"I don't know if that's real or if she was sentimental. But I remember her voice when she said it. She wasn't wondering. She was telling me a fact."

Caeleth didn't say anything for a long time. They walked. The streets narrowed and steepened as they climbed, the buildings pressing close, ironwood balconies overhead casting striped shadows across the cobblestones. Jorath could hear the boy breathing — slow, deep, the same rhythm from the bonding scene in the creature market.

"I build things out of wood and metal," Jorath said. "She builds things out of roots and dirt. We're not that different." He looked sideways at the boy. "Come on. I know a vendor who sells tubers that are worth exactly what they cost."

---

He stopped at a street vendor three blocks from the compound gate. The vendor was selling roasted tubers, skin-on, from a clay oven that radiated heat and caramelized starch and the particular sweetness of food cooked by someone who had been cooking the same thing in the same spot long enough that the oven had learned the recipe.

"Two," Jorath said. He paid without negotiating, which he never did, and he didn't look at Caeleth while he did it.

He handed one to the boy.

"For the walk," he said. "Don't tell Sorvyn."

Caeleth took the tuber with both hands.

They ate in silence the rest of the way up the hill, and Jorath's springs knocked gently against each other inside the cloth, and the numbers didn't balance, and he let them not.
