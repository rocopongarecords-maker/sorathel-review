# Episode Two — Chapter Seven
## The Room
*Drenna*

---

The streets widened three blocks past the river bridge and Drenna felt it in her ankles first.

Different stone. The lower city walked on compressed rubble — grey, uneven, patched where the drainage channels had been rerouted too many times. Up here the ground was quarried flat. Pale gold. Her boots — still carrying the red-brown of Mirathyn field soil in the stitching — landed differently, the impact softer, the sound wrong. Quieter. Even the street was restrained.

She passed a wall where a garden pressed against the stone from inside. Not the wild tangle of frontier yards, plants fighting for purchase in thin soil. This was arranged. Climbing vines trained along wire frames, their leaves broad and dark and fed by something richer than rain. The smell hit her two steps later — sweet, almost oily, the scent of flowers she didn't know the names for blooming in quantities that assumed water and warmth and attention. Behind the wall, she could hear the low chatter of a creature in a private enclosure. Not a working animal. A kept one.

The light changed. The lower city's lamps were spaced for function — every third corner, casting pools you walked between. Up here, the light was continuous. Steady. The shadows had been designed out.

She'd dressed in her best clothes from her time at the Thalvaren Solcarin. Fitted jacket, clean trousers, boots polished as much as field leather could be polished. The threadwork band at her wrist — local plant dyes, her mother's patterns — she hadn't taken off. She could have. She'd thought about it. The band marked her as frontier. She'd spent three years sanding down the accent. But the band stayed. She didn't examine why.

Maarthen's message had come that morning. Three words and an address: *Dinner tonight. Come.*

She'd come.

---

The house was rootwood and warm-quarried stone. Not a palace — a merchant's house, the kind of place that said wealth by saying nothing about wealth. The door was heavy, dark, oiled so the grain showed. The knocker was bronze, shaped into a bird she didn't recognize — something southern, long-necked, detailed enough to have been cast from life.

He opened it himself. No servant, no intermediary. Maarthen Daervos stood in his own doorway in a loose-collared shirt with the sleeves turned back, and his face broke into genuine pleasure when he saw her.

"Drenna." He clasped her hands — both of them, engulfing hers. His were broad, weathered, the knuckles thick from years that hadn't been spent in drawing rooms. Mid-fifties. A full head of graying hair swept back from a tanned forehead. He had the build of a man who'd loaded his own cargo once and hadn't entirely stopped. "You look like you've been scrubbing your boots for an hour."

"Two hours."

He laughed — a big sound, uncontained, the laugh of a man who'd learned long ago that a room would adjust to him, not the other way around. He pulled her inside.

The entry hall was a map. She understood that immediately. Every object on every surface came from somewhere — a carved stone figure from the southern settlements, a bolt of dyed cloth pinned to the wall in deep indigo and gold, brass navigational instruments she'd never seen before, a miniature model of a commercial carrier mounted on a stand of dark wood, every wing detailed, every rib visible. Maps. Actual maps — hand-drawn, annotated in Maarthen's cramped handwriting, charting routes that ran from the Hearthlands to the outer edges and beyond. One map showed territory past the network's reach. Places she'd heard named at frontier tables and nowhere else.

On a shelf near the door: a wooden figure, long-limbed, angular, carved in a style she didn't recognize. The wood was pale, unsanded, with a grain that ran in patterns too regular to be natural. It didn't come from inside the network.

She looked at it longer than she meant to.

"Ah." Maarthen followed her gaze. "That's Korvathi work. From beyond the Ashward edge. I'll tell you about it over dinner." He put a hand on her back — warm, proprietary — and steered her toward the sound of voices.

---

Five men stood in Maarthen's dining room. All holding glasses. All older than her. All at ease in a way that came from decades of good tables and full glasses and knowing which chair was theirs.

Maarthen introduced her with a hand still on her shoulder. "This is Drenna. I heard her speak at Thalvaren two years ago — frontier-born, top of her class — and I've been watching her work ever since. The sharpest mind to come out of Thalvaren in a generation." He squeezed her shoulder. "She's been doing fieldwork on the western frontier. Four communities."

The men nodded. Some smiled. None of them shifted where they stood.

Lucro was the first to speak to her directly. Older, lean, with the precise movements of a man who weighed things for a living — not with his hands, with his eyes. Everything about him was measurement. He traded Mirathyn across regions, and everyone at the table knew it. "Are you with the Amarith?"

"The Solvarei."

His face held. Polite. Blank. He glanced at Devriis, then back. "I'm not familiar."

"Private firm. Fieldwork — community-level diagnostics, branch and capillary maintenance. About three hundred people."


The second man — younger, maybe forty-five, with the soft hands of someone who'd never loaded his own cargo — introduced himself as Devriis. Land holder. Agricultural frontier. He asked her where she'd studied, and when she said Thalvaren, his eyebrows rose. "My daughter's in her first year."

The third man didn't introduce himself. He was the oldest in the room — perhaps seventy, with a face that had stopped trying to be anything other than what it was. White hair. Small, still eyes. He sat in the corner chair with a glass he hadn't touched and watched everything without blinking. She assumed by the looks he was was Hendryck as per Maarthes description. Thirty years of dinners and deals and games at this altitude. He spoke twice that evening. Both times, the table stopped.

The fourth man was closer to Maarthen's age, heavy through the shoulders, with restless hands that kept finding his glass, his chair, the edge of the table. He turned to her before Maarthen had finished speaking.

"How many handlers per circuit on the western routes?"

"Three, sometimes four. Depends on the terrain."

"And the creatures — are they refusing specific paths or entire regions?"

"Specific paths first. Then the paths connect and the refusal widens."

"What about ground transport? Anyone moving cargo overland out there?"

"Some. Slow. The roads aren't built for it — the frontier was designed around flight."

He leaned back and looked at Maarthen. A nod. Not to her — to him. 

Maarthen poured her glass himself. Mirathyn — she knew the smell before the glass was full. Mineral, warm, the gold sweetness. He poured from a dark bottle without a label, the kind of bottle that assumed you knew what was inside.

She picked up the glass. Sipped. It sat in her throat. She'd been swallowing Mirathyn since she was twelve years old. It had never stopped feeling wrong.

"Delverath caves," Maarthen said, watching her face. "Forty years underground."

"Extraordinary," she said, and drank again.

---

The food arrived in courses, and every course was a route.

Roasted bird in a southern glaze — sweet, smoky, the skin crisped until it crackled between her teeth. Root vegetables she recognized from the frontier, but roasted in an oil that turned a food she'd eaten a hundred times into a food she'd never had. Fish laid on quarry stone still warm from whatever had heated it — the flesh white, translucent, seasoned with grains of something coarse and bright. And the bread. The bread was the thing that stopped her. Dense, dark, crusted with seeds, and inside: soft, warm, the taste of earth and grain and time.

She ate. She tried not to stop chewing when the flavors hit. She mostly failed. The men around the table reached for bread and tore it without looking. They refilled their glasses without checking the level. The food was furniture to them — reliable, assumed, beneath notice. Drenna noticed everything.

The dinner was what it looked like — men who knew each other eating at a friend's table. Devriis described his daughter's first semester at Thalvaren and the letter she'd sent complaining about a philosophy instructor. Lucro argued with Maarthen about a supplier in the southern settlements — a disagreement so familiar it had become comedy, both men hitting their marks, the table laughing at the rhythm. Hendryck said nothing. The restless man asked Devriis about land prices in the eastern regions and listened with the particular attention of someone already calculating.

And then Maarthen told the story.

He leaned back, glass in hand — his second, he drank freely, comfortably, the glass an extension of his ease — and said: "I ever tell you about the Korvathi?"

Some groans. Lucro: "Only every dinner." Maarthen waved him off.

"Twenty years ago. My first route past the Ashward edge. Past the network's reach. Two weeks out. The creatures don't go willingly — you have to convince them, and even then, they get slow. They feel the Amarvel thinning underneath them and they want to turn back."

"What's past the edge?" Devriis.

"People." Maarthen's voice shifted — not softer, wider. "Not settlers. Not frontiersmen gone wild. People with families, towns, trade between themselves. They've been there — well, nobody knows how long. Before the last turning some say, maybe. Before anyone kept records about the network."

He swirled his glass. "They don't bond creatures. Can't — there's nothing to bond through out there. So they BUILD. Carts. Platforms on wheels. Tools for moving across ground that would make our engineers stare. Slow compared to flight, but they don't have flight, and what they've built instead—"

The restless man leaned forward. "What kind of platforms? How do they handle inclines?"

"Ropes. Pulleys. Counterweights carved from stone." Maarthen grinned. "I tried to buy one. They wouldn't sell."

"They don't follow the Turnings," Lucro said. Flat.

"They have their own reckoning. Their own names for the old histories — the First Root, the Branching, the Long Silence. They call those by other words entirely. The Branching isn't a blessing in their telling. It's the thing that pushed them to the margins."

"And the creatures?" Devriis had set his fork down. "What do they do with the creatures out there?"

Maarthen paused. "They eat them."

The table shifted. Devriis looked at his plate. Lucro made a sound in his throat.

"The creatures beyond the edge aren't bonded," Maarthen said. "No network, no bond. They're animals out there. Just animals."

"They're still creatures," Devriis said quietly.

"They are." Maarthen took a long drink. "And the Korvathi are still people. I traded with them for three seasons. Good trade. Honest. They have a word for us — *Telvareth.* The ones who listen to roots. Not unfriendly. Accurate."

Lucro shook his head. "Savages who eat creatures and don't recognize the Turnings. And we opened trade routes to them."

"We made money from them," Maarthen corrected. "Good money. And their cloth — rougher than anything at this table, but dyed in colors I've never seen reproduced inside the network. Some of those pieces are in my hall." He gestured toward the entry. "The figure by the door. The indigo bolt on the wall. They have their own beauty, Lucro. Whether you approve of it."

He described them with the pleasure of a man recounting a world that had expanded him. Their food — cured meats and preserved roots, everything built to last through seasons that arrived harder than anything inside the network. Their word for bonding: *valeth-na.* Root-listening. Said with curiosity, not contempt. "Strong people," Maarthen said. "Broad. They walk everywhere — no flight, no bond-runners. Their legs are thicker than ours. Their hands are harder."

"Are there more of them now?" The restless man. Practical.

Maarthen tilted his head. "They've always been there. More of them lately, maybe. Hard to say — I haven't run that route in years. Too long, too slow, the creatures don't want to go that far anymore. The edges used to feel farther away. Routes I ran twenty years ago are shorter now — not because I'm faster, but because there's less distance to cover before you hit the point where the creatures won't go."

Devriis frowned. "The frontier reports mention incursions. Strangers at the edges."

"Displacement events," Lucro corrected, with the particular distaste of a man who believed the right word for a thing was the word that made it manageable.

"Whatever you call it, the western handlers won't fly the Torven circuit anymore."

The conversation moved. Maarthen refilled Devriis's glass. Lucro returned to the bread. The outer edges went back to being a line item — something that existed, that cost money, that someone would eventually handle.

---

Lucro brought up Torven's valley while comparing regional yields. A trade conversation, nothing more. "Three years ago, Torven's valley produced the cleanest Mirathyn on the western circuit. Clear gold, held its color in the glass. Now it sits heavier. My buyers in the Hearthlands notice."

Devriis nodded. "The families are drifting east from that region. Census down three seasons running."

"My father tended the node at Torven's valley."

The room adjusted. Not dramatically — a fork set down, heads turning. Lucro looked at her.

"What's the node reading at now?"

"Barely a shimmer. At midday you can see it if you know where to look."

"And the quality decline? The specialists said it was cyclical."

"The water used to taste like iron from the mineral runoff. Now it tastes like nothing." She kept her voice level. "The families who stayed have gotten used to it. That's the part that worries me — people stop noticing."

Hendryck's eyes moved to her. First time. He said nothing. But his glass shifted a quarter turn in his hand.

Maarthen watched from his seat. His face had the satisfaction of a man whose bet was paying in front of the table.

Then she said something she hadn't planned. "He used to tap the ground with his heel when he was around the node. A rhythm — three taps, then he'd listen. I'd hear it from the house. Every morning. Every evening." She stopped. "He hasn't tapped in years."

That wasn't leverage. That wasn't evidence she could use. That was a girl sitting among men who'd never been to Torven's valley, remembering a sound that had been the rhythm of her childhood, and missing it.

Then she picked up her glass and drank.

---

Korvaan arrived between the fish and the dessert.

Drenna didn't see him enter. She heard the room change. The restless man straightened in his chair. Devriis adjusted his collar. Lucro stopped mid-sentence — a calculation about southern yields — and the pause lasted a half-beat too long. Even Hendryck, who hadn't moved all evening, shifted his weight.

Then the doorway filled with a man who didn't fill it.

Medium height. Weathered face — deep lines around the mouth, sun damage at the temples, the skin of someone who'd spent his first thirty years outdoors regardless of what his station was now. Hair cropped short, steel-gray, receding at the temples without apology. Hands at his sides, thick-knuckled, scarred across the backs. Hands that had learned tools before anything else.

But the clothes. The clothes were impeccable. Dark cloth, tailored close — the jacket sat on his shoulders without a single crease, the collar exact, the cuffs precisely the right length. No ornament. No insignia. The quality was in the fabric itself, in the cut of every seam, in the way nothing moved that wasn't supposed to move. Drenna knew what good cloth cost. This was the most expensive thing in the room, worn by the man who ate the plainest food.

"Korvaan." Maarthen rose, crossed the room, clasped his hand. Warm. Unhurried. Two men who occupied different territories of the same landscape. "I wasn't sure you'd come."

"I said I'd come." His voice was flat, unhurried, declarative. He took the chair Maarthen offered — at the corner of the table, not the head — and when the food was placed before him, he chose the bread and the root vegetables and ignored the fish entirely. Old habit. A body that remembered when food was fuel.

Drenna watched him eat. She'd seen that choosing before. At her father's table. At the frontier wayhouses where practitioners ate what was available and didn't comment. Korvaan ate the way people ate where she came from.

She was still awed by the fish. He'd been here long enough to refuse it.

Maarthen leaned toward Korvaan, gesturing down the table toward Drenna. "You should talk to her. Top of her year at Thalvaren, just finished a frontier rotation — four communities, the western circuit. It's a shame your people haven't recruited her, Korvaan. She's the best talent I've come across in twenty years."

"The Amarith doesn't take rotations." Korvaan didn't raise his voice. "We take practitioners who've earned their place through demonstrated excellence at tier. When she's ready, she'll be evaluated. Not before."

Maarthen's face held for a beat. Then the half-smile — practiced, warm, the expression of a man who'd navigated these conversations for decades and knew when to give ground. "You'd have said the same thing about me thirty years ago, Korvaan. And you'd have been right."

Lucro laughed. Devriis smiled into his glass. Korvaan let the moment pass without acknowledgment. He'd made his point. The point was not about Maarthen.

But he'd looked at HER when he said *when she's ready.*

Not at Maarthen. Not at the table. At Drenna. Dark eyes, still, unhurried. Holding for one beat. Two. A look that found the line of least resistance, the hidden fracture, the place where pressure would split the wood. She felt her posture change — spine straightening, breath catching, her left hand finding the threadwork band at her wrist. Something in her chest contracted. Not fear. Not recognition. Something older — her body's response to being seen by someone who saw everything.

Then his eyes released. He turned to Hendryck and asked a question about territorial defense assessments, and the table rearranged itself around his voice.

She spent the rest of the dinner watching him. He spoke less than anyone. He asked three questions. Each one was the question nobody else had been willing to ask. When Devriis mentioned frontier incursions, Korvaan said: "Organized armed groups with territorial objectives." Five words. The table went quiet. Not because he'd said something alarming — because he'd said something accurate, and accuracy was uncomfortable when everyone else was speaking in euphemisms.

He didn't finish his glass. She'd been watching the level. It hadn't moved.

---

After the dessert — a custard she couldn't name and didn't want — Maarthen pushed back from the table. "Gentlemen. Tiles?"

A shift. The men were standing, moving toward a back room she hadn't seen. Through the doorway she caught a glimpse: a table covered in felt, tile racks, a cabinet of dark wood holding sets in different materials — bone, quarried stone, polished rootwood. The restless man was already reaching for a rack. Devriis was rolling his sleeves. Even Hendryck had risen from his corner, moving with the unhurried certainty of a man who'd been winning this game for thirty years.

Maarthen walked her toward the front entrance. His hand on her back again — warm, paternal, proprietary.

"You did well tonight," he said. He meant it. The warmth was genuine — the warmth of a man who'd placed a bet and was watching it pay. "The Torven detail. Good."

"Thank you."

"The next gathering is in three weeks. Velanthra. Bigger room, different people. I'd like you there." He said it with the certainty of a man who assumed his invitations were gifts. "Think about what you'd want to say if someone asked you about the frontier. Not the field data — the story. These people understand stories."

She nodded. He clasped her hands again, the same two-handed grip, the same broad warmth. Then he turned and walked toward the back room, and the door closed, and the sound of tiles being shuffled came through the wood, and she was standing alone in the entrance hall.

Not alone.

Korvaan was at the door. Pulling on a coat — dark, beautifully cut, the same restrained quality as everything he wore. He wasn't staying for the game.

Her throat was tight. She was twenty years old and the most important person she'd ever met was three paces away and reaching for the door handle.

"I'd like to be considered for the Amarith."

Korvaan paused. Half-turned — his hand still on the door.

"You hated the Mirathyn."

Not a question.

Her breath caught. He'd been watching. While he appeared to be talking to Hendryck, while he asked about territorial defense assessments, while the table bent around his silence — he'd seen her drink something she hated and say *extraordinary* and drink again.

"Nobody at that table noticed." He opened the door. "That's your answer."

He walked into the dark and the door closed behind him and she stood in Maarthen's entry hall with the pale Korvathi carving watching her from its shelf.

---

The streets narrowed around her as she descended.

The pale stone gave way to grey. The continuous light broke into pools with dark intervals between them. The gardens behind walls disappeared, replaced by bare stone fronts and shuttered windows and the smell of cook-fires. Her boots found the familiar uneven surface and her ankles adjusted.

She stopped at a vendor on the corner of the market road. A woman wrapping up for the night, a brazier with tubers still warm in the ash. Drenna bought one and a piece of flatbread. She ate walking. The tuber was honest — dense, earthy, seasoned with nothing but heat. She chewed and thought.

Two kinds of power in that room. Maarthen's kind: built on routes, reputation, warmth, the ability to fill a table with important men and pour them expensive Mirathyn. He knew everyone. He'd been everywhere. He opened doors with a handshake and a story. Real power. She admired it. She wanted it.

But Korvaan had walked in and the geometry shifted. The foxes had money and land and three decades of knowing each other's names. Korvaan had none of that. He had the Amarith — and the Amarith read the entire network while the foxes read their individual routes. He ate the plainest food and spoke the fewest words and the room bent toward him. Different species entirely.

She wanted what he had.

The foxes solved problems one at a time. A route delay, a yield decline, a census drop. Each one separate. Each one solvable with the tools they knew — a new supplier, a different path, an incentive for the families. Individual solutions from individual men. They were brilliant at it. They'd been brilliant at it for decades.

But whoever saw the whole board — whoever could stand in front of a room of foxes and say *these are not separate problems* with the kind of specificity that made dismissal impossible — that person wouldn't be sitting at the table. That person would be running it.

She had something none of them had. Four communities. Ground-level data. The taste of water that had gone flat, the sound of creatures refusing paths, the sight of a girl who wouldn't enter the field. She knew what the frontier looked like from inside it. And the frontier was coming for the rest of Sorathel — she'd heard it in Lucro's yield numbers, in Maarthen's route delays, in Devriis's census figures. The same thing, moving inward.

Whoever held that intelligence when the crisis reached the upper city would become essential. Not just useful — necessary. The person everyone needed in the room.

She passed a garden wall. The vines looked different from the ones she'd seen on the way up — thinner, the leaves smaller, the green faded toward grey at the edges. She noticed. Kept walking.

*That's your answer.*

What answer? That she was good at hiding? That she shouldn't hide? That the Amarith saw what the foxes couldn't? She'd been turning his words since the door closed and they didn't resolve. He'd given her something with no clean edges — something she'd carry and turn and carry again, and each time she turned it the shape would be different.

Maarthen would open doors. He'd open the next one in Velanthra, and the one after that, and each door would put her in front of people who had more than she did and knew less than she could about the thing that was coming. She'd use every door. She'd use Maarthen's warmth and Lucro's trade concerns and her own story — the father, the node, the water — because her story was true and it was useful and both of those things could exist in the same hand.

But the Amarith was the ceiling. Not a door — a destination. Korvaan hadn't climbed through fox dinners. He'd built something that didn't need invitations. And he'd looked at her — twice — with the eyes of someone deciding whether a thing was worth the investment.

She'd make him decide yes.

The threadwork band at her wrist was warm against her skin. Her father's heel against the ground, three taps, then listening. She hadn't taken the band off. She hadn't drunk the Mirathyn without flinching. She hadn't fooled Korvaan. But she'd fooled every fox at that table, and she was twenty years old.

She kept walking.
