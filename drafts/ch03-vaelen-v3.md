# Episode One — Chapter Three
## The Node
*Vaelen*

---

The node woke him.

Not a sound. Nodes didn't make sounds, not in the way anyone who wasn't a pattern reader would understand the word. But something had shifted in the small hours — a change in the rhythm he felt through the floor of the wayhouse, through the stone foundation, through the Amarvel roots that ran beneath this settlement like veins beneath skin. He'd been asleep and then he wasn't, and the transition had the quality of a finger pressed against his collarbone. Not pain. Pressure. Attention demanded.

He lay still. The wayhouse was dark. Sorvyn breathing slowly by the door. Jorath's silence — Jorath was a silent sleeper, which had unnerved Vaelen on their first deployment together until he'd realized the man simply refused to waste energy even unconsciously. Caeleth's breathing was faster, shallow, the breathing of someone caught in a dream he wasn't enjoying. Vaelen thought about waking him and didn't. The boy needed sleep more than rescue from whatever his mind was doing to him.

Drenna's bed was empty. He'd noticed.

He'd noticed the way he noticed everything — automatically, without decision, the information arriving the way light arrives through an open window. She'd left before he did. She'd come back while he was at the node, her boots carrying forty minutes' worth of a different soil — darker red, more clay, the mineral content of ground that sat above a weaker section of the Amarvel. She'd been east. She hadn't told anyone.

He let the observation sit. That was what he did with information — held it, let it settle, let the pattern it belonged to make itself visible. Vaelen's mind had been doing this since he was old enough to walk. His mother had called it his quiet, the way he'd go still at the table and his eyes would move and she'd know he was reading something nobody else could see. At the academy they'd called it sensitivity. At the Solvarei assessment they'd called it extraordinary. He called it nothing. It was like calling breathing something. It was the thing he did.

The node pulsed again. Off-rhythm. He could feel it the way you feel a stone in your boot — not agonizing, but persistent, occupying attention that should be free for other things.

He got up. Dressed in the dark. His body knew the routine: boots, vest, the field knife he'd carried since his third deployment not because he expected to use it but because the weight of it on his hip was a small certainty in a profession built on uncertainty. He stepped over Sorvyn's outstretched legs — Sorvyn didn't stir, but his breathing changed for one cycle, which meant he'd registered Vaelen's movement and decided it wasn't a threat — and went outside.

The cold hit first. Then the dark. Then the Amarvel.

It was always there, of course. The Amarvel was always there. But during the day, with the noise of people and wind and the settlement's small business, it was a background hum — a river you lived beside and stopped hearing. At night, in silence, the hum became the only thing. Vaelen stood on the wayhouse step and felt it rise through his boots and into the bones of his feet and up his legs into his hips and spine, a vibration so deep it was almost below feeling, like standing on the chest of something enormous that was breathing.

The Amarvel beneath Torven's community was old. Vaelen could tell the age the way a woodworker could tell the age of a beam — by density, by resonance. Old roots vibrated differently from young ones. They were slower. Deeper. They carried more information because they'd carried it longer, and the information had layered and compressed until it had its own texture, its own weight. Reading old Amarvel was like putting your hand flat against a wall and feeling, through the stone, the accumulated years of the house — every fire lit, every meal cooked, every child born, every body laid out for the dead. The Amarvel remembered. Not the way people remembered, with stories and distortion and the gentle erosion of time. The Amarvel remembered like stone remembers. Exactly. Permanently. Without opinion.

He walked to the node.

The shimmer was almost invisible in the dark — just a faint disturbance in the moonlight, as if the air above the concentration point were slightly thicker than the air around it. In a healthy system, the shimmer would pulse gold-green, steady as a heartbeat. This one pulsed, but the rhythm had a hitch in it — a missed beat every eight or nine cycles, like a drummer who kept losing the count. Vaelen had been tracking the hitch since yesterday. It was getting worse. Not faster. Wider. The pause between the eighth and ninth beat was lengthening, as if the Amarvel were gathering its strength for each cycle and needing more time to do it.

He stopped at the node's edge. Closed his eyes.

And the world changed.

Not visually. He wasn't seeing anything — his eyes were shut, his face turned toward the sky. But the Amarvel opened beneath him the way a conversation opens between two people who've been waiting to speak. He felt the roots first — the major trunks running north-south beneath the settlement, thick as old trees, carrying the slow deep pulse that was Amarwen's own rhythm transmitted across hundreds of miles of living tissue. The branches off the trunks, thinner, faster, spreading east and west toward the fields. The capillaries at the edges, hair-thin, fragile, reaching into the soil beneath the Mirathyn rows where they fed and were fed in turn.

Each layer had its own voice. The trunks were bass — slow, resonant, the kind of sound that lives in the floor rather than the air. The branches were midrange, complex, carrying traffic: nutrients moving in one direction, information moving in the other, the constant negotiation between what the community needed and what the Amarvel could provide. The capillaries were high and fast and anxious, the chatter of a frontier where resources were thinning and the edges were pulling back.

Vaelen listened. He listened the way you listen to a sick friend's breathing — not for the sound, but for what the sound was telling you about the body making it.

The trunks were strong. That was good. Whatever was wrong here, the main conduits were intact, still carrying Amarwen's pulse from the deep roots near the Hearthlands out to this frontier node. The signal was tired — Vaelen could feel the fatigue like a taste at the back of his mouth, metallic and thin — but it was arriving. The infrastructure was holding.

The branches were where the trouble started. The east-west network that served the Mirathyn fields was thinner than it should have been — not broken, not damaged, just... less. As if the Amarvel had been slowly pulling resources back from the edges, consolidating toward the trunks the way a cold body pulls blood from the fingers to keep the heart warm. Eighteen months of this. Gradual enough that no single day looked different from the last. But Vaelen could feel the accumulated absence — the places where the branches had been full and were now half-empty, the capillaries that had once reached the farthest field rows and now stopped short by ten, twenty, thirty meters.

The dead zone in the eastern fields wasn't a failure. It was a retreat. The Amarvel was pulling back, and the community was living on what remained, and what remained was enough — technically, on paper, by the instruments' measure — but it was enough the way a half-meal is enough. It stops the hunger without providing what the body needs.

He went deeper.

This was the part that cost him. The surface reading — the trunks, the branches, the general health of the local network — was easy. He could do it while walking, while talking, while eating smokebread one-handed on a shaking carriage. It was like glancing at the weather. But going deep meant letting the Amarvel's information replace his own senses, and that was a transaction with a price. The world above — the cold air, the moonlight, the sound of his own breathing — receded. The Amarvel's world replaced it. And the Amarvel's world was vast and slow and did not care about the small warm thing standing above it trying to listen.

He felt the downstream deficit Sorvyn had asked about. There — south-southwest, maybe forty miles along the main trunk. A draw on the network so steady and sustained that Torven's node had been compensating for it without the community knowing. The trunk was carrying resources in a direction they shouldn't be flowing, like a river running uphill. Something down there was pulling. Something was taking more than its share, and the Amarvel, in its blind mechanical way, was providing — redistributing, balancing, keeping the signal alive by stealing from the edges to feed whatever was eating from the center.

Vaelen mapped it. The deficit had a shape — not circular, not random. Directional. A line running south-southwest from this node toward... where? He pushed further and the signal blurred. Too far. His range had limits, and at forty miles through stressed Amarvel, the detail dissolved into static. But the direction was clear. Something was pulling. Something was hungry. And the Amarvel was feeding it because the Amarvel fed everything that asked, because that was what the Amarvel was *for*, because the roots didn't distinguish between a community that needed sustenance and a deficit that would swallow everything it was given.

He came up. Gasped. The surface world flooded back — cold air, the shock of hearing his own heartbeat again after the Amarvel's slower rhythm. His hands were shaking. They always shook after a deep read. He closed them into fists, pressed them against his thighs, and waited for the trembling to stop.

It took longer than it used to.

He stood in the dark and thought about what Drenna had shown him. Four communities. The same sequence. The gap between what the Amarvel reported and what was happening to the people. She'd walked those communities, talked to those families, tracked the decline with a method that his training hadn't given him and couldn't replicate — the method of someone who'd grown up inside the thing she was studying. Her observation and his reading overlapped like two transparencies laid one on top of the other, and where they overlapped, the picture sharpened.

The Amarvel was retreating. It was pulling resources from the frontier edges toward the main trunks, and from the trunks toward somewhere south-southwest. The communities on the edges were being starved — slowly, within parameters, technically recoverable — while the Amarvel fed something deeper in the network that Vaelen couldn't see yet. The instruments measured the edges and found decline within seasonal variance. The instruments were correct. The instruments were also useless, because the problem wasn't the edges. The problem was what was pulling.

He needed to follow the trunk south-southwest. He needed to get closer to whatever was drawing the Amarvel's resources. But that was forty miles through thinning network, and he had a community here that needed help today, not next week.

Right. Today first.

---

Dawn came gray and windless. Vaelen had slept another two hours after the reading — not enough, but enough to make the headache manageable. He'd learned years ago that the body's protest after a deep read wasn't negotiable. You paid the cost or the cost took what it was owed without asking. A headache behind the left eye. A faint ringing that would fade by midday. The metallic taste that stayed longer.

He was eating boiled grain at the communal table — the grain was adequate, the water was nothing, he drank it without flinching because flinching accomplished nothing — when Sorvyn sat down across from him.

"You went out last night."

"The node's rhythm shifted. I wanted to track it."

"You look terrible."

"Thank you, Sorvyn."

"The deep reading." Not a question. Sorvyn had been with him long enough to recognize the signs — the shadows under the eyes darkening by a shade, the left hand cradling the cup too carefully, the way Vaelen held his head slightly still to keep the headache from shifting. "You could have waited until morning."

"The Amarvel's quieter at night. Less traffic. The deep structure's easier to read."

"The deep structure is always there. Your body isn't always up for the conversation." Sorvyn drank his tea. "What did you find?"

Vaelen looked at his grain. At the table. At the settlement waking up around them — Torven's daughter carrying water, a child feeding a small furred creature that was sitting on a fence post with its tail wrapped around the railing, a man sweeping the path between houses with the slow, even strokes of someone who'd been sweeping the same path every morning for decades. The community was alive. It was moving. It was doing the things communities do. And underneath all of it, the Amarvel was pulling back, one capillary at a time, like a tide going out so slowly that the fish didn't notice until they were gasping on dry sand.

"Something's drawing on the trunk line south-southwest. Forty miles or more. Steady pull, sustained. This node's been compensating — redistributing its own resources to feed the deficit. That's why the eastern fields died. The Amarvel chose the trunk over the edges."

"The Amarvel chose?"

"The Amarvel balanced. The way it always does — resources flow toward the greatest demand. The trunk's demand is greater because something downstream is taking. The edges starve. It's not malice. It's physics." He paused. "Or it's the closest thing the Amarvel has to physics."

Sorvyn was quiet for a moment. "Can you fix the balance?"

"I can redirect the local flow. Reinforce the branches, strengthen the capillary connections to the fields. Buy them time — a growing season, maybe two. But if the downstream pull doesn't stop, the Amarvel will redirect again. I'd be bailing water from a boat with a hole in it."

"Then bail. Give them the season. We'll figure out the hole."

Vaelen smiled. Not the careful one — the real one that arrived before he could shape it. He'd discovered at sixteen that his warmth could steady a room if he let it, and somewhere in the years since he'd started choosing when to let it. Both things were true at once — the gladness was real, the awareness of its effect was also real — and he'd stopped trying to separate them the way he'd stopped trying to separate his left eye from his right. He could be glad and deliberate at the same time. He just wished, sometimes, that he couldn't tell the difference.

"I'll need everyone for this," he said. "The branch work is going to pull flow from the main trunk, and I need someone watching the trunk pulse downstream to make sure I'm not starving it. If I redirect too much, the deficit gets worse." He looked at Sorvyn. "That's you. Southern marker. Hands on the ground. You'll feel the pulse slow if I'm pulling too hard."

Sorvyn nodded. The kind of task that suited him — patient, essential, requiring the steady attention he had more of than anyone Vaelen knew.

"Caeleth is with me at the junction. He's going to help me track the flow at the field edge." He paused. "And Jorath — tell him the settlement's south wall collapsed into the drainage channel. He'll want to fix it. But tell him to follow the drainage south. Where it meets the secondary branch line, the ground's been subsiding — roots retreating below the surface, soil collapsing into the gap. If the path is pinched, the flow I'm pushing won't reach the eastern fields. He needs to clear it."

"You want Jorath digging."

"I want Jorath solving a problem that involves digging. There's a difference. He'll figure out the subsidence before I finish explaining it."

Sorvyn almost smiled. It was the closest Vaelen had seen in days. Then he stood and walked away with his hands clasped behind his back, steady pace, and Vaelen watched him go and thought: *he trusts me completely and I am not at all certain I deserve it*.

"And find Drenna," Vaelen called after him. "Tell her I need her at the eastern field edge by midmorning, while she's still on our rotation. She knows something about the soil out there that I need."

He finished his grain. The headache was fading. The metallic taste was not. He went to work.

---

The branch work looked like nothing from the outside. A man kneeling in dirt with his palms flat on the ground and his eyes closed. From the inside, it was an argument with something the size of a continent — and the continent was winning.

Vaelen knelt at the junction where the main trunk line passed beneath the settlement's western edge — a spot he'd identified by feel, where the root structure was thickest and the branches diverged toward the fields. Fifty paces south, he could see Sorvyn settling into position at the southern marker, hands already pressed to the earth, his face carrying the focused stillness of a man who was listening. Somewhere beyond the houses, Jorath was tracing the drainage channel. Vaelen had given him ten minutes before starting the redirect. Probably didn't need five — the man had looked at the collapsed wall yesterday evening and Vaelen had watched his eyes track the problem southward, past the wall, past the channel, already mapping.

The Amarvel didn't think. He'd made that mistake early in his training — projecting intent onto a system that had none. The Amarvel responded. It flowed where the signal was strongest, pulled back where the signal weakened, redistributed along gradients that were as mindless and as elegant as water finding its level. Vaelen's gift was that he could feel those gradients — and, with effort, shift them. The effort was the thing people underestimated. You weren't coaxing a stream. You were leaning against a current that had been flowing the same direction for longer than this settlement had existed, and the current did not want to change.

He found the branch that fed the western fields. Felt along it — here, where the flow was strong, and here, where it thinned, and here, at the capillary level, where the Mirathyn roots were reaching down into soil that the Amarvel had half-abandoned. The capillaries were still alive. They were just... quiet. Waiting for a signal that had stopped arriving.

Vaelen gave them the signal.

It wasn't magic — or rather, it was exactly magic, in the way that everything the Amarvel did was magic, but it felt more like coaxing than commanding. He leaned his awareness into the branch, pressed against the gradient, and created a small bright insistence: *here. Flow here. This matters.* Not in words. In the language the Amarvel understood, which was the language of need — pressure, direction, the warmth of living roots reaching for sustenance.

The branch resisted. The Amarvel had been pulling back from this section for months, and the gradient had settled into a pattern that wanted to be maintained. Changing it was like pushing a boulder uphill — not impossible, but the boulder had opinions about where it preferred to sit.

He held the push and waited for Caeleth.

"Vaelen?"

The boy's voice had the careful quality of someone trying not to interrupt. Vaelen held the read with one part of his mind — the trained part, the part that could maintain a connection the way a juggler maintains three balls while having a conversation — and opened his eyes.

"Kneel here." He indicated a spot two meters to his left, directly above the junction. "Put your palms flat. Close your eyes. Tell me what you feel."

Caeleth knelt. His hands pressed the earth. His eyes shut with the tight-muscled concentration of someone terrified of failing.

A pause.

"Cold," Caeleth said. "The ground is cold."

"What else?"

A longer pause. Caeleth's fingers dug at the soil, pressing harder, as if force could break through to whatever was underneath. It couldn't — the Amarvel didn't respond to force. It responded to presence, to the quality of attention you brought to it, the willingness to be inside something vastly larger than yourself without trying to control it.

"Nothing," Caeleth said. His voice was tight. "I can't — there's nothing."

"There is. You're reaching for it. Stop reaching."

Caeleth tried. Vaelen could see the effort in his shoulders — the conscious attempt to relax, which was its own kind of tension. The boy pressed his palms flatter. His jaw worked. After a moment he opened his eyes, and the frustration in them was sharp enough to cut.

"I don't know what I'm supposed to feel."

"Close your eyes again. Don't listen for the Amarvel. Listen for the thing you already know." Vaelen kept his voice easy, the way you'd talk near a skittish animal. "You've stood beside a Korveth in a harness house. You know what it feels like to be near something alive that's bigger than you — the heat of it, the way the air changes, the way your body knows before your mind does that something enormous is right there. The Amarvel is like that. You don't reach for a Korveth. You just stand close enough, and it arrives."

Caeleth closed his eyes. This time the tension in his shoulders loosened — not fully, but enough. His fingers stopped pressing. His hands lay flat, receiving rather than reaching. Vaelen watched his face change: the concentration softening, the jaw unclenching, the brow smoothing as the effort shifted from pushing outward to opening inward.

Then — there. A flicker in Caeleth's expression. The faintest catch in his breathing, the way you catch your breath when something moves at the edge of your vision. He'd felt something. A brush. The barest edge of the vast thing beneath them, passing across his awareness the way a whale passes beneath a boat — you don't see it, but the water moves and you know.

His eyes flew open. His hands lifted from the ground as if the soil had burned him.

"What was that?"

"That was the trunk." Vaelen kept his voice level. Easy. "You touched the edge of it. Why did you pull back?"

Caeleth looked at his hands. "It was — I didn't expect —" He stopped. Tried again. "It's so *big*."

"It is."

"How do you stay in it?"

"You stop trying to hold it. You're not holding the Korveth. You're standing beside it. Same thing." He watched the boy's face — the fear genuine, the wanting under it just as real. "Hands down. Try again."

The third time, Caeleth went in with his eyes already different. Not the tight focus of someone bracing. Something more open, more reckless — the expression of someone who's decided to step off the edge and see what happens. His palms settled on the ground. His breathing slowed. And this time he didn't flinch.

Vaelen felt the boy's presence arrive in the network the way you feel a new instrument enter a piece of music — tentative, slightly off-key, but unmistakably there. Caeleth's sensitivity was real. Rough where Vaelen's was precise, blunt where his was sharp, but present. The seed was there.

"Something... humming," Caeleth said. His voice was different now. Quieter. Awed. "Very low. Like — like standing near a wall that has bees behind it. You can't hear them exactly. But you know they're there."

"Good. That's the main trunk. Now — can you feel where it branches? There should be a point somewhere between us where the hum splits. One direction keeps going north-south. The other turns toward the fields."

Caeleth was quiet. His face was still, concentrated, but the fear was gone. In its place was something Vaelen recognized — the wonder of being inside the Amarvel for the first time. The discovery that you are standing on something alive that spans a continent and it has noticed you.

"I think..." Caeleth's hands shifted slightly. "There. It's — it's like the hum changes direction. Bends."

"That's the junction. You're on it." Finding a trunk-branch junction on your second deployment. The boy's sensitivity was better than half the practitioners Vaelen had worked with, and those practitioners had years of training. "Now I'm going to push more flow into that branch. You're going to feel it — a change in the vibration, like turning up the volume on the bees. Your job is to tell me when it reaches the field edge. Can you feel that far?"

"I don't know."

"Neither did I, the first time. Try."

Vaelen closed his eyes and leaned into the gradient. The boulder-on-a-hill resistance was still there, but he'd been working it all morning and the pattern was loosening. He pushed. The branch brightened — not visibly, not in any way an observer would see, but in the language of the Amarvel, the flow increased. Resources moving west toward the fields. Capillaries waking. The thin silent threads reaching into the soil beginning to hum again, faintly, like a sleeping instrument being lifted from its case.

And then something happened that he could see.

The Mirathyn stalks nearest the junction — the ones within ten paces, closest to the strengthening branch — shifted. Not moved. Shifted. Their leaves caught the morning light differently, the dull gray-green deepening to something richer, warmer, as if the color had been waiting beneath the surface and the returning flow had drawn it out. The soil around the closest roots darkened with moisture that hadn't been there a moment ago — the Amarvel bringing water up through capillaries that had been dry for weeks. And above the junction itself, the shimmer — nearly invisible in daylight — brightened for two heartbeats, a faint gold-green wash in the air like sunlight through deep water, and then faded.

After ten years of reading the Amarvel through feel and sound and the deep interior senses that no one else could share, he'd almost forgotten that the network could be *seen*. That Amarwen's life expressed itself in color and light and the visible world, not just the felt one.

"It's moving," Caeleth said. His voice had changed. The anxiety was gone. "I can feel it — it's spreading, like water filling channels. The bees are getting louder toward the fields. It's—"

He stopped. His eyes opened. His face was strange — half awe, half something Vaelen couldn't read.

"What?"

"It's beautiful," Caeleth said. He sounded embarrassed by the word. He shouldn't have been.

"It is," Vaelen said. And for a moment — a brief, unguarded moment that he didn't choose and couldn't have replicated — his voice carried something that wasn't the warmth he knew how to offer or the steadiness he'd learned to hold. It carried the thing underneath: the raw, helpless love for the thing he could feel beneath his hands. The Amarvel. The living network. The vast breathing fact of it. He'd loved it since the first time he'd felt it, the way some people love the ocean — not because it was kind, not because it cared, but because it was so much bigger than he was that standing inside it made every small and broken thing about him irrelevant.

Then the moment passed. He caught it leaving — the warmth folding back into the shape people expected, his face settling into the version that knew what it was doing. He'd been catching these doors closing since he was old enough to notice, and noticing hadn't taught him how to leave them open.

"Keep feeling the edge," he said. "Tell me when the flow reaches the last field row."

From the south, Sorvyn's voice, steady and unhurried: "Trunk pulse is holding. You've got room."

Vaelen pushed harder. The gradient shifted another degree. The Mirathyn rows further from the junction were responding now — a subtle wave of color change moving outward like dawn traveling across a hillside, each row catching the returning flow and brightening in turn.

Caeleth closed his eyes and went back in. Vaelen watched him for a second — the concentration, the wonder, the transparent wanting — and something old stirred in his chest. Not a thought. A feeling. The memory of being that young and that open and not yet knowing what it would cost.

He turned back to the work.

---

By midmorning the western branches were running stronger than they had in months. Vaelen could feel the difference — a warmth in the soil, a responsiveness in the capillaries, the Mirathyn rows standing straighter with color returning to their leaves like blood returning to a cold hand. Jorath had found the subsidence and cleared it in under an hour — "The whole secondary line was pinched," he'd reported, dust on his knees and a particular satisfaction in his voice that meant the problem had been exactly the kind he loved. "Whoever built the drainage channel ran it straight across the branch path. When the roots pulled back, the channel collapsed into the gap. I've packed it and rerouted the drainage. Flow should reach the eastern fields now without fighting through mud."

It wouldn't hold forever. The downstream pull was still there, still drawing, and within a season the gradient would reassert itself and the branches would thin again. But a season was a season. A growing cycle. Food on tables. Something instead of nothing.

The satisfaction settled in him like a drink of warm water — real, unforced, the honest pleasure of having done the thing he was good at. And on its heels, the thought he always had after the work went well: *And then the next community. And the next. And each time the same problem in a different valley, and each time the same temporary fix, and each time the leaving.* He was too good for this — he knew it the way he knew the Amarvel's rhythm, automatically, without vanity — and not brave enough to reach for what came after. The ceiling was low and getting lower and he kept choosing to stay under it because the space above it was unmapped and frightening and he didn't know who he'd be up there.

He found Drenna at the eastern field edge, as he'd asked. She was crouched in the dirt, her fingers in the soil, her journal closed beside her. Not writing. Looking. She had the expression of someone reading a sentence she'd read before and finding a word she'd missed.

"The soil's warmer," she said when he reached her. "Since this morning. It wasn't warm yesterday."

"The branch work. I reinforced the western flow — Jorath cleared a blockage in the secondary line, so the eastern fields are getting some of it now. I'll work them directly this afternoon, but the boost should have raised the ground temperature across the whole settlement."

She stood. Brushed the dirt from her hands. "You did that. From the junction."

"That's the job."

"That's not a normal deployment outcome. I've been on three rotations. None of the practitioners I've worked with could affect the ground temperature from a trunk junction."

Vaelen didn't answer immediately. She was looking at him with the directness that was becoming familiar — the way she looked at everything, taking it in whole. It was uncomfortable, being looked at like that. Most people didn't look at him at all — they looked at the warmth, the grin, the part of him that arrived in the room first and did most of the talking. Drenna looked past that. She'd been looking past it since she'd said *you're the pattern reader* on the first day.

"The eastern fields," he said. "You told me the last specialist called the decline cyclical. Seasonal variance. But you said the girl stopped playing here because it felt wrong. I need to know: how long ago did the girl stop?"

"Seven weeks. Before the eastern fields died visibly. Before the yield data showed anything."

"Seven weeks." He knelt. Put his palms flat on the dead field soil. Even through the deep reading's aftereffects, he could feel it — the capillaries here were gone. Not retreated. Gone. The Amarvel had withdrawn from this section completely, the way a body abandons a frostbitten toe. The soil was alive in the ordinary way — bacteria, minerals, the chemistry of dirt — but the Amarvel's presence was zero. Not a hum. Not even silence. An absence.

"The girl felt it seven weeks ago," he said. "The instruments wouldn't have caught it for another three. The yield data another two after that. She was five weeks ahead of the instruments."

"Because the instruments measure the Amarvel. The girl was measuring something else."

Vaelen looked up at her. "What?"

"I don't know." She said it simply. Without frustration. A problem she hadn't solved yet but intended to. "The specialists measure the Amarvel — root density, flow rates, capillary reach. You measure the Amarvel, with more sensitivity than anyone I've met. But the girl wasn't measuring the Amarvel. She was standing barefoot in the dirt and she felt something change before the Amarvel's own readings showed any change. Whatever she felt, it's upstream of the Amarvel. It's the thing that makes the Amarvel retreat."

Vaelen sat back on his heels. The headache was returning. The metallic taste was strong. He'd been in the Amarvel's space too much in the last eighteen hours and his body was informing him, in its calm persistent way, that the conversation was becoming costly.

But Drenna was right. He could feel it — not with his training, not with the sensitivity that the Solvarei had tested and measured and called extraordinary. With something older. Something that wasn't in any curriculum because nobody had thought to teach it, because nobody had a name for the thing that a seven-year-old girl could feel through her bare feet and a trained pattern reader couldn't see in the Amarvel's own data.

The gap. The thing Drenna had been tracking across four communities. The disconnect between the network's self-reporting and the reality on the ground. It wasn't just a measurement problem. It was a category problem. The instruments — including him — were measuring the Amarvel. But the Amarvel wasn't the thing that was failing. Something was failing the Amarvel. Something upstream. Something that the roots carried but didn't originate, the way a river carries a poison that began miles from the water.

"I need to come back tonight," he said. "The deep read. I need to go further this time — past the local network, past the trunk line, down toward whatever's pulling from the south-southwest."

"You look like the last deep read nearly broke you."

"Nearly is the operative word."

"Vaelen." She said his name the way she said everything — directly, with a weight that stopped him from deflecting. "Whatever you're sensing at forty miles is also the thing a seven-year-old felt through her feet. She wasn't pushing further. She wasn't using any instruments at all. She was just standing in the dirt."

He stared at her. She was twenty-one years old. She had three months of Solvarei rotation under her belt and a field journal full of observations that no trained practitioner had thought to make. And she'd just shown him something about his own method that nobody had ever pointed out — not his instructors, not his assessors, not the Solvarei practitioners who'd worked alongside him for years. The way she said it was already reaching toward something further than the conversation, thinking at a scale he hadn't measured yet.

He wanted to argue. He wanted to explain that his method was rigorous, tested, the product of a decade of refinement. He wanted to say that distance *was* the way you found things in the Amarvel — you followed the roots, you traced the gradients, you pushed until the signal resolved.

Instead he thought about the girl in the field. Standing barefoot. Not reaching further. Not pushing deeper. Just... present. Open. Feeling something change before the Amarvel's own systems registered the change.

*Present. Not pushing. Not following gradients. Just... there.*

"I don't know how to do that," he said. It was, he realized, the most honest thing he'd said in months. Possibly years. The admission landed in the air between them and sat there, unadorned, the way Sorvyn's declarations sat — plain, without posture.

Drenna looked at him for a long moment. Then she knelt beside him and put her hands flat on the dead soil of the eastern fields. "Neither do I," she said. "So we figure it out."

---

He went back to the node at midnight.

The settlement was asleep. The wayhouse silent. He hadn't told Sorvyn — Sorvyn would have insisted on staying awake, watching, being present in case Vaelen came back from the deep read badly, and Vaelen didn't want to be watched. There was something about the deep read that was private — not because it was secret but because the person he became inside the Amarvel was not the person anyone knew. Not the warmth. Not the grin. Not the practitioner who made everyone feel steady. Inside the Amarvel he was small. Overwhelmed. A single nervous system trying to hold a conversation with something that spanned a continent. And that was the only version of himself he'd never learned to manage — the one that was simply, honestly, not enough.

He knelt at the node. Closed his eyes.

And didn't push.

Drenna's observation. The girl barefoot in the dirt, feeling something change that every instrument missed. Not pushing further. Not reaching for the source. Just present. Standing in it.

He didn't know what that meant for a pattern reader. His entire training was built on extending — following roots, tracing gradients, pushing until the signal resolved. But his method had given him distance and not clarity. He'd felt the pull at forty miles and it had been a blur. More distance would give him more blur.

So he stopped trying to travel. He stopped following the roots south-southwest. He stopped pushing.

He sat inside the node and listened.

The Amarvel's rhythm surrounded him. The familiar layers — trunk bass, branch midrange, capillary chatter. The tired pulse. The missed beats. The downstream pull, steady and patient and hungry. He let it all wash over him without reaching for any of it. Not reading. Not analyzing. Present.

Minutes passed. Maybe longer. The cold crept through his trousers and into his knees. His headache pulsed in time with the node. The metallic taste was back. He breathed. He waited.

And then he felt it.

Not in the Amarvel. Not through the roots. Not in any layer of the network he'd been trained to read. He felt it in the space between the layers — in the silence between the trunk's bass pulses, in the gaps between the branch traffic, in the pauses where the capillaries went quiet before cycling again.

The silence had a weight.

Not a signal. Not even an absence — he knew what absence felt like. The eastern fields were absent, and that was just nothing, the Amarvel gone and emptiness in its place. This was different. The Amarvel here was *present*. The pulse arrived. The flow continued. The gradients held. But between the pulses — in the space where the rhythm rested before the next beat — something had changed. The silence was thicker than it should have been. Heavier. Like the air in a house where someone has lived for fifty years and then quietly, without announcement, left. The furniture in place. The fire laid but unlit. The shape of a life with the living thing withdrawn.

He stayed with it. Let it wash through him without trying to name it. The pulses came — trunk, branch, capillary — and between each pulse, the silence pooled. Not empty silence. Weighted silence. He could feel it underneath the Amarvel's rhythm now, as if the network's vital signs were running on top of something that had thinned. The flow moved. The gradients held. But the thing that made the Amarvel *alive* rather than merely functional — the quality he'd never had to name because it had always been there, the way you never name the warmth of your own blood until it starts to cool — that quality was thinner here than it should have been. Thinner than he'd felt anywhere.

And thinning still.

His left hand closed into a fist. He couldn't stop it.

He didn't know what he was feeling. He had no name for it. His training had given him the tools to read the Amarvel's patterns — the flow, the gradients, the health of the network expressed in signal and silence. But the thing he was sensing wasn't a pattern. It was the space where a pattern should have been. A cast left behind after the living thing inside withdraws — a perfect mold, every detail preserved, every measurement intact. But the warmth going out of it. The life going out of it. Slowly. From inside.

He opened his eyes.

The moon had moved. He didn't know how long he'd been under. His hands were shaking — not the normal post-read tremor but something deeper, a vibration that lived in the bones rather than the muscles. His breath came in short pulls, too fast, and he made himself slow it by counting. Three in. Hold. Three out. The wayhouse. The settlement. The pale wood of the houses in the moonlight. Real things. Solid things.

A creature was sitting three paces from him. Small, furred — the same one he'd seen on the fence post that morning, the child's companion. It was facing him, its body low, its amber eyes steady. It shouldn't have been here. Settlement creatures stayed with their households at night. This one had crossed the open ground to the node and settled beside him in the dark, and it was watching him with an attention that didn't match its size.

He stood. His knees cracked. The creature didn't move. He walked past it toward the wayhouse and it stayed where it was, sitting in the shimmer of the node, watching him go.

He thought about Torven's grandmother. The quiet that lasted three years in one valley and came back on its own. A node that failed and recovered. An old story about an old wound.

This wasn't an old wound. This was something that didn't fit any story he knew, any pattern he'd been trained to read. The Amarvel was intact and the life inside it was thinning, and he didn't have a word for that, and neither did anyone he'd ever worked with or learned from. The grandmother's stories were maps drawn for a country that no longer existed.

He walked back to the wayhouse with his fists clenched and the taste of metal thick on his tongue. He didn't sleep. He sat on his bed in the dark and listened to his team breathe — Sorvyn steady, Jorath silent, Caeleth turning in dreams — and he held his left hand in his right and tried to stop the shaking and couldn't.

In the eastern fields, where the Amarvel had pulled back to nothing, the Mirathyn stalks stood in the moonlight. Upright. Leaves green. To anyone walking past in the dark, they would have looked healthy. They would have looked fine.

Almost exactly.
