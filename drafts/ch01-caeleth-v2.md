## The Ridge
*Caeleth*

The runner hit a root and the world went sideways.

Caeleth's shoulder struck the railing hard enough to send a bright shock up his neck and into his jaw, and for one terrible instant he was certain the whole carriage was going over. His field journal — three weeks of careful notes, a gift from his mother, the binding still stiff because he hadn't filled enough pages to break it in — slid off his knee and disappeared under the bench where Sorvyn was sleeping. Or pretending to sleep. The man had been motionless since the second hour, his hat tipped over a face that had more creases than a person should earn honestly, his broad shoulders settled into the bench like stone that had found its final resting place. He breathed the way old walls breathe — slowly, with the certainty of things that have stopped needing to prove they're still standing.

"Grab something," Jorath said.

He didn't look up. He never looked up. Jorath had his coins arranged in rows along his thigh — twelve silver, four copper, and a single tarnished half-mark stamped with the seal of a Hearthlands mint guild that Caeleth didn't recognize — and he was holding them steady against the carriage's shaking with three fingers, the way a musician holds strings. His other hand was making small notes in a ledger balanced on his knee, figures so small and precise that Caeleth couldn't read them from across the carriage. He was compact, Jorath. Sharp-jawed. Narrow in the shoulders and quick in the hands. Everything about him suggested a man who had learned early that the world would not give him anything he didn't count, record, and verify for himself.

"Hathren takes the western ridge at speed," Jorath said. "Every time. You'd think a man whose livelihood depends on delivering practitioners alive would care about the alive part."

Caeleth grabbed the railing. His knuckles were white. He noticed this and tried to relax them and couldn't. Through the slats he could see the ground passing beneath them at an angle that was definitely wrong, tilted thirty degrees from level, and the drop — the sheer open distance between the carriage floor and the valley below — pulled at something in his stomach that he'd been pretending wasn't there since the ridge began. He looked away from the gap. He always looked away. Heights had been his particular failure since childhood, the one fear he'd never grown out of the way the instructors promised. Korveth's speed only made it worse — the tilt of the carriage turning the landscape into a tilting bowl with them sliding along its rim.

Outside, the landscape blurred. Rolling green hills cut with dark stone outcroppings, stands of silver-barked trees whipping past close enough that their branches scraped the carriage slats with a sound like fingernails on wood. The air that pushed through the gaps was cold and tasted of crushed leaves and something else — something mineral and sharp that settled at the back of the throat like a word he'd forgotten.

Then the carriage leveled and the speed eased. The trees fell away into open meadow. Caeleth remembered to breathe.

He reached under the bench for his journal. Sorvyn's boot was on it. Not deliberately — the man's boots were simply everywhere, crossed at the ankle, heavy and indifferent. Caeleth tugged it free carefully, brushing dust from the cover. The leather was still stiff. He opened it to check for damage and the front page fell open to his mother's handwriting, the inscription she'd written the night before he left for his first deployment.

*For all the pages you haven't written yet. I'll want to read every one.*

He closed it. Put it in the inside pocket of his jacket, where the carriage couldn't reach it.

He was three weeks out of the academy. This was his second deployment. At the academy, Instructor Halveth had told them that the mark of a good practitioner was the ability to appear calm in conditions that warranted panic. Caeleth was failing this test comprehensively and could only hope that nobody was grading him.

"First time on Korveth?"

That was Vaelen.

He sat across the carriage with one arm slung over the side and his legs crossed at the ankle, occupying the space the way certain men occupy every space they enter — not with size, though he was tall enough to have to angle his shoulders through standard doorways, but with a quality of ease that made the shaking carriage seem like something that was happening to other people. He was older than Caeleth by perhaps a decade, dark-eyed, with a long face that was handsome the way certain landscapes are handsome — not pretty, not symmetrical, but arranged in a way that made you want to keep looking. There were lines at the corners of his mouth that deepened when he spoke and faint shadows under his eyes that might have been fatigue or might have been permanent. He had a piece of Ashara smokebread in his hand — dark crust, flecked with volcanic salt that glinted in the light — and he'd somehow kept hold of it through the lurch that had sent Caeleth's journal flying and Caeleth's dignity along with it.

His eyes were warm. That was the first thing you noticed about Vaelen, before the height, before the intelligence that moved behind the warmth like a current beneath still water. When he looked at you, his attention held — steady, unhurried — and whatever you'd braced yourself to flinch from didn't come.

Caeleth had been watching for mockery since he'd been assigned to this team. He was the youngest, the least experienced, the most obviously out of his depth. In every other group he'd been part of — at the academy, in his home settlement, in the brief miserable apprenticeship with the regional assessor who'd told him he lacked instinct — the dynamic had been the same. The established members closed ranks. The new one was tolerated until they proved themselves or didn't. Vaelen's team didn't work that way. The warmth wasn't a performance — Caeleth had watched for the seams and couldn't find them. Vaelen was simply like this: magnetic, generous, the kind of person who made you feel like the most interesting thing in the room without seeming to try. What puzzled Caeleth wasn't whether the warmth was genuine. It was why, underneath it, something in Vaelen seemed to hurt.

"Second time on a runner," Caeleth said, and was relieved that his voice came out steady. "But the first was Ylveth. She's... calmer."

"Ylveth is a hundred and forty years old and has the temperament of a grandmother who's seen everything and decided none of it warrants rushing," Vaelen said. "Korveth is nineteen and runs like he's being chased by something only he can see. Different experience entirely."

Jorath made a sound that might have been a laugh. "Korveth runs like that because Hathren lets him. And Hathren takes the ridge because he's taken the ridge for thirty years and refuses to consider alternatives. There's a valley crossing at Kelvreth that cuts a full hour — better footing, wider path, less wind shear on the carriage. I mapped it two deployments ago." He shook his head. "Faster route, lower creature strain, fewer passengers arriving with cracked ribs. But the man won't hear it."

"Jorath," Vaelen said.

"I'm just observing."

"You're solving a man who can't hear you while traveling at speed on a creature that could crush all of us by sitting down. Save it for the expense report."

Jorath held a coin up to the light, turning it between his fingers. "The expense report is going to be a work of art."

"At this rate," Caeleth said, "the expense report will cost more to file than the deployment."

He hadn't meant it as a joke. He'd been doing the arithmetic in his head — the filing fees, the auditor rates, the regional office processing charges that Instructor Halveth had made them memorize — and the numbers were absurd and he'd said so. But Jorath went very still. He looked at Caeleth with the expression of a man who has just heard his own religion spoken back to him in an unfamiliar dialect. Then his mouth did something it clearly wasn't accustomed to doing: it twitched upward. Not quite a smile. More like a crack in a wall that had always been solid.

"He's not wrong," Jorath said. He looked at Vaelen. "I want him on the audit."

Vaelen grinned.

Caeleth had seen the grin before — in the briefing room when a logistics officer had asked a stupid question, in the mess hall when Sorvyn had said something so dry it took three seconds to land, on the road when a child in a passing settlement had waved and Vaelen had waved back with a solemnity that made the child shriek with laughter. Each time it transformed him. The sharp angles of his face softened. The intelligence in his eyes became something warmer, something that invited you in — that made you feel like you and he shared a secret the rest of the room hadn't caught. The supply clerk at the regional depot had walked into a door frame after receiving it. Caeleth had pretended not to see. He understood the impulse.

"Jorath's skeleton has survived worse," Vaelen said. "Remember the Veldryn deployment? The ice shelf?"

"I try not to."

"He fell forty feet into a crevasse and his first words, his *very first words* at the bottom, were 'does this count as hazard pay.'"

Caeleth looked at the gap through the slats — the thirty-degree tilt he'd been trying not to think about — and felt his stomach contract.

Sorvyn lifted the hat from his face. His eyes were pale and steady, the color of winter sky, and they held the particular patience of a man who had heard every story worth hearing and most that weren't. "His first words were considerably worse than that. You're editing for the new recruit."

"I'm *curating*," Vaelen said.

Sorvyn looked at Caeleth. The look held no wink, no warmth deployed as a tool — just a plain, honest assessment, the way you'd look at a horse to see if it was sound. Whatever he saw, it apparently satisfied him.

"He's looking out for you," Sorvyn said. "He does that."

He put the hat back over his face. The conversation, as far as Sorvyn was concerned, was finished. But Caeleth had heard something in the old man's voice — a certainty so deep it didn't need emphasis. Not *I think he's looking out for you.* Not *he probably means well.* A statement of fact about the world, delivered with the same conviction another man might say *the sun rises in the east.* Sorvyn believed in Vaelen the way some people believed in the Amarvel — as a given, as a foundation, as something too fundamental to require justification.

Vaelen glanced at Sorvyn's hat. Just for a half-second. Something moved across his face — not discomfort, not gratitude, something faster and more exposed, like a flinch he hadn't been ready for. As if the weight of being believed in that completely was more than the weight of being doubted. Then the warmth came back, easy and natural, and the moment passed so quickly Caeleth almost doubted what he'd seen.

Almost.

"Sorvyn thinks I'm better than I am," Vaelen said. "It's his one consistent belief."

"Two consistent beliefs," Sorvyn said from under the hat. "That, and that Jorath will find a way to bill us for this conversation."

"Only if it gets interesting," Jorath said. "So far you're all predictable."

Caeleth laughed. It came out louder than he'd intended and he felt his neck flush hot, but nobody looked at him strangely, nobody paused, nobody exchanged the glance that said *the new one is trying too hard.* The joke simply absorbed him into its warmth, the way a campfire absorbs a person who sits down at its edge. He was included. The inclusion cost nothing and demanded nothing. That was the thing about Vaelen's team — three deployments together, and they had the rhythms of people who'd been sharing meals and silences and fear for years. Sorvyn's faith. Jorath's perpetual accounting. Vaelen's warmth holding it all in orbit. Even the frictions felt old and loved, like furniture worn smooth by use.

Caeleth wanted to belong here with a sharpness that sat in his chest like a stone. He'd felt it before — at the academy, in the first week, watching the older students move through the halls with the easy confidence of people who knew where they were going. He'd spent his whole life watching from the edge of circles he wanted to enter. This circle was warmer than most. That made the wanting worse.

Through the slats, the landscape was changing. The meadow gave way to cultivated land — Mirathyn fields, recognizable by the tall pale stalks and the faint iridescence that healthy Mirathyn gave off in afternoon light. Jorath pointed.

"That's heavy cultivation. Three, four cycles at least without fallow." He squinted. "And the drainage is wrong — the irrigation channels are running perpendicular to the slope. Every heavy rain pushes topsoil straight into the Amarvel capillaries." He pulled his ledger back open and made a note. "Fixable. Redirect the channels along the contour lines, shore up the downhill bank. Two days' work with a decent crew."

"The extract's regulated," Vaelen said mildly.

"The extract's *taxed*," Jorath said. "That's not the same thing. Every frontier settlement I've been to has someone brewing it stronger than the guidelines allow. It's good business. People want the warmth, the connection — and when the Amarvel's thinning and the bonds don't feel like they used to, they want it more." He shrugged. "Supply meeting demand. Hard to argue with the economics."

Sorvyn's hat came up. He didn't say anything. He looked at Jorath with an expression that could have been disagreement or could have been something older and more tired, and then he put the hat back down.

Caeleth filed the exchange away without fully understanding it. Mirathyn extract — he knew the basics from the academy. Low doses in festival food, a cultural tradition, mostly harmless. The higher concentrations were a different matter, but that was a frontier problem, and Caeleth hadn't spent enough time on the frontier to know what that meant in practice. He noticed that Vaelen hadn't pushed back on Jorath's economics. He'd offered the regulation like a stone skipping water — touching the surface, not sinking in.

---

The carriage began to slow, and the light changed.

Caeleth noticed the light before he noticed the landscape — a subtle shift in quality, as if someone had placed a thin cloth over the sun. The meadow they were passing through was green, undeniably green, but the green was somehow less than it should have been. He couldn't have explained it to anyone. The grass was the right color. The sky was clear. Nothing was visibly wrong. But the light that fell on the hills ahead had a flatness to it, a quality of arriving from very far away and being tired by the journey.

He wondered if he was imagining it. He said nothing.

Through the slats, the terrain was resolving into something inhabited. Terraced fields climbing low hillsides. Stone walls, old but maintained. Clusters of trees with canopies so dark they looked almost black against the afternoon sky. And structures — pale wood, darker stone, arranged in the organic pattern of a settlement that had grown around a center point rather than being planned. A Mirathyn farming community. Caeleth had grown up near one, though smaller than this, and the sight of the familiar architecture — the long communal tables visible through open doorways, the thread-drying racks along the southern walls — produced a pang of homesickness so specific it surprised him.

The center point was visible from here: an Amarvel node. Caeleth had learned to identify them at the academy — the faint shimmer in the air above the place where the roots concentrated, a distortion like heat rising from stone. The shimmer was supposed to pulse with a gold-green rhythm that matched the network's breath. This one pulsed. But the rhythm was irregular, the way a sleeping person's breath becomes irregular when their dreams turn bad.

Vaelen had stopped eating.

The smokebread was forgotten in his hand. His eyes had gone to a place Caeleth couldn't follow — distant, unfocused, his head tilting slightly to one side as if listening to a sound at the very edge of hearing. Caeleth had seen this twice before and it still made the hair on his forearms rise. When Vaelen read the Amarvel, his face emptied. The warmth, the intelligence, the easy magnetism that made rooms rearrange themselves around him — all of it went somewhere else, and what was left was something stripped to function, a precision instrument that happened to be wearing a person's face. It lasted three seconds, perhaps four. Then Vaelen blinked and was Vaelen again, the warmth returning like a light coming back on in a room, and the transition was so seamless that Caeleth almost doubted what he'd seen.

Almost.

Vaelen looked at the smokebread in his hand with mild surprise and ate the rest of it in two bites. He brushed the dark crumbs from his chest. "The node's stressed but functional," he said. "Soil depletion pattern — probably eighteen months of gradual decline. The Mirathyn crops are pulling more than the Amarvel can replenish."

He said it the way someone would say *it's going to rain later.* Caeleth was still trying to process the fact that Vaelen had just extracted eighteen months of agricultural and network data from a three-second stare at a shimmer in the air two hundred meters away.

"How bad?" Jorath's coins were put away. His ledger was closed. The shift was instant — a different man, alert and professional, the accounting forgotten.

"Not emergency. Not comfortable either. The community will have noticed it in the yields. Smaller harvests. Lower Mirathyn potency. Bond creatures a little more sluggish than usual. They'll have attributed it to weather, or season, or bad luck. People always do."

"And the real cause?"

Vaelen paused. Caeleth was learning to read Vaelen's pauses. The short pause meant he was organizing information. The medium pause meant he was deciding how much to share. This was a medium pause.

"The node's compensating for something downstream," Vaelen said. "Drawing resources from the local Amarvel to feed a deficit further along the line. Like a body pulling blood from the extremities to protect the core."

Sorvyn's hat was off. "What's downstream?"

"I don't know yet."

There was a silence after that which Caeleth didn't like. Vaelen always had the next sentence ready. The absence of one felt like a gap in a wall where a stone should be.

The carriage shuddered and dipped, tilting forward into descent, and Caeleth's hands found the railing before he knew they'd moved. The ground dropped away through the slats — far, too far, the settlement still a distant arrangement of pale roofs and dark trees — and his throat closed in the old familiar way. A sharp whistle from above — Hathren, calling out a command in a low guttural tone that vibrated strangely in the air, more felt than heard. Caeleth felt it in his teeth. The creature beneath them groaned in answer, a sound so deep it traveled up through the carriage floor and into Caeleth's spine, and the descent smoothed from terrifying to merely unpleasant. The ground rushed up. There was a final jolt that knocked Jorath's ledger from his hands — "If those calculations are smudged I'm doing the whole ridge again," Jorath said to nobody — and then they were still.

Vaelen swung over the side and dropped to the ground with the ease of a man stepping off a porch. Caeleth looked over the railing. The drop was six feet, maybe seven — nothing, a simple step down for anyone who wasn't built the way Caeleth was built. He sat on the railing and lowered himself until his boots found the ground, which was slower and less dignified but meant he didn't have to fall. His legs were shaking in a way he hoped nobody noticed.

Behind them, Hathren was already descending from the driving perch — a wiry, sun-darkened man with a face like dried fruit and the permanent squint of someone who'd spent decades looking into wind. He moved down the creature's neck with a familiarity that bordered on contempt, as if the massive animal beneath him were a particularly stubborn piece of furniture.

And now Caeleth saw Korveth properly, from the ground, at rest, and the scale of the thing stopped his breath.

The creature was broader than a house. Low-slung, with six legs folded beneath a body plated in something that was neither stone nor bark but shifted between the two depending on how the light struck it — gray-brown, mottled, alive in a way that mineral shouldn't be. Its head was tucked against its chest, each exhalation a slow mist that flattened the grass in a circle wide enough to build a dwelling in. Along its spine, the plating had been worn smooth by years of harness — pale grooves where leather had rubbed the stone-bark surface into something almost soft.

Hathren put his hand on the creature's jaw without looking. Korveth's eye — one enormous amber disc, flecked with green, more intelligent than any animal eye had a right to be — tracked him with a patience that made Caeleth think of old married couples.

Korveth exhaled — a slow, vast sigh that settled through the creature's body like something finally put down.

Hathren began unfastening the carriage bolts without a word. Vaelen said, "Thank you, Hathren," and Hathren responded with a grunt that managed to acknowledge the gratitude, dismiss it, and communicate that further conversation was unwelcome, all in a single exhalation. He had the carriage unbolted and stowed within minutes, and then he climbed Korveth's flank, settled into the driving perch, and whistled. The creature unfolded its six legs — an operation that seemed to involve the rearrangement of the earth itself — and rose, and turned, and was moving before Caeleth had fully processed that the ground was shaking again. Within a minute, Korveth and Hathren were a shape diminishing against the green hills, and the settlement was quiet in the way places are quiet when something very large has just left them.

---

She was already walking toward them when Caeleth turned around.

His first thought was that she was young — younger than him, which startled him, because he was accustomed to being the youngest person in every professional context he entered. She was perhaps twenty, twenty-one at most, with long dark hair pulled back in a way that was practical without being severe, loose strands falling around a face that was — Caeleth noticed and immediately felt foolish for noticing — striking. Wide brown eyes set above sharp cheekbones. A mouth that looked like it was perpetually considering whether to say something cutting. She was small — not short, but built with a compactness that made her movements seem precise, deliberate, as if she'd never in her life taken a step she hadn't thought about first.

She wore Solvarei field markings. New ones — the fabric still stiff at the shoulders, not yet shaped to her body, the insignia too bright. A rotation practitioner, fresh from her Solcarin assignment. But below the new markings, at her wrist, Caeleth caught a glimpse of threadwork — a thin band woven in colors he recognized. Frontier work. The kind of threadwork you saw in settlements on the Amarvel's edge, where the dyes came from local plants and the patterns were passed down through families. She'd come from out here. Or somewhere very like it.

But she didn't walk like someone coming home. She walked like someone returning to a place she'd already left — sure of the ground beneath her, knowing every path, and choosing to cross it with the stride of a person who'd learned to move through very different rooms. The threadwork said frontier. Her posture, her precision, the way her gaze inventoried everything it crossed without lingering — that said somewhere else entirely. Somewhere that had taught her to carry herself like a weapon that looked like a tool.

Her boots were already caked with the red-brown soil of the Mirathyn fields. She'd been here since the night before, Caeleth would learn later, and had walked the community's fields that morning before dawn. She carried a field journal under her arm that was thick with loose pages and handwritten annotations — the journal of someone who recorded everything, who trusted paper more than memory and memory more than anything else.

She stopped a few paces from the team. Her eyes moved across them with a frankness that reminded Caeleth of Instructor Halveth conducting assessments — thorough, unhurried, uninterested in making anyone comfortable. She took in Jorath pulling equipment from the storage compartment, Sorvyn stretching his back with the slow care of a man managing old complaints, and Caeleth standing beside a kit he wasn't sure he was supposed to carry. She catalogued them, filed them, and turned to Vaelen.

"You're the pattern reader," she said.

It wasn't a question. She'd done her preparation. She knew who was coming and what they could do. Caeleth watched Vaelen receive the directness, and for the first time since joining the team, he saw something he didn't expect: a beat of hesitation. Not long — a fraction of a second, less than a breath — where the easy warmth paused, as if whatever lived beneath it had been startled into showing its face. Attention, maybe. Recognition. The look of a man who'd expected a junior practitioner and found something that didn't fit the category.

Then the warmth came, and if it arrived a half-beat late, Caeleth was probably the only one who noticed. Probably.

"I am," Vaelen said, and extended his hand. "Vaelen."

She shook it. Firm grip, brief contact. "Drenna."

"Drenna." He held the name for a moment, the way he held data — feeling its weight. Then he turned to the team and the warmth broadened, became the inclusive thing it always was. "Let me introduce everyone. Sorvyn — he's been doing this longer than any of us and knows more than he'll ever volunteer. He'll tell you the most important thing he does is nap, and he's wrong about that but right about most other things. Jorath handles logistics, equipment, and finance. If you need something, ask him. If you need something that costs money, ask him twice. And Caeleth is our newest — two weeks ahead of you on the experience of not knowing where anything is."

The introductions were easy and generous. They gave Drenna a shape of the team without demanding anything in return. Caeleth noticed that Vaelen had described each of them by what they contributed, not by their rank — a kindness so precise it seemed to arrive without effort, the way light arrives through a window. He couldn't tell if Vaelen knew he was doing it. He suspected Vaelen couldn't tell either.

"I'm your rotation practitioner for this deployment," Drenna said. She nodded at each of them — Sorvyn, Jorath, Caeleth — with a precision that suggested she'd remember their names, faces, and Vaelen's descriptions of them long after this conversation ended. "I arrived last night. The rotation is a short one — I'm assigned to the regional assessment body after this."

She said it without emphasis, a scheduling fact. But the way she said *after this* — already past it, already somewhere else in her mind — made Caeleth think she was not a woman who planned to settle into any team for long.

"You're from this region," Vaelen said.

Again, not a question. He'd read it somewhere — in the threadwork at her wrist, in the soil on her boots, in the way she'd walked toward them from the settlement's interior rather than waiting at its edge. She'd walked the way someone walks through a place they know — not checking the path, not looking for landmarks. Moving through memory.

Something shifted in Drenna's expression. A flicker of — not surprise. Reassessment. She hadn't expected him to see that so quickly. And in the half-second that followed, Caeleth caught something else in her face — a calculation so fast it was barely visible, the way her gaze sharpened on Vaelen not just as a practitioner but as something she was measuring against a scale Caeleth couldn't see. She saw his gift. And she was already turning it over in her mind, fitting it into some larger frame that had nothing to do with this deployment.

"I grew up forty minutes east," she said. "I know this community. I know the families. I know the node. I walked the eastern fields this morning." She opened her journal to a tabbed page. "The eastern Mirathyn fields stopped producing six weeks ago. Three families have relocated to the inner Hearthlands. The node specialist who came through last season called it cyclical." She looked at him directly. "It's not cyclical."

She said this without anger, without drama. The way you report damage after a storm. *Here is what is broken. Here is what I know. Are you going to do something about it.*

Vaelen took the journal. He studied her measurements — not glancing, not skimming. Reading, the way he read the Amarvel. His eyes moved across her notations and the focus was absolute, and Caeleth saw something happen that he didn't fully understand: Vaelen's body changed. The easy posture, the warmth, the looseness that said *nothing here is urgent* — all of it went away, replaced by something straighter and colder. Not hostile. Functional. A man shelving everything that wasn't necessary.

"Your observations are better than what we'd have gotten from the regional office," he said. Simply. Without flattery.

"I know," Drenna said. Without vanity. A fact measured and recorded, like the node's decline curve.

"What else?"

"Something's wrong with the water. It's not contaminated — I've tested it. But it tastes like nothing. Started four weeks ago." She paused. "People are used to it already. That's the part that concerns me."

Caeleth didn't understand why that detail — *people are used to it* — made Vaelen's left hand close into a fist. He saw it happen. The fingers curling slowly, one at a time, until the knuckles were white. The rest of Vaelen's body remained perfectly composed — the face attentive, the voice steady, the posture professional. But the hand had made its own decision.

"Right," Vaelen said, and his voice had shifted register. Lower, quieter, stripped of the warmth that made people feel at ease. This was the voice that gave orders. "Here's what we'll do. Sorvyn — perimeter walk, full settlement, not just the node radius. I want you talking to the elders. Listen more than you ask. Jorath — field station at the node, standard readings. I want numbers on paper before I go in for a direct read." He looked at Caeleth. "You're with Jorath. Watch everything. Write everything down."

Then he looked at Drenna.

"You're with me. The community elder will want the official meeting — the speech, the reassurances. We'll give him that. He's earned it. He's the elder of a place that's frightened and hasn't been listened to, and we're going to listen before we do anything else." He handed the journal back to her. "After that, you take me to the eastern fields. And you tell me everything you know about this place that isn't in any report. The things you know because you grew up here."

Drenna looked at him. The assessment was visible — the calculations adjusting behind her eyes. She'd arrived with an argument prepared, built from data and frustration and the specific fury of someone who'd watched her home region be mishandled by outsiders. Vaelen had taken the argument out of her hands by agreeing with it before she could make it — and then he'd added something she hadn't accounted for: respect for the elder. The instinct to honor the community's own authority before asserting his.

She'd expected competence. She hadn't expected that.

"Twenty minutes," she said.

"Twenty minutes," Vaelen agreed.

She turned toward the settlement. Vaelen fell into step beside her — not behind, not ahead, beside — and they walked together toward the elder's house, and within ten paces they were talking. Caeleth watched them go: the tall man with the easy stride and the young woman with the precise one, her journal between them, her hands moving when words weren't enough, his head inclined toward her in the posture of someone who was listening with everything he had.

Jorath appeared at Caeleth's shoulder. "Secondary kit," he said. "On the carriage. Move."

---

Caeleth hefted the kit — heavier than expected — and followed Jorath toward the node. They walked through the settlement's main path, past houses with open doorways and the sound of someone grinding grain, past a communal well where a woman was drawing water without looking at what she was doing.

A child waved at him from a stone wall.

She was perhaps seven or eight, sitting with her legs swinging, bare feet dusty. She had the round face and dark eyes common to frontier children, and she waved the way children wave — with her whole arm, her whole body, an act of complete and unselfconscious greeting. Caeleth stopped. He set down the kit.

"Hello," he said.

The girl looked at him. She didn't say anything. But she was looking, which seemed like something.

"I'm Caeleth. We're the deployment team." He fished in his jacket and found the piece of smokebread Vaelen had offered him during the ride — he'd been too nervous to eat it. He held it out. "Do you like volcanic salt? It's from Ashara. It makes your tongue go numb if you eat too much."

She took it. She turned it over in her small fingers, studying the dark flecks. She bit into it. She smiled.

It was a normal smile. He would have said so if anyone had asked. The muscles of her face did what muscles do. Her mouth curved. Her cheeks rose. But something in the smile stopped at the surface, like a stone skipped across water that never sinks. It reached her mouth and stayed there. Her eyes participated the way a person participates in a ceremony they've attended too many times — correctly, completely, without the thing that makes correctness matter.

"Caeleth." Jorath's voice, already twenty paces ahead. Impatient. "The kit doesn't carry itself."

He picked up the kit. He looked back once. She was eating the smokebread with the careful attention of a child who has learned that good things should be made to last. She wasn't watching him anymore. She was looking at the ground between her feet with an expression that had no name Caeleth knew, because the expression was the absence of expression — a face at rest in a way that a child's face should not be at rest.

He thought about it for the next forty paces. He tried to stop thinking about it. He couldn't articulate what was wrong. Perhaps nothing was wrong. Children grew quiet sometimes. It didn't mean anything.

The flowers in the window boxes were blooming.

He noticed them the way you notice a picture hanging slightly crooked on a wall — not with alarm, with the faint persistent itch of something not quite aligned. Every house had them. Well-tended. Colorful. Reds, yellows, the deep blue-purple of late-season selvareth. But the colors didn't do what colors were supposed to do. They didn't catch his eye. They didn't pull. It was as if someone had mixed the pigments from memory instead of from life, and the memory was ninety-five percent accurate, and the missing five percent was the difference between a garden you wanted to stop and look at and a garden you walked past without slowing down.

Caeleth didn't slow down. Neither did Jorath. Neither, he realized, did anyone. The settlement's inhabitants moved past the window boxes without glancing at them. The flowers had become invisible. Not because they were gone, but because the quality that made flowers worth noticing had left so gradually that nobody had registered the departure.

He wanted to say something. He didn't know what he would say. *The flowers seem odd* was not the kind of observation that earned respect from a team like Vaelen's. He said nothing, and followed Jorath to the node, and the itch in the back of his mind settled into a low hum that would not, for the rest of the day, go quiet.

---

That evening they ate at the elder's table.

The settlement's wayhouse was the nicest building on the main path — pale wood with a slate roof, a proper hearth, and beds with actual frames rather than pallets. It served traders and travelers when they passed through, which wasn't often enough to keep the dust off the windowsills. Torven had aired it out for them with the careful attention of a man who wanted these visitors — rare, important visitors — to see his community at its best. But meals were communal in frontier settlements, and he had insisted. He was a heavy man, white-haired, with the broad hands of someone who'd farmed Mirathyn his entire life, and he served them with the careful pride of a person who wanted the guests to understand that this community still knew how to be generous, even now, even with things the way they were.

The stew was root vegetables and smoked grain, hot and filling. It tasted, to Caeleth, like it should have tasted like more than it did.

Torven's daughter was arguing with Jorath about the drainage problem he'd spotted from the carriage — the irrigation channels running against the slope. He'd sketched a fix on the back of a napkin within minutes of sitting down, and she was telling him it wouldn't work in the wet season, and Jorath was explaining why she was wrong with the focused relish of a man who could not leave a problem unsolved, even someone else's — when Torven himself leaned back and said, to no one in particular, "My grandmother used to say the water goes before everything else. She said it happened when she was a girl — the node in their valley went quiet and the first thing was the water. Then the colors. Then the bonds." He looked at his hands. "She said the quiet came and stayed for three years before the Amarvel recovered. But that was a small thing. One valley. One node. This feels..." He didn't finish the sentence.

Vaelen was listening. His cup sat untouched, and his attention was on Torven with the same absolute focus Caeleth had seen him give the Amarvel. "Did your grandmother say what brought the recovery? What made the node come back?"

Torven shook his head slowly. "She said they waited. She said the old women sang at the node every morning and the men worked the fields even when nothing grew, and after three years the shimmer came back and the water had taste again. She said you couldn't make it happen. You could only keep living until it did." He looked at Vaelen. "But she also said that was one valley. She never heard of it happening to more than one place at once."

A silence settled over the table.

Caeleth drank his water. Tasted nothing. Set the cup down.

"My mother always said," he offered, and then stopped, because he hadn't planned to speak and the eyes of the table were on him. He felt his neck flush. But the sentence was already started and there was nothing to do but finish it. "She said when the water loses its taste, add salt. And when the salt loses its taste, pack a bag." He paused. "I think she meant it as advice. But she'd also never left our settlement, so I'm not sure where she expected anyone to go."

Torven looked at him. For a moment Caeleth thought he'd said something wrong — something flippant about a man's dying community. But Torven's face cracked into the first real smile Caeleth had seen in the settlement. Deep, lines spreading into his weathered cheeks, reaching his eyes. "Your mother sounds like my grandmother," he said. "Practical women. They always knew before the rest of us."

"Smart boy," Jorath muttered, without looking up from the drainage sketch. From Jorath, this was an embrace.

Drenna's hands had been still on the table since the exchange started. She sat across from Vaelen, her boots caked to the ankle, a smear of red soil on her cheek that she either hadn't noticed or didn't care about. Now she was looking at Caeleth with an expression he couldn't read — something between warmth and pain, there and gone so quickly he wasn't sure he'd seen it.

The conversation drifted to the Mirathyn harvest, and Torven was explaining the community's process — the careful tending, the seasonal rhythms, the pride his family took in producing the lightest batch in the region — when Sorvyn spoke up from behind his stew.

"Had a handler on the northern circuit who brewed his own concentrate," he said. Not loudly — Sorvyn never said anything loudly. "Kept a flask in his coat. Said it took the edge off the altitude." He spooned his stew without hurrying. "Don't know about the altitude. But he was the happiest man I ever saw on a mountain."

A few people at the table smiled. Torven chuckled. It was the kind of story people told about extract when it hadn't touched their lives — a mild anecdote, a character sketch, someone else's harmless excess.

Drenna's jaw tightened. A small movement — the muscles along her jawline drawing taut, her fingers pressing flat against the table. It lasted a moment. Then she picked up her cup and drank, and her face was neutral, and Caeleth didn't know why he felt, suddenly and without reason, that something at the table had shifted.

Vaelen and Drenna had been in the eastern fields for three hours before the meal. Drenna was explaining something to him now — gesturing with the intensity of someone who'd been holding this information alone for too long — and Vaelen was listening with the absolute focus that Caeleth was beginning to understand was his rarest and most genuine mode. Not the warmth. Not the grin. This. The listening. The way he turned his whole body toward the person speaking and made the rest of the world go quiet.

Sorvyn sat beside Caeleth, eating slowly. He'd completed his perimeter walk — hands clasped behind his back, steady pace, pausing at a window box to look at the flowers and then looking away. He'd spoken with the elder and three other families. He hadn't shared his findings with anyone except Vaelen, and that conversation had lasted two minutes — a look, a few murmured words, a nod. Whatever Sorvyn had found, Vaelen now carried it, and Sorvyn was content to let him carry it. The trust was so complete it felt like gravity.

"He's worried," Sorvyn said quietly, not looking up from his stew.

"How can you tell?"

"The hand."

Caeleth looked. Vaelen's left hand rested on the table beside his untouched water cup. It was closed. A fist. Tight. Still. The rest of him was animated — nodding, asking questions, his voice measured and professional. But the hand had its own opinion, and the opinion was fear.

"What does it mean?" Caeleth asked.

"It means he's sensed something he doesn't have a name for yet," Sorvyn said. He spooned his stew without hurry. "But he will. He always figures it out."

Caeleth wanted to believe that. Sorvyn clearly did — his faith was a living thing, steady and warm and absolute. But Caeleth looked at Vaelen's fist, and then he looked at the stew in his bowl that should have tasted like something, and then he looked at the window box behind Vaelen's head where the flowers were blooming in colors that were almost right.

Almost.

---

He went to bed in the wayhouse thinking about the water. About Torven's grandmother, who'd watched a valley go quiet and waited three years for it to come back. About how she'd said it was one valley, one node, and how Torven had looked at his hands when he said *this feels different.* About how quickly the people here had stopped noticing the water tasted like nothing. About what else might be fading that nobody had noticed yet.

He opened his journal. The pages were mostly empty — three weeks of careful notes that didn't amount to much. Observations from his first deployment, measurements he wasn't confident about, a sketch of a capillary junction that Instructor Halveth would have called adequate and nothing more. His mother's inscription faced him from the front page. *For all the pages you haven't written yet.*

He turned to a blank page and wrote: *The flowers are wrong. I don't know how.*

He closed the journal and put it beside his pillow and slept badly.

Sometime deep in the night — he couldn't have said the hour — he woke needing to relieve himself and stepped outside the wayhouse into air that was colder than it should have been for the season. The settlement was quiet. Moonlight turned the pale wood of the houses silver-blue. Somewhere distantly, a bond creature made a low sound — not distressed, but watchful. The kind of sound an animal makes when it senses something its body knows about before its mind does.

He saw Drenna.

She was standing at the eastern edge of the settlement, where the last houses gave way to the Mirathyn fields and the fields gave way to open land. Her back was to him. She was very still. Her arms were at her sides, her hair loose now, black against the silver-blue of the moonlit air. She was looking east, toward where she'd grown up, toward the forty minutes of road between this community and whatever was left of hers.

Caeleth almost called out. He didn't. Something in her stillness stopped him — a rigidity in her shoulders, a tension in the way she held her head. This was not a woman enjoying the night air. This was not homesickness, or nostalgia, or the quiet contemplation of a young practitioner on her first rotation.

She was afraid. Of what, Caeleth could not have said.

She stood there for a long time, looking east into the dark. The moon moved. The bond creature called again, farther away now. The cold deepened.

Caeleth went back inside. He did not sleep again that night.
