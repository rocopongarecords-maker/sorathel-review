# Episode Two — Chapter One
## Vaelen

*Version 2 — Revised Draft*
*Created: 2026-03-08*

---

The Solvarei compound smelled like wet stone and breakfast smoke.

Vaelen crossed the central yard with his collar turned up against the morning and a cup of Sorvyn's coffee in his hand — the real kind, the kind Sorvyn ground himself in a clay mortar he'd been carrying for six years and refused to let anyone else touch. It was too strong. It was always too strong. Vaelen drank it because Sorvyn made it, and Sorvyn made it because they were home, and being home meant the mortar came out and the coffee was ground by hand and the cup was offered without asking.

Home. The compound didn't look like much from the outside — a cluster of low stone buildings inside a perimeter wall, set into a hillside above the city of Darachet. Functional. The wall was thick enough to tell you something about the age of the organization and modest enough to tell you something about its budget. Inside, it was something else. Training yards where practitioners ran field exercises against wooden frames that simulated root junctions. Workshops where the instruments were cleaned and recalibrated — Vaelen could hear Jorath in there now, arguing with a quartermaster about replacement casing for a resonance probe. A mess hall that served food only slightly better than wayhouse stew, which was why Sorvyn cooked in his quarters. Living quarters — small rooms with thick walls and narrow windows, allocated by seniority and not much else. A library nobody used. A courtyard garden somebody maintained without credit.

And practitioners. Moving through all of it, at different speeds, in different configurations — the senior teams heading to or from deployments, equipment loaded, boots heavy with frontier mud. Junior practitioners between training sessions, too loud, too eager, their field markings still bright. The support staff who made everything work and whom most practitioners forgot to see.

Vaelen saw them. He saw the woman who managed the instrument shop pausing at the courtyard gate with her hands behind her back, watching something in the garden. He saw the kitchen assistant carrying two baskets of grain across the yard, angling around the training group with the practiced dodge of someone who'd been doing it for years. He saw a handler leading a dispatch creature — a small flighted messenger with dark plumage and quick, anxious movements — toward the perimeter roost where the outbound signals were staged. The handler held the bird against her chest with both hands, and it pressed its head into the hollow of her throat, and she murmured something to it that Vaelen couldn't hear but could read in the angle of her mouth — reassurance, or apology, or the particular private language that handlers and their creatures built between themselves. The bird's wings settled. She walked on.

"Vaelen."

Theryn was crossing the yard from the direction of the equipment shed — a second-year team lead, broad face, the kind of man who said your name before you'd finished deciding whether to acknowledge him.

"Commander Essren wants you. Her office, when you're done with —" He looked at the coffee. "That."

"That bad?"

"She didn't say bad. She said *when you're available*, which from Essren means now."

Vaelen raised his cup. "Tell her I'm walking slowly."

Theryn almost smiled. "I'll tell her you're on your way." He turned, then turned back. "The anomaly attachment in your Torven report — Hathral's team got one with similar readings from the Delverath corridor. She's been in there since dawn cross-referencing. Just so you know."

He left. Vaelen stood in the yard with his too-strong coffee. The anomaly attachment. The raw data he'd included because protocol required pattern-reading appendices on every deployment report, and because he was thorough, and because the thoroughness had a second function: it put the truth on record without requiring him to say it out loud. The summary had said *within acceptable parameters.* The attachment had said something else entirely.

He'd expected the attachment to be filed and forgotten. Most people didn't read the appendices. Essren read the appendices.

He drank his coffee and walked toward her office.

---

The mess hall was loud at midmorning. Vaelen passed through it on his way to Essren's wing and the room adjusted around him — people orienting slightly, making eye contact, finding reasons to intersect his path. A junior practitioner looked up from her bowl and straightened. An older field technician raised a hand. He nodded back. Smiled.

He saw Caeleth.

The boy was sitting at a table near the wall, alone. Not the dramatic loneliness of someone who'd been excluded — the quieter kind. The kind where you'd found a seat and nobody had told you to move but nobody had sat down either, and the meal in front of you was getting cold while you waited for a pause in someone else's conversation that would be wide enough to step through.

Vaelen recognized it. Not from observation — from memory.

He could have sat down. Caeleth would have looked up with that expression — the one that tried to be casual and wasn't — and Vaelen would have said something warm and easy and the wanting would have been soothed.

He walked past.

His left hand closed. He opened it.

At the far end of the mess, an old woman was wiping down the serving counter with a rag so threadbare it was mostly memory. She'd been there when Vaelen was a junior — same counter, same rag, same expression that suggested she'd seen every practitioner who'd ever lived rotate through this hall and found them all equally in need of feeding. He'd never learned her name. He'd been meaning to for twelve years. Every time he passed, the intention renewed itself and then slipped away, the way small intentions do when they compete with larger ones.

She caught his eye now and jerked her chin toward Caeleth's table. Then toward the empty seat across from the boy. Then back at Vaelen. The message was clear and had nothing warm about it.

Vaelen kept walking.

---

Essren's office was small and full of paper.

Not the way a disorganized person's office is full of paper — the way a person who reads everything is full of paper. Reports stacked by region. Maps pinned to the wall with thread connecting locations, the thread color-coded in a system Vaelen didn't recognize. A desk that had been cleared at some point and then reoccupied by new documents with the inevitability of a tide returning. Essren sat behind it with her reading spectacles pushed up on her forehead and a cup of something that had stopped being warm a long time ago.

She was older than Vaelen by fifteen years, maybe more. Her face was angular, lined, settled into an expression of permanent consideration. She didn't perform authority. She occupied it.

"Sit down."

He sat. The chair was hard. Everything in Essren's office was hard — the chair, the light, the questions.

She pulled a document from a stack. He recognized his own handwriting on the cover sheet. The Torven deployment report. Summary and recommendations on the first two pages. Pattern-reading appendix — eighteen pages of raw readings, network gradients, flow maps, anomaly notations — stapled behind it.

"Your summary says the node is stabilized. Within acceptable parameters. Branch work successful, capillary recovery projected through one growing cycle. Standard language. Competent." She turned to the appendix. "Your readings say something else."

He waited.

"A downstream pull of increasing intensity, south-southwest, active across the full monitoring period. Silence density in the trunk-level pauses exceeding anything in our records — and I checked the records. A capillary recovery that's being siphoned by the same pull your branch work was designed to counteract." She looked at him over the spectacles she'd forgotten were on her forehead. "And a secondary oscillation in the silence layer that you've noted as *unclassified, anomalous, not matching any standard frequency.*"

"That's what I recorded."

"That's what you recorded in an appendix that most field coordinators don't read."

"Protocol requires pattern-reading attachments on every deployment report."

"Protocol requires them. You wrote eighteen pages. The standard attachment is three." She set the report down. "Vaelen. The summary and the appendix are telling two different stories. Which one is true?"

His left hand rested on his thigh. It didn't close. He kept it open.

"Both," he said. "The node is stabilized. The branch work held. The community will have a growing season. All of that is accurate."

"And?"

"And the downstream pull is active and getting stronger, and the silence in the trunk-level pauses is unlike anything I've read in twelve years of practice, and the branch work may have accelerated it. The fix may be feeding the problem."

She let that sit.

"Hathral's team filed from the Delverath corridor three weeks ago," she said. "Standard summary. Acceptable-parameters language. But I read their appendices too, because I read everyone's appendices, and their pattern-reader flagged a trunk-level anomaly with similar characteristics to yours. Not identical — the readings are different in scale and frequency. But the same quality. Something in the pauses. Something that isn't supposed to be there."

Vaelen's breathing didn't change. He was aware of it not changing, which was its own kind of tell.

"Two independent readings," Essren said. "Different regions. Same phenomenon. Hathral's reader doesn't have your gift — she's good, methodical, but she's working with standard instruments and standard training. She flagged it as unusual and moved on. You flagged it as eighteen pages of data that you buried in an appendix."

"I didn't bury it."

"You wrote it where nobody would look."

He didn't argue.

Essren stood. She walked to the wall map — the one with the thread connecting field reports. Red thread for anomalies. Green for standard readings. Blue for Amarith communications. The red threads were clustered on the frontier, and there were more of them than Vaelen expected.

"The frontier reports have been changing for eight months," she said. "Gradually. The way water rises — you don't notice until your feet are wet. Node stress, recovery rates declining, farming yields down in five districts, transport creature reluctance on three major routes. Individual events, individual explanations." She turned from the map. "Your readings are the first field data that suggests it might not be individual. That the events might be connected. That there might be a pattern."

"I don't know what the pattern is."

"No. But you sense that there is one. And your readings are unlike anything in our records." She paused. The lines around her eyes deepened. "Whatever you're sensing, Vaelen, it's real. I've cross-referenced enough data to be certain. I trust your perception."

She held his eyes.

"I need you to trust it too."

He nodded. Not the warm nod. A different one. Smaller.

"The Amarith's assessment directorate has been briefed on the frontier reports," Essren said. She said it carefully, the way you mention something that has weight. "The standard channel — field summaries, regional analysis, trend indicators. They haven't deployed teams yet. But if the pattern holds — if the next quarter's data confirms what yours and Hathral's suggest — they'll want firsthand readings." She looked at him. "They'll want yours."

He'd seen their work — through reports, through the rare occasions when an Amarith team passed through a deployment zone and the Solvarei practitioners stood straighter and spoke more carefully. They operated at trunk level. They redirected flows that Vaelen could barely track.

He'd watched one of their assessment teams once, at a regional junction two years ago. Three practitioners — quiet, precise, moving through the network with a fluency that made his own gift feel like a dialect of their language. They'd corrected a trunk-level misalignment in an afternoon that would have taken a Solvarei team a week. Afterward, they'd filed their report and left without speaking to the local practitioners. Not cruel. Just uninterested. The work was the work, and the locals were the locals, and the gap between the two wasn't worth bridging.

He'd stood at the edge of their perimeter and watched them go and thought: *you could be so much more than this.*

"Velanthra," Essren said. She was moving on. "Your next deployment. Network connection interrupted in the commercial district — trade infrastructure, creature dispatch routes, the usual urban systems. The assignment came through from the regional coordinator two days ago. Your team is cleared and equipped."

"We'll be ready by end of day."

"Vaelen." She stopped him at the door. "The next report. Write the summary like you write the appendix."

He turned back. She was watching him over those spectacles.

"I'll think about it," he said.

"That's what worries me."

---

Sorvyn's quarters smelled like rosemary and rendered fat and something Vaelen couldn't identify that turned out to be a tuber he'd found at the Darachet market that morning and was roasting whole in a clay pot he'd seasoned for three years.

"Don't touch the pot."

"I wasn't going to touch the pot."

"You were looking at the pot."

"I was looking at the food inside the pot."

"The food and the pot are inseparable. The seasoning is the food is the pot. Three years of oil and heat in those walls. I put the tuber in raw and it comes out tasting like every meal the pot remembers." Sorvyn lifted the lid. Steam rose. The smell thickened — earthy, warm, something sweet underneath. His face changed. The stillness that normally settled over his features like old leather softened into something that was almost — not quite — a smile.

He tasted it. Nodded. Reached for a jar of ground spice — dark red, coarse — and added a measure with the particular precision of someone who'd done this exact movement a thousand times and still cared about getting it right.

"The market in Darachet is better than the one near the southern route," he said. "Better produce, better spices, and the woman at the grain stall doesn't —"

The door opened and Jorath came in already talking.

"Transport is booked. Commercial carrier, Darachet-Velanthra route, third departure. I talked to the routing clerk about the deployment discount —"

"— try to sell you a premium on standard weight," Sorvyn continued, to nobody. "Honest scales."

"— and he said fourteen percent but only if we file as a four-person unit with combined equipment tonnage, so I need the weight on Vaelen's instrument case because I estimated but if the actual is higher we drop to twelve —"

"Rare quality, honest scales. I was saying."

"— and the seating allocation is open on the upper deck, which means we can get the windward rail if we board early, and the windward rail has the better —"

"Jorath." Sorvyn had set down his spoon. "I am talking about the grain merchant."

"I heard you. Honest scales. Very good. The windward rail —"

"I wasn't finished."

"You were finished. You said 'rare quality' and 'honest scales.' That's a complete thought. The transport leaves at second bell and if we don't pre-file the equipment manifest —"

Sorvyn picked up his spoon and turned back to the pot. "I'm not discussing the transport."

"You don't have to discuss it. You have to be on it."

"I am aware."

Jorath sat down and took a piece of the dark bread from the table without asking. Three kinds were laid out — one dark, one pale, one studded with seeds. "What are these?"

"Don't eat those."

"I'm eating them."

"They're for the flatbread I'm making tomorrow. Jorath. Put it down."

Jorath put the seed bread down and took the dark bread instead. He chewed with the efficiency of a man who regarded meals as intervals between problems. "Sorvyn's going to hate the flight," he said to Vaelen.

From behind the pot: "I already hate the flight."

"The flight is six hours. Maybe seven if the winds are wrong."

"Every flight is too long. The previous one was too long. The one before that was too long. I have been on thirty-seven commercial flights and I have hated thirty-seven of them and the number is about to be thirty-eight."

"You could walk."

"I'd walk to Velanthra if the world were smaller." Sorvyn said it once, without emphasis, and meant it completely. "I'd walk everywhere. Different ground, different air, different food. That's worth the travel. It's the traveling I can't stand."

The room was small — same allocation as everyone else's — but Sorvyn had made it into something that felt larger. Not through decoration. Through density. The pot. The mortar. A knife with a worn handle hanging from a wall hook. A set of ceramic bowls, mismatched, collected from different markets across different deployments. A cutting board with knife-marks so deep they'd become part of the grain. Every object was used, maintained, kept.

"Is Caeleth eating with us?" Sorvyn asked.

Vaelen thought of the boy at the mess hall table. The old woman jerking her chin.

"He's at the mess."

"Alone?"

"He needs to learn the compound."

Sorvyn said nothing. He served the tuber onto three plates — split it open, the flesh golden-brown where it met the pot and pale where it hadn't, the steam carrying the rosemary and the rendered fat and the three years of seasoning that lived in the clay. He carved it with the precision of someone who knew exactly how a tuber should come apart. The slices fell clean.

The first bite was extraordinary. The skin had crisped where the pot's heat was strongest — a crackle that gave way to something dense and smooth underneath, the sweetness of slow roasting married to the salt of the fat and the warmth of the spice. The rosemary wasn't a flavor so much as an atmosphere — it lived in the steam, in the back of the throat, in the way the breath carried it after swallowing. Vaelen tore the pale bread and used it to soak the juices pooling on the plate. The bread was better than the market bread — lighter, with a chew that held the liquid without dissolving.

"This is the best thing you've made," Vaelen said.

"This is adequate. The best thing I made was the lamb shoulder in Vethrane, and you weren't there because you'd taken the coast deployment." Sorvyn ate with the unhurried method of a man who had cooked the food and knew exactly what each bite would be. "Jorath was there. Tell him about the lamb."

"The lamb was good." Jorath was already on his second piece of bread. "The sauce was better. Something with citrus."

"Pressed bitter-rind and stock. Twenty hours of reduction." Sorvyn said this with more warmth than he said most things. The lamb shoulder in Vethrane existed in his memory as a fixed point — the meal against which other meals were measured and found wanting.

Jorath reached for the seed bread again.

"Jorath."

"You have more seeds."

"I have exactly the amount of seeds I need for tomorrow's flatbread, and if you eat them now I have to go back to the market, and if I go back to the market I'm buying more spice, and if I'm buying more spice you're paying for it because you ate the seeds."

"How much is the spice?"

"More than the seeds."

Jorath put the bread down. Picked up the dark bread instead. Calculated something silently. Then: "I'll buy the spice if you make the flatbread tonight instead of tomorrow. Because if we're flying tomorrow, I want something for the transit, and the mess hall travel rations are —"

"Terrible."

"I was going to say overpriced."

"They're both."

Vaelen ate and listened to them negotiate, the way they'd negotiated a hundred times across a dozen deployments — Sorvyn's food against Jorath's budget, quality against economy, the steady warmth of a man who cared about meals against the restless efficiency of a man who cared about margins. Neither won. Neither lost. The friction between them was worn smooth from years of use, and the smoothness was its own kind of affection.

When the plates were empty and Sorvyn was wrapping a portion in cloth — for Caeleth, without asking, without commenting on Vaelen's decision not to invite him — Vaelen stood.

"I'll take it to him."

Sorvyn looked up. His pale eyes — the color of winter sky — held Vaelen's for a moment. Then he handed over the bundle.

"The bread too," he said. "The boy doesn't eat enough."

---

The compound was quieter in the evening. Vaelen walked the main courtyard after delivering Sorvyn's food to Caeleth's quarters — the boy's face when he opened the door and saw the wrapped plate, the way his hands reached for it before his mouth could form the question. Vaelen hadn't stayed. He'd said "Sorvyn" and left, and the weight of the word — the whole weight of it, the pots and the precision and the silent correction — had been enough.

The evening air was cool. The compound walls blocked the wind but not the sky — enormous here, spread above the hillside city, the stars sharp. Below, Darachet spread in the dark — lights in windows, the faint movement of transport creatures on the low routes, the amber glow of the market district where the last vendors were closing.

Essren's map was in his head. The red threads clustered on the frontier. The same quality in two independent readings from two different regions. *I trust your perception. I need you to trust it too.*

Tomorrow they'd fly to Velanthra. The city waited with its interrupted network and its standard deployment parameters.

He went to bed and didn't sleep well.
