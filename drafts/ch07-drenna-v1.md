# Chapter Three
## Drenna

*Version 1 — First Draft*
*Created: 2026-03-19*
*Word count: ~4,800*
*Based on: Episode 2 Chapter Outline v3, Plan v3*

---

The streets widened three blocks past the river bridge and Drenna felt it in her ankles first.

Different stone. The lower city walked on compressed rubble — grey, uneven, patched where the drainage channels had been rerouted too many times. Up here the ground was quarried flat. Pale gold. Her boots — still carrying the red-brown of Mirathyn field soil in the stitching — landed differently, the impact softer, the sound wrong. Quieter. As if the street itself had been told to keep its voice down.

She passed a wall where a garden pressed against the stone from inside. Not the wild tangle of frontier yards, plants fighting for purchase in thin soil. This was arranged. Structured. Climbing vines trained along wire frames, their leaves broad and dark and fed by something richer than rain. The smell hit her two steps later — sweet, almost oily, the scent of flowers she didn't know the names for blooming in quantities that assumed water and warmth and attention. Behind the wall, she could hear the low chatter of a creature in a private enclosure. Not a working animal. A kept one.

The light changed. The lower city's lamps were spaced for function — every third corner, casting pools you walked between. Up here, the light was continuous. Steady. Fed by something in the infrastructure she couldn't identify. The shadows had been designed out.

She'd dressed in her best Solcarin clothes. Fitted jacket, clean trousers, boots polished as much as field leather could be polished. The threadwork band at her wrist — local plant dyes, her mother's patterns — she hadn't taken off. She could have. She'd thought about it. The band marked her as frontier the way an accent marked her as frontier, and she'd spent three years learning to sand down the accent. But the band stayed. She didn't examine why.

Maarthen's message had come that morning. Three words and an address: *Dinner tonight. Come.*

She'd come.

---

The house was rootwood and warm-quarried stone. Not a palace — a merchant's house, the kind of place that said wealth by saying 
nothing about wealth. The door was heavy, dark, oiled so the grain showed. The knocker was bronze, shaped into a bird she didn't recognize — something southern, long-necked, detailed enough to have been cast from life.

He opened it himself. No servant, no intermediary. Maarthen van der Hoeven stood in his own doorway in a loose-collared shirt with the sleeves turned back, and his face broke into genuine pleasure when he saw her.

"Drenna." He clasped her hands. His were broad, weathered, the knuckles thick from years that hadn't been spent in drawing rooms. Mid-fifties. A full head of graying hair swept back from a tanned forehead. He had the build of a man who'd loaded his own cargo once and hadn't entirely stopped. "You look like you've been scrubbing your boots for an hour."

"Two hours."

He laughed — a big sound, uncontained, the laugh of a man who'd learned long ago that the room would adjust to him, not the other way around. He pulled her inside.

The entry hall was a map. She understood that immediately. Every object on every surface came from somewhere — a carved stone figure from the southern settlements, a bolt of dyed cloth pinned to the wall in deep indigo and gold, brass navigational instruments she'd never seen before, a miniature model of a commercial carrier mounted on a stand of dark wood, every wing detailed, every rib visible. Maps. Actual maps — hand-drawn, annotated in Maarthen's cramped handwriting, charting routes that ran from the Hearthlands to the outer edges and beyond. One map showed territory past the network's reach. Places she'd heard named at frontier tables and nowhere else.

On a shelf near the door: a wooden figure, long-limbed, angular, carved in a style she didn't recognize. The wood was pale, unsanded, with a grain that ran in patterns too regular to be natural. It didn't come from inside the network.

She looked at it longer than she meant to.

"Ah." Maarthen followed her gaze. "That's Korvathi work. From beyond the Ashward edge. I'll tell you about it over dinner." He put a hand on her back — warm, proprietary — and steered her toward the sound of voices.

---

Five men stood in Maarthen's dining room. All holding glasses. All older than her. All at ease in a way that came from having been at ease in rooms like this their entire lives.

Maarthen introduced her with a hand still on her shoulder. "This is Drenna. She's been doing fieldwork with the Solvarei — just came off a frontier rotation. The sharpest young practitioner I've seen in twenty years, and I've been looking." Pride in his voice. The particular pleasure of a man who believed his taste was excellent and wanted the room to agree.

The men nodded. Some smiled. None of them adjusted the way they were standing.

Lucro was the first to speak to her directly. Older, lean, with the precise movements of a man who weighed things for a living — not with his hands, with his eyes. Everything about him was measurement. He was the Mirathyn trader, and the room knew it. "Frontier rotation," he said, looking at her boots. "Which region?"

"Western circuit. Four communities."

He made a sound — not dismissal, assessment. "The western yields have been erratic. You'd have seen that."

"I saw the communities."

The second man — younger, maybe thirty-five, with the soft hands of someone who'd never loaded his own cargo — introduced himself as the holder of agricultural lands in the frontier regions. His name was Devriis. He asked her where she'd studied, and when she named the Solcarin, his eyebrows rose slightly. The third man didn't introduce himself. He was the oldest in the room — perhaps seventy, with a face that had stopped trying to be anything other than what it was. White hair. Small, still eyes. He sat in the corner chair with a glass he hadn't touched and watched the room without blinking. She learned later his name was Hendryck. Thirty years in rooms like this. He spoke twice during dinner. Both times, the room stopped.

The fourth man was closer to Maarthen's age, heavy through the shoulders, with restless hands that kept finding his glass, his chair, the edge of the table. He was building something — she didn't know what yet, but the energy was unmistakable. He asked her three questions in rapid succession about transport infrastructure on the frontier, and when she answered the third, he leaned back and looked at Maarthen. A nod. Not to her — to him. *All right. She's real.*

Maarthen poured her drink himself. Something clear, in a glass with no color. Not Mirathyn. She didn't recognize it. The smell was sharp — botanical, with a heat underneath.

She didn't want to drink it.

The taste was wrong for her mouth. Too refined, too clean, the kind of flavor that erased what came before it. She'd grown up on Mirathyn from frontier stills — the gold warmth, the familiar burn. This was something else. Something designed for mouths that had never tasted the frontier version. She drank because not drinking would have marked her more than drinking, and she'd spent three years learning to not be marked. She sipped. It sat in her throat. She sipped again.

Maarthen watched her face and smiled. "Give it a minute."

She gave it a minute. It didn't get better. She kept drinking.

---

The food arrived in courses, and every course was a route.

Roasted bird in a southern glaze — sweet, smoky, the skin crisped until it crackled between her teeth. Root vegetables she recognized from the frontier, but roasted in an oil that tasted of something she couldn't name, something that turned a food she'd eaten a hundred times into a food she'd never had. Fish laid on quarry stone that was still warm from whatever had heated it — the flesh white, almost translucent, seasoned with grains of something coarse and bright. And the bread. The bread was the thing that stopped her. Dense, dark, crusted with seeds, and inside: soft, warm, the taste of earth and grain and time. She'd never had bread that tasted of time. On the frontier, bread tasted of necessity.

She ate. She ate genuinely, with the pleasure of someone who'd spent her childhood eating what was adequate and was now being handed what was extraordinary. She tried not to stop chewing when the flavors hit. She mostly failed. The men around the table reached for bread and tore it without looking. They refilled their glasses without checking the level. The food was furniture to them — reliable, assumed, beneath notice. Drenna noticed everything.

The dinner was not a meeting. It was what it looked like — men who knew each other eating at a friend's table, talking about everything and nothing. Devriis described his daughter's first semester at a Solcarin and the letter she'd sent him complaining about a philosophy instructor. Lucro argued with Maarthen about a supplier in the southern settlements — a disagreement so familiar it had become comedy, both men hitting their marks, the room laughing at the rhythm. Hendryck said nothing. The restless man — she hadn't caught his name, or he hadn't offered it — asked Devriis about land prices in the eastern regions and listened with the particular attention of someone who was already calculating.

And then Maarthen told the story.

He leaned back, glass in hand — his second, he drank freely, comfortably, the glass an extension of his ease — and said: "I ever tell you about the Korvathi?"

Some groans. Lucro: "Only every dinner." Maarthen waved him off.

"Twenty years ago. My first route past the Ashward edge." Warmth in his voice. The particular weight of something that had shaped him. "Past the network's reach. Two weeks out. The creatures don't go willingly — you have to convince them, and even then, they get slow. They feel the Amarvel thinning underneath them and they want to turn back."

"What's past the edge?" Devriis.

"People." Maarthen's voice shifted — not softer, wider. The voice of a man who'd been genuinely surprised by what he'd found. "Not settlers. Not frontiersmen gone wild. People with families, towns, trade between themselves. They've been there — well, nobody knows how long. Before us, maybe."

"Before the network?"

"Before anyone kept records about the network." He swirled his glass. "They don't bond creatures. Can't — there's nothing to bond through out there. So they BUILD. Carts. Platforms on wheels. Tools for moving across ground that would make our engineers stare. Slow compared to flight, but they don't have flight, and what they've built instead is — I've been doing this thirty years and I hadn't seen anything I'd call ingenious until I saw what the Korvathi do with a plank and a rope and a hill."

He described them with the pleasure of a man recounting a world that had expanded him. Their food — cured meats and preserved roots, different base ingredients from anything inside the network, but the same principle of making scarcity last. Their cloth — rougher than anything at this table, but dyed in colors extracted from stone and clay and insects that Drenna recognized from her own frontier. Their word for the people inside the network: *Telvareth.* The ones who listen to roots. "Not unfriendly," Maarthen said. "Accurate."

He'd traded with them. Successfully. Made money. Brought back objects that were now arranged on his shelves and walls — the carved figure she'd noticed at the door, the pale wood with the too-regular grain. "Good business," he said. "Hard to get to. But good business when you get there."

"Are there more of them now?" The restless man. Practical, pointed.

Maarthen tilted his head. "They've always been there. More of them lately, maybe. Hard to say — I haven't run that route in years. Too long, too slow, the creatures don't want to go that far anymore." He shrugged. "The edges used to feel farther away. Routes I ran twenty years ago are shorter now — not because I'm faster, but because there's less distance to cover before you hit the point where the creatures won't go."

Devriis frowned. "The frontier reports mention incursions. Strangers on the edges."

"Displacement events," Lucro corrected, with the particular distaste of a man who believed the right word for a thing was the word that made the thing manageable. "Unauthorized territory occupation."

"Whatever you call it, the western handlers won't fly the Torven circuit anymore."

The conversation moved. Maarthen refilled Devriis's glass. Lucro turned to the bread. The outer edges went back to being a line item — something that existed, that cost money, that someone would eventually handle.

---

Lucro brought up Torven's valley while comparing regional Mirathyn yields. A trade conversation, nothing more. "Three years ago, Torven's valley produced the cleanest product on the western circuit. Clear gold, held its color in the glass. Now it sits heavier. My buyers in the Hearthlands notice."

Devriis nodded. "The families are drifting east from that region. Census down three seasons running."

"My father tended the node at Torven's valley."

The room adjusted. Not dramatically — a shift of attention, heads turning half a degree. Lucro looked at her.

"What's the node reading at now?"

"It's barely a shimmer. At midday you can see it if you know where to look."

"And the quality decline? The specialists said it was cyclical."

"The water used to taste like iron from the mineral runoff. Now it tastes like nothing." She kept her voice level. "The families who stayed have gotten used to it. That's the part that worries me — people stop noticing."

She didn't connect it to the route delays. She didn't connect it to the families drifting east or the creatures refusing to fly. She offered one detail from the ground, and the detail was vivid, and the room held it because it was specific in a way their second-hand reports never were.

Hendryck's eyes moved to her. First time. He said nothing. But his glass shifted a quarter turn in his hand — a gesture so small she wasn't sure she'd seen it.

Maarthen watched from his seat. His face had the particular satisfaction of a man whose painting had just drawn a second look from someone who knows paintings.

Then she said something she hadn't planned. "He used to tap the ground with his heel when he was reading the node. A rhythm — three taps, then he'd listen. I'd hear it from the house. Every morning. Every evening." She stopped. "He hasn't tapped in years."

That wasn't strategy. That wasn't evidence. That was a daughter sitting in a room full of men who'd never been to Torven's valley, remembering a sound that had been the rhythm of her childhood, and missing it. For one sentence, the ambition went quiet and the grief came through, unguarded, pointless, real.

Then she picked up her glass and drank.

---

The Commander arrived between the fish and the dessert.

Drenna didn't see him enter. She heard the room change. The restless man straightened in his chair. Devriis adjusted his collar. Lucro stopped mid-sentence — a calculation about southern yields that had been heading toward a complaint — and the pause lasted a half-beat too long. Even Hendryck, who hadn't moved all evening, shifted his weight.

Then the doorway filled with a man who didn't fill it.

Medium height. Weathered face — deep lines around the mouth, sun damage at the temples, the skin of someone who'd spent his first thirty years outdoors regardless of what his station was now. Hair cropped short, steel-gray, receding at the temples without apology. Hands at his sides, thick-knuckled, scarred across the backs. Hands that had learned tools before they'd learned anything else.

He wore clean lines. Good fabric. Nothing that announced. He entered Maarthen's dining room and the conversation shifted around him without breaking.

"There he is." Maarthen rose, crossed the room, clasped the Commander's hand. Warm. Unhurried. Two men who occupied different territories of the same landscape. "I wasn't sure you'd come."

"I said I'd come." His voice was flat, unhurried, declarative. He took the chair Maarthen offered — at the corner of the table, not the head — and when the food was placed before him, he chose the bread and the root vegetables and ignored the fish entirely. Old habit. The kind of choosing that came from a body that remembered when food was fuel.

Drenna watched him eat. She'd seen that choosing before. At her father's table. At the frontier wayhouses where practitioners ate what was available and didn't comment. The Commander ate the way people ate where she came from.

She was still awed by the fish. He'd been here long enough to refuse it.

Maarthen introduced her. Warmly, casually — a hand gesture toward her end of the table. "This is Drenna. My frontier practitioner. Sharp as a blade and twice as useful. She's been—"

"The Amarith doesn't take rotations." The Commander didn't raise his voice. He didn't need to. "We take practitioners who've earned their place through demonstrated excellence at tier. When she's ready, she'll be evaluated. Not before."

Maarthen's face held for a beat. Then the half-smile arrived — practiced, warm, the expression of a man who'd been navigating rooms for three decades and knew a bruise from a wound. "You'd have said the same thing about me thirty years ago, Korvaan. And you'd have been right."

Lucro laughed. Devriis smiled into his glass. The room settled. The Commander — Korvaan — let the moment pass without acknowledgment. He'd made his point. The point was not about Maarthen.

But he'd looked at HER when he said *when she's ready.*

Not at Maarthen. Not at the table. At Drenna. Dark eyes, still, unhurried. Holding for one beat. Two. The look of someone who reads rooms the way a blade reads grain — finding the line of least resistance, the hidden fracture, the place where pressure would split the wood. He looked at her and she felt her posture change — her spine straightening, her breath catching, her left hand finding the threadwork band at her wrist and gripping. Something in her chest contracted. Not fear. Not recognition. Something older, simpler — the body's response to being seen by someone who sees everything.

Then his eyes released. He turned to Hendryck and asked a question about territorial defense assessments, and the room rearranged itself around his voice.

She spent the rest of the dinner watching him. She couldn't help it. He spoke less than anyone at the table. He asked three questions. Each one was the question nobody else had been willing to ask. When Devriis mentioned frontier incursions, the Commander said: "Organized armed groups with territorial objectives." Five words. The room went quiet. Not because he'd said something alarming — because he'd said something accurate, and accuracy was uncomfortable when everyone else was speaking in euphemisms.

He didn't finish his glass. She'd been watching the level. It hadn't moved.

---

After the dessert — a custard made with something floral that she couldn't name and didn't want to — Maarthen pushed back from the table. "Gentlemen. Tiles?"

A shift. The men were standing, moving toward a back room she hadn't seen. Through the doorway she caught a glimpse: a table covered in felt, tile racks, a cabinet of dark wood holding sets in different materials — bone, quarried stone, polished rootwood. The restless man was already reaching for a rack. Devriis was rolling his sleeves. Even Hendryck had risen from his corner, moving with the unhurried certainty of a man who'd been winning this game for thirty years.

Maarthen walked her toward the front entrance. His hand on her back again — warm, paternal, proprietary.

"You did well tonight," he said. He meant it. The warmth was genuine — the warmth of a man who'd placed a bet early and was watching it pay. "The Torven detail. Good."

"Thank you."

"The next gathering is in three weeks. Velanthra. Bigger room, different people. I'd like you there." He said *I'd like you there* with the certainty of a man who assumed his invitations were gifts. "Think about what you'd want to say if someone asked you about the frontier. Not the field data — the story. These people understand stories."

She nodded. He clasped her hands again, the same two-handed grip, the same broad warmth. Then he turned and walked toward the back room, and the door closed, and the sound of tiles being shuffled came through the wood, and she was standing alone in the entrance hall.

Not alone.

The Commander was at the door. Pulling on a coat — plain, dark, without insignia. He wasn't staying for the game. The Amarith had business, or he didn't play, or he simply didn't stay in rooms longer than he needed to.

Her throat tightened. She was twenty years old and the most important person she'd ever met was three paces away and reaching for the door handle.

"The node at Torven's valley." She said it before she'd decided to. Short. Too direct. "You know it?"

He stopped. Turned. Not fully — a half-turn, his hand still on the handle. The dark eyes found her again.

"I grew up forty minutes south of it."

Four words. Her breath held. He was FROM there. The same dirt. The same thin Amarvel, the same water that used to taste like iron. He'd walked the same ground and he'd climbed from it to — this. To a room that arranged itself around his silence.

She didn't say anything else. There wasn't time. He looked at her for one more beat — shorter than the first, but heavier — and said: "The water used to taste like iron there, too."

Then he opened the door and walked into the dark.

---

The streets narrowed around her as she descended.

The pale stone gave way to grey. The continuous light broke into pools with dark intervals between them. The gardens behind walls disappeared, replaced by bare stone fronts and shuttered windows and the smell of cook-fires and the sounds of people living close. Her boots — the same boots, the same red-brown field soil in the stitching — found the familiar uneven surface and her ankles adjusted. Home ground. Not home, but the body's version of it.

She stopped at a vendor on the corner of the market road. A woman wrapping up for the night, a brazier with tubers still warm in the ash. Drenna bought one and a piece of flatbread. She ate walking. The tuber was honest — dense, earthy, seasoned with nothing but heat. The bread at Maarthen's table had tasted of time. This tasted of hunger satisfied. Both were real. She chewed and walked and thought.

Five men in a beautiful room eating beautiful food. Each one had built something — routes, holdings, trade networks, reputation. They played their cards brilliantly. Maarthen had opened routes to territories past the network's reach. Lucro had tracked Mirathyn quality across regions for decades. Devriis knew his census numbers to the family. Hendryck had survived thirty years of rooms and games and hadn't lost yet. The restless man was already building whatever came next.

And each of them had mentioned, between the bread and the fish and the argument about Solcarin philosophy instructors, a problem. Mirathyn quality declining. Routes getting shorter. Families drifting. Creatures refusing. Each problem separate. Each problem solvable with the tools they knew — a new supplier, a different route, an incentive for the families, a younger handler. Individual solutions from individual men. Cards played one at a time.

Nobody at the table had said: *these are the same problem.*

She wouldn't have said it either. Not because she hadn't seen it — she'd seen it at four communities before she'd ever entered that room. But because saying it would mean the tools they knew weren't enough, and the men who wielded those tools would have to become something else, and nobody becomes something else willingly. Not even her.

The Commander had shown her something different. Not a better hand of cards. A different game. He didn't own routes or land or trade networks. He'd built something else — the Amarith, an organization that read the whole board while the foxes read their individual squares. He ate the plainest food and spoke the fewest words and the room bent toward him. Not because he was wealthier or better born — because he saw something they couldn't. The shape of the whole. And the whole was shifting.

She passed a garden wall where the vines looked different from the ones she'd seen on the way up. Thinner. The leaves smaller, curled at the edges, the green faded toward grey. She stopped. Looked closer. The vine was still growing — still alive — but something about the growth was wrong. Slower. Tentative. The way a plant grows when the ground underneath it has started to change and the roots are the first to know.

She'd seen this on the frontier. Two years ago. The same thinning, the same grey at the edges, the same tentative reach. On the frontier, it had been a signal — the Amarvel pulling back, the ground losing the thing that fed it. Here, in the upper city, nobody had noticed. The vine was still growing. The garden was still green. But the edges were different, and the edges were where it always started.

Further down: a creature dispatch — a small station where neighborhood messengers were kept. The creatures inside were restless. She could hear them through the walls — shuffling, the low clicking sound they made when their bonds were stressed. On the frontier, she'd heard that sound before the nodes declined. Before the water went flat. Before the quiet.

The city was two years behind the frontier. Maybe three. The same symptoms, earlier stage. The men in Maarthen's dining room didn't know yet. They wouldn't know until the trade routes stopped being delayed and started being refused entirely, until the Mirathyn didn't just sit heavier in the glass but stopped coming at all, until the families didn't drift but ran. By then the game would have changed and the cards they knew would be the wrong cards, and the Commander — Korvaan, born forty minutes south of Torven's valley, who'd eaten the plainest bread at the richest table — would be the one who'd built the thing that still worked.

*The water used to taste like iron there, too.*

He'd heard her. When she'd spoken about her father, about the water, about the tapping — he'd appeared to be talking to Hendryck. But he'd heard. Every word. And at the entrance, in the dark, with one hand on the door, he'd given her the one detail that told her they were from the same ground.

She held it. She didn't know yet what it meant — an invitation or a test or simply a man acknowledging that he recognized the dirt under her nails because he'd carried the same dirt once. She'd replay it for weeks. She'd carry it the way she carried everything — filed, assessed, weighed for what it was worth.

But underneath the filing, underneath the assessment: a girl from a dying frontier settlement, standing in a city that was dying too, carrying a tuber in one hand and an invitation to a table in the other, and somewhere in the space between them, the threadwork band at her wrist that she hadn't taken off and the sound of her father tapping the ground that she'd never hear again.

She kept walking.
