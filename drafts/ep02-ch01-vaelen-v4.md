# Episode Two — Chapter One
## Vaelen

*Version 4 — Revision*

---

The Solvarei compound smelled of wet stone and breakfast smoke.

Vaelen walked up the hill from the lower city with his collar turned up against the morning and a cup of Sorvyn's ashroot in his hand — the real kind, ground from Ashara stock in the clay mortar Sorvyn had been carrying for six years and refused to let anyone else touch. He'd stopped at Sorvyn's apartment on the way up, the same detour he made most mornings when they were in Darachet. The door had been open, the mortar already working, the cup pressed into his hand before he'd finished lacing his boots. Sorvyn didn't ask if he wanted it. The ashroot was ground. The cup was offered. That was the morning.

It was too strong. It was always too strong. Vaelen drank it because Sorvyn made it, and the bitterness had its own kind of warmth — smoky, deep, the volcanic soil of the Ashara region concentrated into something that sat heavy on the tongue and didn't fade.

Home wasn't the compound — home was Darachet, the city spread below the hillside where the Solvarei kept their compound. But the compound was where you worked, where you prepped, where you came back dirty and tired from the frontier and started planning the next deployment. It was the center.

He came through the gate and the compound opened around him. The pale stone caught the early sun on its upper stories — local quarry stone, warm-toned where the light hit it, grey-green where it didn't, with the thickness of walls built by people who expected them to still be standing in a century. At ground level the courtyard was still in shadow, the flagstones cool underfoot, worn smooth along the central path between the main hall and the workshops. Twelve years of boots following the same line had polished a track into the stone — Vaelen's feet knew it without looking.

The main hall anchored the north side, its double doors propped open with an iron wedge that had dished the stone beneath it from use. The morning light came through the wide windows in long, clean bars — the building had been designed for it, the windows positioned to pull the sun in and hold it. Inside, the hall opened up — high-ceilinged, the morning light falling in bars across long wooden tables where practitioners sat shoulder to shoulder, voices overlapping, the scrape of benches on stone, the clatter of ceramic bowls, the smell of bread and hot fat and something herbed drifting from the kitchen at the far end.

To the east, the training yards. Vaelen could hear them before he saw them — the crack of wooden practice frames against each other, a voice correcting someone's stance, the rhythm of drills that hadn't changed since he'd been a junior running them himself. The frames simulated root junctions, and a cluster of second-years were working a branching pattern, their movements too careful, too deliberate. They'd learn. The body had to stop thinking about the form before the form would work.

To the west, the workshops. Hammering. The smell of oil and heated metal. Vaelen could hear Jorath in there, arguing with a quartermaster about replacement casing for a resonance probe — Jorath's voice patient and specific, the quartermaster's tired. Between the workshops and the training yard, the courtyard garden — dark soil turned and worked, early shoots pushing through in rows that suggested a practitioner's precision applied to growing things. Somebody maintained it without credit. The herb beds closest to the kitchen gave off a green, resinous scent that mixed with the stone-smell and the faint musk drifting from the creature roost at the compound's eastern edge.

The kitchen occupied the ground floor of the main hall, and it was good. Fresh market produce delivered from Darachet each morning: cut fruit in stone bowls, seed bread still warm from the ovens, smoked fish laid on wooden boards, warm flatbread with herb oil, roasted root slices with salt, soft eggs in clay cups, honeyed nuts in a carved wooden bowl. A large pot of ashroot sat on the hearth, kept warm, the standard compound brew — decent, sourced in bulk, nothing Sorvyn would touch. A well-run compound kitchen was a perk. You came back from weeks of wayhouse stew and frontier rations and the first morning at the compound you ate roasted squash with herb oil and a soft egg and bread that someone had baked before dawn, and the taste of it said *you're back, the work is done for now, rest.*

The assignment board hung on the west wall of the main hall — a wide slate surface with postings arranged by region, each one a community's request for practitioners. Vaelen scanned it out of habit as he passed. Three new postings since yesterday. A settlement in the Hearthlands reporting reduced Mirathyn yield. A trading town on the eastern corridor with creature dispatch failures. And Velanthra — the one already marked with his team's name.

Beyond the board, the creature dispatch station.

Everything the Solvarei knew about the world outside these walls came through this station. A covered roost structure at the eastern perimeter, open-sided, built from the same pale stone as the rest of the compound but roofed with heavy oilcloth that kept the rain off the perches. The perches climbed the back wall at different heights, each one assigned to a route: local routes low, regional routes higher, the long-distance perches — cross-frontier, multi-day — at the top where the creatures could launch into open air without obstruction. Each creature bonded to a specific handler. Not interchangeable. A handler sent and received through their creature only, the bond ensuring the message went where it was meant to go and came back true. The whole communication network of the Solvarei ran through this roost — field teams reporting in, client requests arriving, deployment orders going out. Every instruction Commander Essren — the woman who ran the Solvarei's field operations from her map-covered office on the second floor — gave to a team in the field passed through a handler's hands and a creature's wings.

Vaelen watched the morning rhythm of it. A handler at the low perches was preparing a dispatch — a woman with close-cropped hair and sun-darkened hands, murmuring to a dark-plumed bird pressed against the hollow of her throat. The private language between handler and creature, built over years: specific tones, specific cadences, words that meant nothing to anyone else. She fixed a thin strip of treated cloth to the creature's leg band — the message, written in the compact notation that handlers used, dense with abbreviation. Then she tossed the bird upward with a practiced motion of her forearm and it caught the air and banked east and was gone. Across the roost, a different handler was receiving — stepping forward as a creature landed on the mid-level perches, the bird agitated, feathers ruffled from a long flight. The handler read the cloth strip, frowned, read it again. Walked it toward the assignment board.

At the far end of the roost, a third handler stood with her arms crossed, watching an empty perch. The creature that should have been on it — the creature bonded to the eastern corridor route — hadn't returned. Three days now, someone near the board said. Not the first time this season. The handler's face was flat, patient, the kind of patience that covered something harder underneath. She'd wait. The creature would come back or it wouldn't. In the meantime, the eastern corridor was quiet.

Maps of past deployments covered the wall beside the dispatch station — pinned charts showing team movements, markers indicating completion, the accumulated history of the Solvarei's work rendered as geography. Twelve years of Vaelen's career was somewhere in those pins. He didn't look for himself.

The compound had its own weather — people arriving and people preparing to leave, the overlap between those two states filling the yard with a friction that was its own kind of warmth. A senior team crossed toward the equipment shed, their boots caked with the red-brown soil of the southern frontier. A group of juniors watched them pass with the too-obvious attention of people who wanted to look casual. A railing near the kitchen entrance had been polished to a dark shine by decades of hands leaning on it — Vaelen's own hand found it without thinking, and the wood was smooth and warm.

"Vaelen."

Theryn was crossing the yard from the direction of the equipment shed — a second-year team lead, broad face, the kind of man who said your name before you'd finished deciding whether to acknowledge him.

"Commander Essren wants you. Her office, when you're done with —" He looked at the ashroot. "That."

"That bad?"

"She didn't say bad. She said *when you're available*, which from Essren means now."

Vaelen raised his cup. "Tell her I'm walking slowly."

Theryn almost smiled. "I'll tell her you're on your way." He paused. "She's been going over the Torven readings since dawn. Cross-referencing with Hathral's data from the Delverath corridor. Just so you know."

He left. Vaelen stood in the yard with his too-strong ashroot.

The Torven readings. He'd shared them the way all practitioners shared field data — directly, through debrief, practitioner to practitioner. He'd sat in Essren's office two days after returning and walked her through what his team had found: branch restoration, capillary recovery, the downstream pull, the silence in the gaps. Standard verbal debrief. He'd given the assessment his training gave him. *Within parameters. Node stabilized. The community will have a growing season.*

He hadn't lied. He'd said what he knew to be true. The node was stable. The branch work had held. What he hadn't said — what he hadn't known how to say — was the rest. The downstream pull that got stronger when you fixed what it was pulling. The silence between the trunk pulses that was heavier than anything in twelve years of practice. The vibration underneath — the geological hum that existed where the network thinned, that had no name and no precedent.

He'd recorded all of it in his raw readings. Every frequency, every gradient, every anomalous silence. That was standard practice — a practitioner's readings went to their commander for review, and Vaelen was meticulous. The data was complete. But when he'd sat across from Essren and given the verbal assessment, the words he'd found were the words his training provided. *Within parameters.* Because the node was stable. Because the branch work had held. Because the things he'd sensed in the silence didn't fit any pattern he'd ever read, and Vaelen didn't report feelings. He reported data.

He drank his ashroot and walked toward her office.

---

The mess hall was loud at midmorning. Vaelen passed through it on his way to Essren's wing and the room adjusted around him — people orienting slightly, making eye contact, finding reasons to intersect his path. A junior practitioner looked up from her bowl and straightened. An older field technician raised a hand. He nodded back. Smiled.

He saw Caeleth.

The boy was sitting at a table near the wall, alone. Not the dramatic loneliness of someone who'd been excluded — the quieter kind. The kind where you'd found a seat and nobody had told you to move but nobody had sat down either, and the meal in front of you was getting cold while you waited for a pause in someone else's conversation that would be wide enough to step through.

Vaelen recognized it. Not from observation — from memory.

He could have sat down. Caeleth would have looked up with that expression — the one that tried to be casual and wasn't — and Vaelen would have said something warm and easy and the wanting would have been soothed.

He walked past.

His left hand closed. He opened it.

At the far end of the mess, an old woman was wiping down the serving counter with a rag so threadbare it was mostly memory. She'd been there when Vaelen was a junior — same counter, same rag, same expression that suggested she'd seen every practitioner who'd ever lived rotate through this hall and found them all equally in need of feeding. He'd never learned her name. He'd been meaning to for twelve years. Every time he passed, the intention renewed itself and then slipped away, crowded out by something louder.

She caught his eye now and jerked her chin toward Caeleth's table. Then toward the empty seat across from the boy. Then back at Vaelen. The message was clear and had nothing warm about it.

Vaelen kept walking.

---

Essren's office was small and full of maps.

The walls were covered — charts pinned edge to edge, overlapping where the frontier zones met. Thread connected locations in a system Vaelen didn't fully recognize: red for anomalies, green for stable readings, blue for Amarith communications. The threads crossed and clustered and the clusters told a story that the individual data points didn't. A desk occupied the center, its surface clear except for a stack of reading notes in Essren's tight handwriting and a cup of something that had stopped being warm a long time ago.

She sat behind it. Her face was angular, lined, the skin settling along the bones in a way that recorded years of thinking about things she hadn't shared. Her hands were flat on the desk. She watched him come in without moving, without adjusting her posture, without doing anything that suggested she'd been waiting. The room felt inhabited, compressed, the air carrying the weight of sustained attention.

"Sit down."

He sat. The chair was hard. Everything in Essren's office was hard — the chair, the light, the questions.

"Tell me about Renathi's contract," she said.

He blinked. Not the topic he'd expected. "Renathi? The farming cooperative?"

"They've asked for a follow-up team. Specifically requested a senior practitioner. Their node's been stable for two seasons — the work Dhaven's team did last year is holding. But the community lead wants a verification assessment before the planting cycle. Standard client request." She leaned back. "I'm thinking of sending Terys."

"Terys is good. Clean reader, methodical. He'd give them a solid assessment."

"He would. But the Renathi lead has been difficult — she wants senior, she means senior, and she's been vocal about it in the regional network. If I send a second-year, she'll take it as a downgrade." Essren tapped the desk once. "The Solvarei can't afford to lose Renathi. They pay well and they refer. I'm considering having you stop through on the way to Velanthra."

"That adds two days to the route."

"It adds a satisfied client and a renewed contract. Two days against sustained revenue." She looked at him. "I'll leave it to Jorath's routing. If he can make it work without missing the Velanthra window, do it. If not, Terys goes."

This was Essren — the work before the mystery. The Solvarei ran on relationships, on reputation, on communities that trusted them enough to keep hiring. She managed that trust precisely, with an eye on what each decision cost.

"Now," she said. "Torven."

The shift was slight. Her voice didn't change. Her posture didn't change. But the room contracted — routine falling away, the real conversation surfacing underneath.

"You told me the node was stable," she said. "Standard debrief. Within parameters. Branch work holding."

"It is stable."

"Your readings say something you didn't."

He waited.

"A downstream pull of increasing intensity, south-southwest, active across the full monitoring period. Silence density in the trunk-level pauses exceeding anything in our records — and I checked. A capillary recovery that's being siphoned by the same pull your branch work was designed to counteract." She reached for the reading notes on her desk — her own handwriting, her own cross-references. "And a secondary oscillation in the silence layer that doesn't match any standard frequency."

"That's what I recorded."

"Yes. You recorded all of it. Meticulously." She set the notes down. "And then you sat across from me and said *within parameters.*" She looked at him — not accusation, something more precise than that. "Your readings are telling a story you haven't read yet, Vaelen."

His left hand rested on his thigh. It didn't close. He kept it open.

"The node is stabilized," he said. "The branch work held. The community will have a growing season. All of that is true."

"I know it's true. What else is true?"

He breathed. "The downstream pull is active and getting stronger. The silence in the trunk-level pauses is unlike anything I've read in twelve years of practice. The branch work may have accelerated it. The fix may be feeding the problem." He paused. "And there's something underneath the silence that I don't have a name for. Something that doesn't match any frequency I've ever recorded. It was there. I documented it. I don't know what it is."

"But you sense what it means."

"I sense something. I don't trust it."

"Why not?"

The question was simple. The answer wasn't. Because his training told him to report what he could verify, and this was beyond verification. Because the instruments hadn't flagged it, and the instruments were the baseline. Because what he'd felt in the silence at Torven — the weight of it, the geological hum beneath the gap — didn't fit any model he'd been taught. And Vaelen didn't trust what didn't fit a model. His precision was the thing that made him good. It was also the thing that made him hesitate when precision failed.

"Because I don't know what it is," he said. "And I don't report what I don't understand."

Essren studied him. Then she stood. Walked to the wall map. The red threads clustered on the frontier — more of them than Vaelen had expected. "The frontier readings have been shifting for eight months. Gradually. Node stress, recovery rates declining, farming yields down in five districts, transport creature reluctance on three major routes. Individual events, individual explanations." She traced a line of red thread with her finger. "Your data is the first that suggests it might not be individual. That there might be a pattern underneath."

"I don't know what the pattern is."

"No. But you perceived something your instruments didn't. And your readings are unlike anything in our records." The lines around her eyes deepened. "Whatever you're sensing out there, it's real. I've cross-referenced enough to be certain."

She turned from the map. Held his eyes.

Said nothing.

The silence was specific. Not empty — weighted, deliberate, the kind of silence a person holds when the words would diminish what the silence conveys. She held it until Vaelen felt the instruction in it — not spoken, not softened, just present: *your gift is ahead of your training. Trust it.*

He nodded. Not the warm nod. A different one. Smaller.

"Hathral's team debriefed from the Delverath corridor three weeks ago," she said. "Standard assessment. Same language — stable, within parameters. But when I compared their readings to yours — the actual data — their pattern-reader flagged a trunk-level anomaly with similar characteristics. Not identical. The readings differ in scale and frequency. But the same quality. Something in the pauses. Something that isn't supposed to be there."

Vaelen's breathing didn't change. He was aware of it not changing, which was its own kind of tell.

"Two independent readings," Essren said. "Different regions. Same phenomenon. Hathral's reader is good, methodical, but she's working with standard instruments and standard training. She noted it as unusual and moved on. You —" She looked at him. "You gave me everything. But you walked out of this office without saying what it means."

"I don't know what it means."

"Yes you do. You just don't trust it yet."

The room held the words. Vaelen felt the weight of them — not anger, not accusation. Something harder to argue with. She was right. He'd felt the pattern at Torven. Felt it in his body, in the way the silence pressed against his reading, in the wrongness that his instruments couldn't measure but his gift could. He'd recorded the data because that was what a good practitioner did. He hadn't spoken the interpretation because the interpretation was just a feeling, and feelings weren't evidence.

But Essren was telling him: the evidence was catching up to the feeling.

"The Amarith's assessment directorate has been reviewing frontier data through their own channels," Essren said. She said it carefully, each word placed. "They work their own sources — different scope, different scale. Trunk-level, systemic. They haven't deployed teams to the frontier yet. But if the next quarter's data confirms what yours and Hathral's suggest, they'll want firsthand readings from the field." She looked at him. "They'll want yours."

Vaelen had crossed paths with an Amarith assessment team once, at a regional junction two years ago. Three practitioners — quiet, precise, moving through the network with a fluency that made his own gift feel provincial. They'd worked the trunk level: the deep structural flows, the arterial pulses, the systems that the Solvarei never touched because the Solvarei worked the branches and capillaries, the community-level problems, the ground where it met the people. Different scope. The Amarith worked on Amarwen herself. The Solvarei worked on the world she fed.

He'd watched them. Three people doing in an afternoon what would have taken his team a week — not because they were better, but because they were working at a scale where the problems were larger and the tools matched. Afterward, they'd left without speaking to the local practitioners. Not cruel. Just operating in a different register. Their clients were governments and regional bodies. The Solvarei's clients were communities and settlements. Two firms, same world, different altitude.

He'd stood at the edge of their perimeter and watched them go and thought: *I could work at that scale. I could read what they read.* And then: *I'd have to leave the Solvarei to do it.* And then he'd gone back to his team and his branch work and his communities and hadn't thought about it again. Or had thought about it, quietly, the way you think about a door you chose not to open.

"Velanthra," Essren said. She was moving on. "Your next deployment. Network connection interrupted in the commercial district — trade infrastructure, creature dispatch routes, the usual urban systems. The assignment came through two days ago. Your team is cleared and equipped."

"We'll be ready by end of day."

"Vaelen." She stopped him at the door. "Next time. Tell me what you actually find. All of it. Even the parts you can't explain."

He turned back. She was watching him with those flat, unreadable eyes.

"I'll think about it," he said.

"That's what worries me."

---

Sorvyn's apartment was on the third floor of a stone building in the lower city, five streets from the market and close enough to the compound that the walk took ten minutes in good weather. He'd chosen it for the kitchen — a room with a wide stone counter, a hearth with an iron grate, and a window that caught the afternoon light. Every other practitioner Vaelen knew chose their lodging for proximity, or price, or the view of the hills. Sorvyn had walked through the door, looked at the counter and the hearth, and signed the lease.

The apartment smelled of rosemary and rendered fat and something Vaelen couldn't identify that turned out to be a tuber Sorvyn had found at the Darachet market that morning, roasting whole in a clay pot on the hearth.

"Don't touch the pot."

"I wasn't going to touch the pot."

"You were looking at the pot."

"I was looking at the food inside the pot."

"The food and the pot are inseparable. Three years of oil and heat in those walls. I put the tuber in raw and it comes out tasting like every meal the pot remembers." Sorvyn lifted the lid. Steam rose. The smell thickened — earthy, warm, something sweet underneath. The creases in his face rearranged — his eyes softened, his mouth loosened, the permanent set of his jaw eased into something quieter.

He tasted it. Nodded. Reached for a jar of ground spice — dark red, coarse — and added a measure with the particular precision of someone who'd done this exact movement a thousand times and still cared about getting it right.

"The market in Darachet is better than the one near the southern route," he said. "Better produce, better spices, and the woman at the grain stall doesn't —"

The door opened and Jorath came in already talking.

"Transport is booked. Commercial carrier, Darachet-Velanthra route, third departure. I talked to the routing clerk about the deployment discount —"

"— try to sell you a premium on standard weight," Sorvyn continued, to nobody. "Honest scales."

"— and he said fourteen percent but only if we're listed as a four-person unit with combined equipment tonnage, so I need the weight on Vaelen's instrument case because I estimated but if the actual is higher we drop to twelve —"

"Rare quality, honest scales. I was saying."

"— and the seating allocation is open on the upper deck, which means we can get the windward rail if we board early, and the windward rail has the better —"

"Jorath." Sorvyn had set down his spoon. "I am talking about the grain merchant."

"I heard you. Honest scales. Very good. The windward rail —"

"I wasn't finished."

"You were finished. You said 'rare quality' and 'honest scales.' That's a complete thought. The transport leaves at second bell and if we don't pre-file the equipment manifest —"

Sorvyn picked up his spoon and turned back to the pot. "I'm not discussing the transport."

"You don't have to discuss it. You have to be on it."

"I am aware."

Jorath sat down and took a piece of the dark bread from the table without asking. Three kinds were laid out — one dark, one pale, one studded with seeds. There was a drying rack beside the basin with three plates on it — two ceramic, mismatched, and a third that was larger, darker, a different set entirely. Jorath reached for the seed bread. "What are these?"

"Don't eat those."

"I'm eating them."

"They're for the flatbread I'm making tomorrow. Jorath. Put it down."

Jorath put the seed bread down and took the dark bread instead. He chewed with the efficiency of a man who regarded meals as intervals between problems. "Sorvyn's going to hate the flight," he said to Vaelen.

From behind the pot: "I already hate the flight."

"The flight is six hours. Maybe seven if the winds are wrong."

"Every flight is too long. The previous one was too long. The one before that was too long. I have been on thirty-seven commercial flights and I have hated thirty-seven of them and the number is about to be thirty-eight."

"You could walk."

"I'd walk to Velanthra if the world were smaller." Sorvyn said it once, without emphasis, and meant it completely. "I'd walk everywhere. Different ground, different air, different food. That's worth the travel. It's the travelling I can't stand."

The apartment was compact — one main room with the kitchen, a sleeping alcove behind a half-wall, a reading chair by the window that had a blanket folded precisely on its arm. The kitchen was the real room. Mismatched ceramic bowls collected from different markets across different deployments. A knife with a worn handle on a wall hook. A cutting board with knife-marks so deep they'd become part of the grain. Every object used, maintained, kept.

"Is Caeleth eating with us?" Sorvyn asked.

Vaelen thought of the boy at the mess hall table. The old woman jerking her chin.

"He's at the compound mess."

"Alone?"

"He needs to learn the compound."

Sorvyn said nothing. He served the tuber onto three plates — split it open, the flesh golden-brown where it met the pot and pale where it hadn't, the steam carrying the rosemary and the rendered fat and the years of seasoning that lived in the clay. He carved it with the precision of someone who knew exactly how a tuber should come apart. The slices fell clean.

The first bite was extraordinary. The skin had crisped where the pot's heat was strongest — a crackle that gave way to something dense and smooth underneath, the sweetness of slow roasting married to the salt of the fat and the warmth of the spice. The rosemary wasn't a flavor so much as an atmosphere — it lived in the steam, in the back of the throat, in the way the breath carried it after swallowing. Vaelen tore the pale bread and used it to soak the juices pooling on the plate. The bread was better than the market bread — lighter, with a chew that held the liquid without dissolving.

"This is the best thing you've made," Vaelen said.

"This is adequate. The best thing I made was the lamb shoulder in Vethrane, and you weren't there because you'd taken the coast deployment." Sorvyn ate with the unhurried method of a man who had cooked the food and knew exactly what each bite would be. "Jorath was there. Tell him about the lamb."

"The lamb was good." Jorath was already on his second piece of bread. "The sauce was better. Something with citrus."

"Pressed bitter-rind and stock. Twenty hours of reduction." Sorvyn said this with more warmth than he said most things. The lamb shoulder in Vethrane existed in his memory as a fixed point — the meal against which other meals were measured and found wanting.

Jorath reached for the seed bread again.

"Jorath."

"You have more seeds."

"I have exactly the amount of seeds I need for tomorrow's flatbread, and if you eat them now I have to go back to the market, and if I go back to the market I'm buying more spice, and if I'm buying more spice you're paying for it because you ate the seeds."

"How much is the spice?"

"More than the seeds."

Jorath put the bread down. Picked up the dark bread instead. Calculated something silently — the cost of the spice against the value of the flatbread against the indignity of compound travel rations. Vaelen could see the arithmetic happening behind his eyes.

Vaelen ate and listened to them settle it — Sorvyn's food against Jorath's budget, quality against economy. Neither won. Neither lost. The friction between them was worn smooth from years of use, and the smoothness was its own kind of warmth.

When the plates were empty and Sorvyn was wrapping a portion in cloth — for Caeleth, without asking, without commenting on Vaelen's decision not to invite him — Vaelen stood.

"I'll take it to him."

Sorvyn looked up. His pale eyes held Vaelen's for a moment. Then he handed over the bundle.

"The bread too," he said. "The boy doesn't eat enough."

---

The compound was quieter in the evening. Vaelen walked the main courtyard after delivering Sorvyn's food to Caeleth's lodging in the lower city — the boy's face when he opened the door and saw the wrapped plate, his hands reaching for it before his mouth could form the question. Vaelen hadn't stayed. He'd said "Sorvyn" and left, and the weight of the word — the whole weight of it, the pots and the precision and the silent correction — had been enough.

The evening air was cool. The compound walls blocked the wind but not the sky — enormous here, spread above the hillside, the stars sharp. Below, Darachet spread in the dark — lights in windows, the faint movement of transport creatures on the low routes, the amber glow of the market district where the last vendors were closing. The dispatch roost was quiet now, the perches occupied, the handlers gone home. One creature shifted on its perch — a soft sound of talons adjusting on wood — and then stillness.

Essren's map was in his head. The red threads clustered on the frontier. The same quality in two independent readings from two different regions. The silence she'd held — weighted, unblinking, the instruction she hadn't needed to speak. *Trust it.* And his own answer, honest and insufficient: *I'll think about it.*

He'd been thinking about it since Torven. The silence in the trunk-level pauses. The weight that had no name. The way his gift had reached into the gap and found something his training couldn't hold.

Tomorrow they'd fly to Velanthra. The city waited with its interrupted network and its standard deployment parameters.

He walked back through the quiet compound and down the hill to his own apartment and didn't sleep well.

*End of Chapter One*
