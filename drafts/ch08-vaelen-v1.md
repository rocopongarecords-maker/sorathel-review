# Episode Two — Chapter Eight
## The Voyage
*Vaelen*

*Version 1 — First Draft*
*Created: 2026-03-19*

---

The docking platform smelled of creature and salt wind and the particular sweetness of bonding oil applied in quantity.

Vaelen arrived with his team at second bell, the morning still grey and the air carrying the dampness of a city built on a hillside where the weather came up from below. The platform occupied a wide stone terrace at the western edge of Darachet — quarry stone, the same warm-toned variety as the Solvarei compound, but weathered darker here from years of creature waste and the oils that handlers rubbed into the landing surfaces to keep the stone from cracking under repeated impact. Iron mooring posts stood at intervals along the edge, bolted through the stone with fastenings thick enough to anchor something enormous. The chains that hung from them were heavy, dark, warm to the touch — residual heat from the creatures that had been tethered there overnight.

Three carriers were staged on the platform. Two were loading — handlers moving between the creatures and the cargo holds with the practiced economy of people who had done this before dawn and would do it again before dark. The third carrier stood at the far end, already loaded, its handler sitting on the forward ridgeline with her hands flat on the creature's neck, reading something Vaelen couldn't see.

The creature was vast.

He'd seen commercial carriers before — thirty-seven flights in twelve years, the number lived in his body more than his memory — but the size still worked on him. Forty feet at the shoulder, at least, with a body that was more hull than animal: broad, deep-chested, the skin dark and ridged in overlapping plates that had the texture of old bark. The deck structure grew from its back — not built, grown, the wood-and-bone framework that handlers cultivated over years of bonding until the creature's body produced the platform as naturally as it produced skin. Railings curved along the edges, smooth, polished by hands and weather. Seating benches lined the interior — rootwood, warm, shaped to the body. A cargo hold below, accessible through hatches in the deck that sealed with organic closures, the creature's own tissue forming the gaskets.

The wings were the thing. Four sets, mounted in pairs along the creature's flanks — not feathered, not membraned, but plated, each wing a broad articulated surface of overlapping bone-scales that caught the light in shifting patterns. They beat in sequence rather than unison — when the forward pair rose, the second was already falling, and the third rising behind it, a rolling rhythm that ran down the creature's body and produced a continuous undulating lift. The motion was nothing like a bird's. It was smoother, stranger — each pair rising and falling in its turn, the plates adjusting their angle in constant micro-corrections, the whole system generating flight the way a river generates current. Not effort. Accumulation. Generations of selective bonding had shaped the rhythm, but the rhythm itself was the creature's. The difference between built and grown was that the grown thing breathed.

"Upper deck, windward rail, forward section," Jorath said. He'd arrived at the platform before anyone else and had already spoken to the boarding clerk — a thin man with ink-stained fingers who was checking names against a manifest carved into a wooden tablet. "The firm's paying for this. Full deployment rate. I intend to get every mark's worth."

"You've been here since dawn," Sorvyn said. It wasn't a question.

"I've been here since the routing clerk opened. There's a difference. Dawn is when normal people arrive. The routing clerk opens a quarter-bell before dawn, and the quarter-bell is when the good seats go."

Sorvyn looked at the carrier. His jaw was already set — the muscles along his temple visible, his hands at his sides but not relaxed. His pale eyes moved across the creature's body and the deck and the handlers and the chains and found nothing in any of it that made the next six hours acceptable.

"Thirty-eight," he said.

"What?"

"Thirty-eight flights. This will be thirty-eight."

Jorath was already walking toward the boarding ramp. "And you've survived all of them. The statistics are in your favor."

"Statistics aren't in anyone's favor at a thousand feet."

Vaelen watched them go — Jorath moving with the particular energy of a man about to enjoy something he'd optimized, Sorvyn moving with the particular dread of a man who couldn't optimize the sky. Caeleth was beside him, the field journal tucked under one arm, his face tilted up toward the carrier with an expression Vaelen recognized from the node at Torven — the look of someone whose senses were already reaching for something his mind hadn't named.

"First flight?" Vaelen asked.

"Second. The first was the academy transfer. But that was a smaller creature. Regional."

"This is different."

Caeleth nodded. His eyes hadn't left the carrier. His breathing had deepened — the same slow rhythm Vaelen had seen at the transport hub, when the boy's body had responded to the creatures before his words could. Up close, on the platform, the carrier's presence was enormous — not just size but density, the weight of a living thing that had been bonded to routes and handlers for decades, its connection to the network so deep that it hummed through the stone underfoot. Vaelen could feel it — faintly, from this distance, through the platform, the creature's bond resonating in the root network that ran beneath the city. It was like standing near a furnace with the door closed. You couldn't see the fire but the heat reached you.

"It's louder up close," Caeleth said quietly.

"The creature?"

"Everything."

---

They boarded through a ramp that extended from the creature's flank — a ridge of bone and hardened tissue that the handlers unfolded into a gangway, its surface warm and slightly yielding underfoot. The interior of the deck opened around them as they climbed: wider than Vaelen expected, the ceiling low but not cramped, the rootwood benches arranged in rows with a central aisle. Light came through openings in the creature's sides — not windows but translucent patches in the hull where the skin thinned, letting in a diffused amber glow that made the interior feel not quite built — grown, inhabited, the inside of something alive. Because it was.

The deck smelled of wood oil and warm hide and the faint mineral undertone of a creature connected to the network. The benches had the patina of years — smooth, dark at the armrests, lighter where backs pressed, each one shaped slightly differently because the creature's growth was organic, not uniform. No two seats were identical. The deck shifted under their feet — not motion, breath. The slow expansion and contraction of a living hull, subtle enough that you stopped noticing after a minute but present enough that the first step always reminded you: you were standing on something that was alive and choosing to carry you.

Jorath had secured the windward rail seats — forward section, upper deck, where the view was unobstructed and the air came from the direction of travel rather than the creature's wake. He was already inspecting the rail fastenings, his hands moving along the joints where the railing met the deck structure, testing tension, checking the bolts that secured the bench to the creature's back. He'd seen the reversion at the transport hub. He hadn't mentioned it since. But his hands remembered.

Sorvyn sat down heavily and gripped the armrest. His knuckles went white and stayed that way.

"Six hours," he said.

"Maybe seven," Jorath said. "The routing clerk said the western currents are pulling north. The creature will compensate but it adds time."

"Seven hours." Sorvyn closed his eyes. Opened them. Closed them again. "There's a bathhouse in Velanthra. Southern quarter, near the old market. Scalding pools cut into the rock — volcanic runoff, natural heat. A colleague told me about it four years ago and I've been thinking about it since."

"You've been thinking about a bath for four years?" Jorath said.

"I've been thinking about the ground for four years. The bath is a bonus." Sorvyn settled into his seat with the deliberation of a man constructing a position he intended to hold for the duration. His boots were planted flat. His hands held the armrests. His jaw was set against what was coming, and what was coming was the sky.

Vaelen sat beside him. The creature's breath moved through the bench — a pulse, deep and slow, the rhythm of something enormous at rest. Other passengers were boarding: a merchant with crates of sealed bottles, directing two porters with the impatience of a man whose schedule was already behind. A family — mother, father, two children, luggage that was too much for a visit. The mother's face had the particular composure of someone who was being practical about something that hurt. The father carried a trunk on his shoulder and a smaller case in his free hand and didn't look back at the city. The children were young enough to be excited. The smaller one pressed her face to the hull's translucent patches, trying to see out.

A group of students, laughing. A regional official in good clothes reading a folded document. Two handlers who weren't working this route but were riding to Velanthra for reassignment — Vaelen could tell by their posture, the way they sat without checking the rigging, the ease of people who trusted the creature because they knew creatures.

And Drenna.

She came up the ramp after the students, her hair pulled back, her field jacket over a civilian shirt that was better cut than the one she'd worn on deployment. Not expensive — just right. The kind of clothes you wore when you were going somewhere that noticed clothes. Her wrist still carried the frontier threadwork band. She hadn't taken it off.

She found them. Smiled — the real one, the one that started in her eyes before her mouth caught up. Then she looked at her boarding token and at the section marked on the deck, and her section was two rows forward and across the aisle, in seats that were slightly wider and closer to the forward rail where the ride was smoothest.

"Better seat," Jorath observed.

"Maarthen arranged it." She said the name easily — a name she'd been saying for a while now, a relationship that had its own weight and rhythm. "He has an account with the carrier guild. Apparently if you ship enough cargo on a route, the seats come with it."

"That's a trade route patron discount. Those are worth eight to twelve marks per flight depending on the carrier class." Jorath's voice held the specific admiration of a man who respected a good deal regardless of who made it. "What does he ship?"

"Everything. Mirathyn, rootwood, mineral stock, textiles from the eastern valleys. He has routes I haven't even heard of."

Vaelen listened. The name — Maarthen — sat in his ears with the particular weight of something he'd heard before and hadn't asked about. Drenna had mentioned Maarthen's dinner in passing, the night before their departure. A good evening, she'd said. Interesting people. She hadn't said the Commander's name. She hadn't described the room or the food or the conversation. She'd said *interesting people* and left it, and Vaelen had heard the door close on whatever she wasn't sharing and had smiled and said something warm and supportive and meant it, mostly.

"You'll be in the upper city?" he said.

"Maarthen has contacts in the trade district. He wants me to meet some people — merchants, a guild representative. He thinks my frontier experience is useful context for them." She said it carefully. Selecting. Choosing which version of the truth fit this audience.

"That's good," Vaelen said. "You should. The frontier perspective is valuable at that level."

The words were right. The timing was right. The warmth was real — he did think it was good, he did think her experience mattered, he did believe that the world she was walking into needed people who'd stood in the dirt. All of that was true.

What was also true, underneath, was a contraction in his chest that he didn't examine. Drenna was sitting at tables. Tables that required a different kind of reaching — the naked kind, the ambitious kind, the kind where you announced yourself and asked for things and let people see you wanting. Vaelen didn't reach. Vaelen was reached for. People came to him, consulted him, deferred to him — the warmth was a gravity that pulled others into his orbit without him ever having to extend his hand. Drenna extended her hand. Drenna pulled back the chair and sat down and said *I'm here* and meant it. Something in that stung, faintly, in a place he didn't have words for.

"Give Maarthen our best," he said. The warmth deployed, exact, landing where it should.

Sorvyn's eyes moved to Vaelen. Then away.

Drenna went to her seat. The frontier band on her wrist caught the amber light from the hull as she turned, and for a moment the threadwork looked like the capillary patterns Vaelen read in the soil — branching, intricate, a map of something that had fed a life.

---

The carrier launched at second bell.

The sensation was not flight — it was detachment. The creature's body tensed beneath them, the plates along its flanks locking tight, the wings extending in a slow articulated unfurl that sent a vibration through the deck. Then the mooring chains released — handlers pulling the pins in sequence, bow to stern — and the creature lifted. Not with effort. With intention. The massive body rising from the platform with a smoothness that made the size feel impossible — something too large for the air simply choosing to occupy it anyway.

The platform dropped away. The city opened below — Darachet spreading across its hillside, the Solvarei compound on the upper slope, the market district in the middle band, the lower city sprawling toward the river. The quarry-stone buildings caught the morning light and held it, and the streets between them were dark lines filled with movement, and then the altitude swallowed the detail and the city became a pattern — a cluster of warm stone against green hills, a node in the network, one point among many.

Sorvyn's hands hadn't moved from the armrests. His breathing was controlled — deliberately controlled, the rhythm of a man managing his body's insistence that the ground was where he belonged. His boots pressed flat against the deck. His eyes were open but fixed on the bench in front of him, not on the view.

"There's a market in Velanthra," he said. His voice was steady. Too steady — the steadiness of deliberate construction. "Southern quarter. Near the bathhouse. A colleague told me they sell a spice blend you can't find anywhere else — ground with river clay and dried in smoke. Changes the base of everything you cook with it."

"You're thinking about spices right now?" Jorath said.

"I'm thinking about the ground. The spices are on the ground. Everything worth thinking about is on the ground."

"We're at four hundred feet."

"Thank you, Jorath. Altitude reports are exactly what I need."

The creature banked gently, adjusting its heading, the windward wings falling a beat behind the others to compensate. The deck shifted — a slow, smooth roll that passengers experienced as a settling, a lean into a turn. Sorvyn's grip tightened. His jaw muscles flexed. But he didn't close his eyes. He sat with it, eyes forward, and endured.

Vaelen had crossed his arms on the rail and was looking out. Below, the landscape was changing — the green of the cultivated valley giving way to wilder terrain as they passed beyond Darachet's immediate hinterland. The network was visible from up here if you knew what to look for. Not as the sensory field he read on the ground — from altitude, the Amarvel was beyond his reach, the connection severed by distance, the roots too deep and the air too high. What remained was the visual evidence: the color of the land. Where the network was strong, the vegetation was dense, dark green, the kind of green that came from soil being fed from below. Where it weakened, the color shifted — paler, thinner, the green giving way to grey-brown at the margins. The transition was gradual. You couldn't see the edge. But the gradient was there, and from this height the gradient told a story that was harder to see from the ground, where you were standing in one color and couldn't compare.

He couldn't read. The absence sat in him — not painful, not frightening, just present. An instrument set down. Hands without their work. Twelve years of reaching into the ground and feeling the world answer, and up here the world was silent. Not hostile silence, not the weighted silence of Torven's gaps. Just distance. The network running beneath them, far below, its rhythms continuing without him.

He watched the landscape and felt empty-handed and thought about Essren's map. The red threads. The frontier readings that were shifting. The pattern she'd shown him — two independent readings, two regions, the same quality in the silence. From up here, the frontier was visible as a thinning — the green giving way to grey at the edges, the grey advancing inward. You could almost measure it. Almost draw the line where the world stopped being fed.

Nobody on the deck commented. The merchant was checking his cargo seals. The official was still reading. The students were passing a flask and laughing. The family sat quietly — the children had stopped pressing against the hull and were sleeping, curled together on the bench, the smaller one's hand gripping her brother's sleeve. The mother looked out at the landscape with an expression that wasn't wonder. Recognition, maybe. Or resignation. She was watching the place she'd come from get smaller and not looking at the place she was going.

---

Two hours into the flight, the deck crew brought food.

A young man with sunburned forearms and a leather apron — one of the creature's regular crew, his hands steady on the deck despite the gentle sway — moved along the aisle with a wooden tray. Sliced cured meat, dark and marbled. Flatbread wrapped in cloth, still warm from whatever heating surface they used below deck. A hard cheese in pale wedges. Dried fruit in a clay bowl. A flask of something clear that smelled faintly of anise.

Jorath accepted his portion with the particular attention of someone evaluating a gift.

"This is good," he said. He was examining the cured meat, turning a slice in his fingers, reading the marbling. "This is frontier-style curing — the slow method. Eighteen days minimum. Possibly twenty." He put it in his mouth and chewed slowly. His eyebrows rose. "The carrier guild feeds better than half the wayhouses I've eaten in."

"The Solvarei is paying for this," Sorvyn said. He hadn't touched his food. His hands were still on the armrests.

"Exactly. Which is why I intend to eat all of it. This is compensation. The firm provides the flight, I provide the appetite. It's a contract."

"You're going to eat a contract."

"I'm going to eat an excellent contract." Jorath reached for the cheese. "The firm doesn't pay for flights often. When they do, I want them to know they're getting their money's worth." He ate a wedge of cheese and nodded at it with something close to approval. "I could source this. Three marks per wheel, maybe four for this grade. The flatbread is commercial-quality but the cheese is artisan. Someone on this crew knows someone."

Sorvyn looked at the food. His jaw worked. Then his hand released the armrest — the left one, slowly, finger by finger — and picked up a piece of flatbread. He didn't eat it. He held it. The bread was warm in his hand and the warmth was the closest thing to ground he could reach.

Then he ate it. One bite. The tension in his face changed — not released, redirected. His jaw was still set but the muscles around his eyes softened.

"The bread is decent," he said.

"It's good bread," Jorath said.

"I said decent. Good bread requires a proper oven. This was heated on a rack, probably over the creature's body heat channeled through the deck structure. Which is resourceful but not baking." He took another bite. "The cheese is better."

Caeleth was at the rail, the food untouched on the bench beside him. His journal was open on his knees, the binding cracked, the pages half-filled with handwriting that was too large and too uneven to be a practitioner's notes. He was writing and looking up and writing again, his eyes moving between the page and the landscape below with the rapid attention of someone trying to catch something before it changed.

Vaelen watched him. The boy's mouth moved slightly as he wrote — forming the words before his hand found them, silent and absorbed. His left hand held the journal's edge against the wind, his right moved the pen, and between the writing and the looking he was entirely absent from the deck, from the crew, from the food. He was somewhere else. Somewhere between the air and the page, in the space where what he saw became what he wrote, and Vaelen wondered — not for the first time — what the world looked like from inside that sensitivity. What the landscape below was doing to a boy whose gift reached for the network even through distance, even through stone and air. Even from up here. Even through distance. The boy's body was listening to something the altitude should have silenced.

"He hasn't eaten," Sorvyn said.

"He's writing."

"He can write and eat." Sorvyn picked up the boy's portion of cured meat and flatbread and carried it to the rail with the unhurried authority of a man who had decided that a young person was going to eat and that was the end of it.

"Your food," he said, setting it on the rail beside Caeleth's journal.

Caeleth looked up. Blinked. Saw the food. Saw Sorvyn. His face did the thing it always did — the wanting-to-belong surfacing before he could manage it, the gratitude too visible, too young, too honest for the social register of the deck.

"Thank you."

"Don't thank me. Eat." Sorvyn turned back toward his seat, and as he passed Vaelen, his eyes paused on Vaelen's face. A beat. Not accusation — measurement. The practitioner who noticed things noticing something specific about the way Vaelen had watched the boy write and hadn't brought him food. Then the look moved on. Sorvyn sat down. Gripped the armrest.

---


In the fourth hour, over open terrain where the landscape had shifted from cultivated green to scrubland — pale grasses, exposed rock, the grey-brown of land that the network touched lightly or not at all. The passengers had settled into the torpor of sustained travel. The merchant was dozing against his crates. The students were quiet. Even Jorath had stopped evaluating the food and was watching the landscape with the rare stillness of a man between problems.

Vaelen felt the change before he saw it. A shift in the creature's body — a tension running through the deck, the rhythm of its breathing accelerating, the plates along its flanks tightening. The handler at the forward ridgeline straightened. Her hands, which had been resting flat on the creature's neck in the easy contact of routine, pressed down. Her body went rigid.

Then the creature banked.

Not the gentle heading correction from earlier — a sharp turn, one wing set dropping, the other rising, the deck tilting enough that the passengers grabbed for rails and each other. Sorvyn's hands locked on both armrests. The merchant's crates slid. A child cried out.

The creature dove.

It was hunting. Something below — a smaller creature, a wild flyer, dark-winged and fast, crossing the scrubland on whatever errand a wild thing ran. The carrier's head tracked it with a precision that didn't belong to a domesticated animal. The dive was controlled but not commanded — the handler was pulling at the neck ridgeline, her voice raised, sharp syllables that Vaelen couldn't make out over the wind. The creature ignored her. Its body had locked into the pursuit with the single-mindedness of something that remembered, in its bones, what it had been before the bonds and the breeding and the generations of partnership had taught it to carry people instead of kill things.

The intercept took four seconds. The carrier caught the wild flyer at the bottom of the dive — one massive plated foot extending, closing, the impact barely a shudder. The smaller creature went still. The carrier's head dipped once, then rose. It didn't eat. It released the thing — the body falling, turning in the air, gone.

The carrier leveled out. The handler was standing now, both hands on the ridgeline, her face white. She said something to her co-handler — a younger man who had braced himself against the forward rail — and the younger man moved aft along the creature's spine, checking the deck structure, speaking to passengers in a voice that was too calm.

"Turbulence," he said. "Just turbulence. The creature corrected for a downdraft."

He was lying. Vaelen could see it in his posture, in the set of his shoulders, in the way his eyes didn't meet anyone's for longer than a breath. The handler at the forward ridgeline had pulled a cloth strip from her belt and was binding it to a dispatch creature perched on a secondary roost near the bow — a message being sent, fast, to whoever needed to know that a commercial carrier had just killed something mid-flight in front of forty passengers.

Jorath was on his feet, his hands on the rail, his eyes on the creature's flank. Not checking the view — checking the hull. The deck structure, the joints, the fastenings. His fingers found a bolt and tested it. Found another. His face was unreadable but his hands were doing what they always did when something broke: assessing what could be fixed.

"Bonded carriers don't do that," he said quietly.

"No," Vaelen said.

"That was predatory behavior. Hunting instinct. Bonded commercial carriers don't hunt. The breeding removes it. Three generations of selective bonding and the prey drive is suppressed."

"Apparently not completely."

Jorath looked at him. The joke hadn't landed. Jorath's face was the face of a man who lived among equipment and rigging and things that worked because people built them right, and had just watched a piece of it forget what it was supposed to be.

Caeleth hadn't moved from the rail. His journal had fallen — closed, wedged between his boots and the deck. His hands were flat on the railing, pressed hard, his fingers white. His breathing was the deep slow rhythm again — the one that matched the creature's, or tried to. His eyes were wide and aimed at nothing in particular, seeing inward. Whatever the creature had felt during the dive — the resurgence of something old and wild, the bond thinning enough for instinct to surface — Caeleth had felt it too. The network that connected the creature to its handler, the same network that ran through the boy's sensitivity, had transmitted the surge. Vaelen had seen Caeleth's reaction to the transport creatures in the yard — the overwhelm, the drawn-toward-and-flinching-from. This was worse. This was a bonded creature's domestication failing in real time, and the boy's gift had felt the bond stretch and thin and the thing underneath push through.

"You all right?" Vaelen said.

Caeleth looked at him. His pupils were dilated and his face had the strange calm of someone whose body was managing more input than his expression could show.

"It wasn't hunting," Caeleth said.

"It looked like hunting."

"It was remembering."

Vaelen didn't know what to do with that. He picked up the boy's journal from the deck — the binding was cracked further from the fall, a page bent at the corner — and handed it back. Caeleth took it with both hands. Held it against his chest. The page he'd been writing on was visible for a moment before he pressed it closed, and Vaelen caught a fragment — not words but a drawing. Lines that branched and connected and spiraled inward, the pattern of a root system or a river delta or a network seen from above. Or a creature's nervous system. Or all of those at once.

---

The last two hours were quiet. The creature flew steady, the handler's hands never leaving its neck. Whatever had surfaced had gone back down, but the handler wasn't trusting it. She rode the creature's ridgeline with the focused attention of someone watching a door she'd thought was locked.

The landscape below shifted as they moved south and west. The scrubland gave way to cultivated land again — greener, richer, the network's influence visible in the density of the vegetation. Rivers appeared as dark threads through the fields. Settlements dotted the terrain — clusters of stone buildings with the warm color of local quarry, each one centered on a node marker that Vaelen couldn't see from altitude but could infer from the radiating pattern of the fields. The world was organized around the network. From up here, it was obvious. The root system determined where people lived, what they grew, how they traveled. Everything depended on the thing beneath the ground. Everything.

The green deepened as they approached Velanthra's basin. The city was visible from twenty minutes out — a spread of stone and movement in a wide river valley, larger than Darachet by a factor Vaelen couldn't estimate from the air. The buildings were different — paler stone, taller structures, the dense vertical growth of a city that had run out of horizontal space and gone upward. A trading hub on the main route. The machinery of commerce visible in the width of the streets, the size of the docking platforms, the creature yards that ringed the city in a wide perimeter.

The upper city was visible as a separate district — higher ground to the north, the buildings spaced wider, the streets lined with what looked from above like trees but might have been anything. That was where Drenna was going. The lower city, denser and louder, was where the client outpost waited.

Vaelen looked toward Drenna's section. She was sitting with her head tilted against the hull, her eyes open, looking at the city below. Her face in profile was sharp — the cheekbones, the line of her jaw, the particular set of her mouth that could be contemplation or calculation and was probably both. She was reading Velanthra before she'd landed. Filing the streets, the districts, the altitude between upper city and lower. Mapping the table before she sat down.

He looked away.

"She's going to be fine," Sorvyn said.

The words were simple. Sorvyn said them once, flat, without emphasis. But his eyes were on Vaelen when he said them, and the precision of the timing — the words arriving at the exact moment Vaelen looked away from Drenna — said something that the words themselves didn't.

Vaelen smiled. "I know."

"You were looking at her the way you look at a reading you can't interpret."

"I was looking at the city."

"You were looking at her." Sorvyn's voice held no challenge. Just the plain fact. The pale eyes steady, reading Vaelen the way Vaelen read the network — not with a gift but with years. "She's doing what she should be doing. The upper city, the connections, the people who can use what she knows. It's the right path for her."

"I know it is."

"Then why does your jaw do that?"

Vaelen's hand went to his face. He stopped it. Too late — the gesture was a tell, and Sorvyn had seen it, and both of them knew it.

"It doesn't do anything," Vaelen said.

"It tightens. Right here." Sorvyn touched his own jaw, the corner where the muscle met the bone. "Half a second. Then you smile. The smile is good — it's warm, it's right. But the half-second before it isn't smiling. The half-second is something else."

Vaelen breathed. "She's sitting at tables I won't sit at. That's all."

"Won't?"

"Can't. Won't. The distinction doesn't matter."

"It matters." Sorvyn looked forward again. The creature was beginning its descent — the handler guiding it into a long slow turn that would bring them around to the Velanthra docking platform from the north. The deck tilted. Sorvyn's grip tightened on the armrests, but his voice stayed level. "You could sit at those tables. You choose not to. She chooses to. That's not the same as can't."

"Leave it, Sorvyn."

Sorvyn left it. His hands gripped the armrests as the creature descended, and his jaw was set against the altitude, and whatever he'd seen in Vaelen's face he filed — without judgment, without urgency, just the careful accumulation of evidence by a man who believed in his own observations absolutely. Until the evidence said otherwise.

---

Velanthra's docking platform was three times the size of Darachet's. Four carriers on the ground, two more circling in a holding pattern above — their wings beating in slow rolling sequences that cast moving shadows across the platform stone. The handlers on the ground were shouting, the mooring crews hauling chains, cargo being offloaded from a carrier that had landed minutes before, the organized chaos of a hub that processed a hundred arrivals a day.

The creature settled onto the platform with a grace that its size denied — the wings slowing, angling, the body lowering itself onto the stone with a controlled descent that ended in a contact so gentle the passengers barely felt it. The mooring crews moved in. Chains secured. The handler at the forward ridgeline leaned forward and pressed her forehead to the creature's neck — a gesture that might have been bonding ritual or gratitude or exhaustion or all three. The creature exhaled. The deck settled. The flight was over.

Passengers stood, gathered belongings, moved toward the ramp. The family with too much luggage was first — the father already at the ramp, the trunk on his shoulder, not looking back. The mother followed with the children, the smaller one gripping her hand and craning to see the other carriers on the platform. The world was new to her. She was still young enough for everything to be an arrival.

Jorath was checking the deck one final time — a habit, not a task, his hands moving along the rail as he walked toward the ramp. He stopped at a fitting where the railing met the deck structure. Frowned. Ran his thumb along the joint. Whatever he found, he filed it — not his creature, not his problem, but a thing that wasn't right, and Jorath noticed things that weren't right automatically and without being able to stop.

"Structural fatigue in the forward rail mount," he said to no one in particular. "Third bolt. The shearing pattern suggests the creature's body is growing unevenly around the hardware. Someone should tell the crew."

"Are you going to tell the crew?"

"I'm going to tell the crew."

Sorvyn stood. The movement was deliberate — his hands releasing the armrests finger by finger, his weight shifting forward, his boots finding the deck with the particular gratitude of a man about to be on the ground. He didn't look at the view. He didn't look at the city. He looked at the ramp, and the ramp went down, and down was where Sorvyn needed to be.

"Ground," he said. Just the word.

They moved toward the ramp. And Drenna was there.

She'd collected her things — a satchel, lighter than the deployment kit, packed for a different kind of work. The frontier band on her wrist. Her hair escaping its tie in the wind. She stood at the top of the ramp with the particular stillness of someone at a junction — a person about to go in a direction that diverged from the people she'd traveled with.

"I'll be at the trade district," she said. "Maarthen's contacts. Three days, maybe four."

"We'll be at the outpost," Vaelen said. "Standard deployment. If you need anything —"

"I'll find you." She smiled. The real one again. Warmth underneath the strategy, the genuine affection for these people who had been her team for five weeks on the frontier and were still, despite everything, the people she trusted most in a city she'd never seen.

"Keep an eye out," she said. "Velanthra is bigger than Darachet. More to read."

"Nothing to read from here. The Amarvel doesn't reach this high."

She tilted her head. "I wasn't talking about the Amarvel."

She walked down the ramp. At the bottom, the platform opened in two directions — south toward the lower city and the client outpost, north toward the upper district where the streets were wider and the stone was paler and the tables were the kind that Drenna was learning to sit at. She turned north without hesitating, and the crowd on the platform closed behind her, and then she was a shape among shapes, her hair dark against the pale stone, the satchel on her shoulder, the frontier band on her wrist catching the afternoon light.

Vaelen watched her go.

"Coming?" Jorath was on the ramp, looking back. Sorvyn was already at the bottom, his boots on the platform stone, his shoulders dropping, the tension releasing from his body in a visible wave — the ground man restored to ground.

Vaelen turned from the railing. The carrier's body rose behind him — massive, warm, breathing, its handler still pressed against its neck. The dispatch creature at the bow was gone — the message sent, wherever it was going, about whatever had happened in the fourth hour over scrubland when a bonded creature remembered what it had been before the bonds.

He walked down the ramp. The platform stone was warm under his boots. Velanthra opened around him — the noise, the scale, the commerce of a city that existed because the network made it possible. The upper city visible on the northern hill. The lower city dense and loud to the south. The team moving together toward the outpost, one member less than they'd arrived with, and the afternoon light catching the place where Drenna had been and finding only the crowd.
