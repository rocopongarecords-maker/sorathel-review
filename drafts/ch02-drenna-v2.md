# Chapter Two
## Drenna

---

She left before dawn.

The wayhouse was quiet. Sorvyn slept in the chair nearest the door — boots on, hat over his face, his body angled toward the entrance the way a man sleeps when he's spent years expecting to be woken badly. Jorath had claimed the corner furthest from the drafty window, his equipment pack arranged within arm's reach, his ledger under his pillow. The young one — Caeleth — lay curled on his side with his field journal clutched against his chest. He'd been awake when she came back in the night. She'd heard him shift at the wayhouse entrance, heard the held breath that meant he'd seen her. She hadn't turned around.

Vaelen's bed was empty. The blanket had been folded. She didn't know where he'd gone.

On the table by the door, a folded note with the Solvarei regional seal. She'd seen it when she'd come in last night — slid under the wayhouse door, addressed to her by name — not the standard dispatch format, because this wasn't a standard placement. She picked it up now, read it again. The assessment body had confirmed her placement. Three weeks from the end of this deployment, she'd report to Veranthal for the regional intake. The appointment she'd been working toward for two years, the one her Solcarin patron had written four letters to secure, reduced to two sentences and an administrative signature.

She put the note in her jacket. She didn't need to read it again. She'd memorized it the first time.

The path east was forty minutes on foot, longer in the dark, but Drenna's feet knew it the way they knew her own name. The dip where the irrigation channel crossed under the road. The place where the silver-bark roots had buckled the ground and you stepped wide or twisted your ankle. She'd walked it at eleven, carrying flour from the communal store because her father couldn't. At fourteen, carrying a letter of recommendation from a man she'd never met, addressed to a school she'd never seen, signed in an ink that smelled like the Hearthlands. Her body carried the map. Her feet didn't need light.

The air was cold. Colder than it should have been for the season. She noticed. Four weeks ago, the water. Six weeks, the fields. Now the temperature. The decline had a sequence — a grammar — and she'd been tracking it across four communities, and the grammar was the same in each. The old people at Torven's table had talked about the quiet — the grandmother's story, the three-year wait, the node that came back on its own. Drenna had listened to that story and wanted to believe it. She also knew that one valley recovering on its own was not the same as what was happening across four communities simultaneously. The grandmother's story was about a wound that healed. This was something else. She didn't have a name for it yet. Nobody did.

---

Her father's settlement was not like Torven's.

Torven's community was old and rooted, built around a strong node, its structures solid and maintained. Her father's was what happened when a node weakened and the people who could leave did. Eight dwellings packed close along a narrow path — not planned, accumulated. Walls of mismatched materials: pale wood on one side, darker salvage on the other, a section of woven Mirathyn stalk that had been repaired so many times the original structure was invisible beneath the patches. Roofing of layered bark and cloth, weighted with stones. The drainage channel that ran between the houses was clogged with silt that nobody had cleared because the people who used to clear it had moved to the Hearthlands or stopped doing things.

There had been twenty families here once. Now there were five, and two of those were single occupants who stayed because staying was cheaper than leaving and they had no one to leave toward.

The node was barely a shimmer. At midday you could see it if you knew where to look — a faint disturbance in the air, like the last heat rising from a fire that had gone out hours ago. The Amarvel here was thin. Had been thin for years. When the Amarvel thinned, bonds weakened. When bonds weakened, people looked for ways to make the connection feel the way it used to. And in settlements like this one, where the dyes came from local plants and the regulations came from a regional office three days' travel away, the ways people found were not the ways the guidelines recommended.

Maret's light was on.

It was always on when Drenna came. Maret was the kind of woman who heard things in her sleep — footsteps on a path, the weight of a particular walk, the difference between a stranger and a girl she'd watched grow up. Drenna didn't knock at her father's door. She shifted the latch — it stuck, it always stuck, the wood had swollen with moisture that nobody had sanded down — and went in.

The dwelling was clean. Maret's work. The fire had been kept, the bedding changed, the plates washed and stacked on a shelf that tilted slightly because one of the wall-pegs had loosened. There was food on the table — a bowl of something cold, a piece of bread with one bite missing. He'd eaten some of it. Not because he wanted it.

He was sitting by the window.

Not looking out. Not looking at anything. His hands were in his lap, palms up, fingers slightly curled. He was forty-seven. His hair had gone entirely gray. She remembered it dark — dark like hers, thick, the kind of hair that held the rain. She remembered him at forty, before the worst of it: the shoulders that could carry a Mirathyn bale without bending, the hands that were always holding something, the laugh that scattered birds from the silver-bark outside this window. He'd started the extract when she was ten. He'd been — she searched for the word and found one she didn't like — restless. Not unhappy exactly. But reaching for something, always reaching, the way his hand still reached now. Other families in the settlement had managed fine. Her father couldn't sit with what he had. A pinch in the evening tea. The extract made him feel the way he thought he was supposed to feel — the warmth coming back, the creature at the door lifting its head, the bond humming. He'd been happy those evenings. She remembered that. For a while that had been enough.

It was never enough.

The amounts grew. The sources shifted — not the regulated powder from the communal stores but the concentrated brew that came from men who traveled between settlements with clay jugs and no names. The warmth stopped being warmth and became something hungrier. By the time she was twelve the man who could carry a Mirathyn bale was sitting in this chair most of the day, and by the time she was fourteen the creature at the door had stopped lifting its head when he moved, because the bond between them — the thing the extract was supposed to restore — had been burned through like a rope held too close to a fire. Not weakened. Not frayed. Destroyed. The creature had gone dull — its coat flat, its plating colorless, the intelligent amber in its eyes faded to something clouded and empty, like windows in a house where no one lives.

The creature was outside. She'd passed it on the path. It lay in the flattened grass beside the house, where it had lain every day since the bond broke — not waiting, not the way Hathren's Korveth waited with that intelligent patience. This creature lay there because it had nowhere else to go. Its amber eyes tracked her as she passed, and they tracked her without lifting, without focus — flat amber, like stones at the bottom of still water.

Drenna set down her pack. Dried fish. Root-flour that Maret could bake. A shirt, because his old ones hung off him now. She unpacked with quick hands, moving around the dwelling the way she'd moved through it since she was twelve — the year she'd understood, with the clarity of a child who sees the world without the cushion of adult excuses, that her father was gone. Not dead. Not leaving. Gone the way a fire is gone when the last coal dims and the ash is still warm but there's nothing left to burn.

She stacked the flour. She folded the shirt. She sat across from him.

His eyes were open. They received light the way a window receives light.

"The node at Torven's community is stressed," she said. She talked to him. She always talked to him. The alternative was the silence, and the silence in this room had a weight that pressed against her skin. "Eighteen months of decline. The eastern fields are gone. The water's flat — you'd have known that before anyone. You always knew." She paused. "They sent a team. The pattern reader is good. Best I've seen."

His hand moved.

The fingers of his right hand uncurled slightly — reaching for something on the table that wasn't there. A cup. A spoon. The small clay vessel he'd once used for the evening dose, before the doses stopped being evening and became all day and became everything. The movement lasted less than a second. Then the hand settled back, palm up, fingers curling to their resting position.

Drenna stared at the hand. She thought: *even now. Even after all of it.* The body reaching for the thing that had emptied it. She'd stopped being angry about it years ago. What replaced the anger wasn't cold — it was clear. The way water is clear when you stop stirring it.

And sitting here, watching his fingers settle, her mind did the thing it always did — the thing she couldn't stop and had stopped trying to. She looked at her father's hand and she saw Torven's community. The same reaching. The same quiet. A man who poisoned himself with concentrated extract and a settlement being drained by something in the Amarvel itself, and both of them arriving at the same emptiness. She didn't know yet what to do with that. But she knew it was the kind of observation that nobody in the regional offices was making, because nobody in the regional offices sat in rooms like this.

She touched his shoulder. The bone was too close to the surface. She left her hand there for three breaths — she counted — and then she picked up her empty pack and walked out.

Maret was on her porch. Arms crossed. She was perhaps fifty, thick through the shoulders, her face set in the way of someone who'd been getting up before dawn for decades and resented the dawn for not meeting her halfway. She held a cup of something hot. She did not offer Drenna a cup.

"He ate yesterday," Maret said. "Most of the bowl."

"Thank you."

"The new shirt won't fit. He's smaller than last time."

Drenna said nothing. There was nothing to say to a fact.

Maret drank from her cup. She looked at Drenna the way you look at weather — assessing, without expectation. "The deployment is always close," she said. Not accusation. Just weather too. The kind of observation that had been true so many times it had worn smooth of meaning.

Drenna held the words. She didn't answer, because the answer was yes, and Maret already knew it, and saying it aloud would make it weigh more than Drenna could carry back down the path.

Maret went inside. At the door she paused, her back to Drenna, one hand on the frame. She stood like that for a moment. Then the door closed.

Drenna walked back. The forty minutes passed in what felt like ten. She was thinking about the hand. The reach that wasn't a reach. She was thinking about what Torven's community looked like — the water going flat, the colors fading, the people growing quiet — and how the signs were the same signs she'd grown up watching in her father. The same emptying. The same slow departure of the thing that made a person present. But her father had done it to himself. She knew that. Other families had sat with the same thin bond, the same quiet evenings, and hadn't reached for the clay jug. He had. She didn't like thinking about why — about what had been missing in him that wasn't missing in them — so she didn't. She'd stopped pulling at that thread years ago.

What was happening at Torven's community was different. Nobody there was brewing concentrated Mirathyn. The water had gone flat on its own. The fields had stopped producing on its own. The children had stopped playing on their own. Something was draining the life from the settlement and it wasn't a substance — it was something in the Amarvel itself, something the instruments couldn't see, something that looked, from the outside, exactly like what had happened to her father but came from a place she couldn't name.

The cold pressed through her jacket. She walked faster.

---

The team was awake when she returned. Breakfast was happening at the communal table — boiled grain, dried fruit, a tea brewed from something local that tasted like warm earth. The wayhouse had its own small kitchen but Torven had insisted. Frontier hospitality. You fed the people who came to help, even when the help was uncertain and the food was adequate and the water you served them tasted like nothing, and nobody mentioned the water because mentioning it made it real.

Drenna took a bowl and sat at the end of the table and ate the way she did everything — quickly, efficiently, without lingering.

Jorath was at the center of a small commotion. He was crouched beside a boy of perhaps twelve — one of Torven's grandchildren — who had a creature beside him on the ground. Not a bonded creature like Korveth — smaller, scruffier, the kind of animal that attached itself to a household and stayed. The boy had built a harness for it out of rope and scrap leather and rigged a small cart behind it — planks, rough-cut wheels, the kind of thing a child builds by watching adults and guessing at the engineering. One wheel sat crooked. The harness dug into the creature's shoulder where the weight distributed wrong.

Jorath had the harness apart in his hands.

"Your hitch point's too high," he said. He was turning the leather over, studying where the stitching had bunched. "You watched Hathren's rig on Korveth, yeah? The weight sits here —" he touched the creature's back, just behind the plating where the shoulders widened — "not here." He tapped the spot where the boy's harness had been riding. "Move the anchor down two fingers' width and the pull comes from the chest, not the neck. The creature does half the work instead of fighting you."

"I don't have better leather."

"You don't need better leather. You need the same leather in the right place." Jorath was already re-threading the harness, his thick fingers working with a precision that didn't match his hands. "And this wheel — you've got the axle seated off-center. See that wobble? Bore a new hole here. One finger to the left. The whole cart tracks straight."

The boy was grinning. Not at the cart — at Jorath. At the absolute certainty radiating from a man who hadn't been asked to fix this and couldn't stop himself.

Drenna watched this and felt something she hadn't expected: envy. Not of Jorath — of the ease. The willingness to take a problem that wasn't his and wrestle it to the ground for no reason except that it was there. She would never do that. She didn't know if that was strength or something she'd lost so long ago she'd forgotten it was supposed to be there.

Vaelen was at the node.

She could see him from the table — two hundred paces away, standing at the center of the shimmer with his eyes closed and his hands at his sides. He'd been there since before she left — "First light," Sorvyn had said when she'd asked, in the clipped way of a man who considers unnecessary words a waste of everyone's time. The elder had brought him tea an hour ago. It sat on a rock beside him, untouched, going cold.

She'd confirmed something about him yesterday in the eastern fields — three hours walking the dead Mirathyn rows, watching him work. His hands moving through the air, reading something invisible with a sensitivity that made her own training feel blunt. At one point he'd stopped in the middle of a dead field, turned ninety degrees, and said, "The failure started here. This exact spot. Fourteen months ago." He'd pointed at a patch of dirt that looked identical to every other patch. When she checked the community's planting records later, he was right.

She'd asked him how he knew. "The Amarvel remembers," he'd said. "It's like reading a scar. The tissue tells you when the wound happened, how deep it went, which direction the blade was moving." He'd said this while walking, the way someone describes a route they've taken a hundred times.

It was maddening. She'd spent months walking communities, talking to families, cross-referencing what she saw on the ground with the data the specialists kept citing. He could stand in one field and see in three seconds what she'd pieced together from hundreds of conversations. And then he'd moved on to the next row. Brushed the dirt from his hands. The gift didn't stagger him — he carried it the way someone carries a face they've always had, without knowing what it looked like from the outside. She knew what it looked like. She'd grown up in a settlement where someone who could read the Amarvel that clearly could have seen what was happening before the quiet started, before the extract, before her father ended up in the chair. And here was Vaelen, walking dead fields with that impossible sensitivity, and when he finished what he felt wasn't urgency — it was satisfaction at work done well. She wanted to grab him by the shoulders. Not out of anger. Out of something closer to grief — the distance between what he had and what he knew about having it.

She thought about her Solcarin — the cold hallways, the students who'd grown up knowing which fork to use and which names to mention and how to hold their bodies in rooms where decisions were made. She'd arrived with dirt under her fingernails and an accent that marked her before she opened her mouth. She'd left with top marks and a voice that could move through any room without snagging on anything. She'd also left knowing something the other students didn't: that the rooms where decisions were made were just rooms. The people in them were just people. They weren't smarter than her — most of them were considerably less smart than her — they'd simply been born closer to the chairs. That could be changed. She intended to change it.

She also, sometimes — late at night, in the dark — missed the girl with the dirt under her fingernails. That girl hadn't known how to read a room. She'd known how to read the ground. The ground didn't lie to you. Rooms did. But rooms were where the power lived, and power was the only thing that had ever changed anything for settlements like her father's. Compassion without power was just a nicer way of watching people disappear.

Vaelen opened his eyes.

She saw it from two hundred paces — the shift, the return. He stood still for another moment, then bent and picked up the cold tea and drank it in one long swallow. He turned toward the communal table, and his stride had changed — longer, purposeful, a man who'd found something and needed to talk about it. Even from here she could see his left hand. It was closed.

She put down her bowl and went to meet him.

---

They walked the western fields together, and Drenna showed him what the specialists never saw.

"Torven's granddaughter used to play here," she said. They were standing at the edge of a field that was still producing — the Mirathyn stalks upright, the leaves green, the root structures intact. On paper, this field was healthy. "Every afternoon. She'd run between the rows and the stalks would lean toward her. Mirathyn does that — the living stalks respond to movement, to warmth. Children especially. The stalks lean."

Vaelen looked at the field. "They're not leaning."

"There are no children in this field. The girl stopped coming. Her parents didn't stop her — she stopped herself. She told her grandmother the field felt wrong." Drenna looked at him. "She's seven. The field reads as healthy in every assessment. The output is down eight percent, which is within seasonal variance. The specialist who came last year walked this row and marked it stable."

"The specialist wasn't barefoot and seven years old."

"No. The specialist had instruments. The girl has her feet in the dirt and a connection to this land that started before she could walk." She paused. "The instruments are measuring the wrong thing."

Vaelen went quiet. He'd stopped walking. His head had tilted — the reading posture — but this time he wasn't reading the Amarvel directly. He was layering her observation over what he'd sensed, trying to see through a seven-year-old's bare feet what trained practitioners couldn't.

"There's a gap," he said. "Between what the Amarvel reports and what's actually happening."

"Yes."

"The node reads as stressed but functional. The soil data shows decline within parameters. The Amarvel's own diagnostic says this is recoverable. But the water has no taste and the children won't come to the fields and three families have left."

"Yes."

"The Amarvel's own readings don't match what's happening to the people."

Drenna felt something loosen in her chest. Not surprise — she'd known this for weeks. The relief of someone else saying it. "The Amarvel measures itself and finds itself adequate. And everyone who reads those measurements agrees, because the measurements are correct. They're just not measuring what's actually failing."

Vaelen looked at her. Not the warm version. The other one — focused, stripped down, taking a problem seriously enough to put everything else aside.

"What do you think is happening?" he asked.

She'd prepared for this question. But what came out wasn't the prepared version. What came out was: "My father started on the Mirathyn extract when I was ten. He was —" She paused. "He wasn't managing well. Other families were fine — same settlement, same conditions. He couldn't settle. The extract made him feel connected again. It brought the bond back, or something that felt like the bond. For a while." She kept her voice level. This was evidence, not confession. "The amounts grew. The sources got worse. By the time I was twelve he was in the chair. By fourteen the bond was gone — not weakened, burned through. The creature stopped responding — wouldn't lift its head, wouldn't look at him. My father stopped speaking."

She didn't pause for sympathy. She didn't want it. She also knew — in the part of her mind that was always running, always mapping the shape of a conversation — that this was the moment Vaelen would stop seeing her as a competent rotation practitioner and start seeing her as someone who understood the frontier from inside its wounds. That mattered. Not because she was putting on a show. The pain was real. But pain that served no purpose was just pain, and Drenna had stopped believing in purposeless suffering the year she'd left her father's settlement with a scholarship letter and hadn't looked back.

"The signs were the same. The quiet. The emptying. A person going away while the body stays. And here's what I can't stop thinking about — what's happening in this community looks exactly like what happened to my father. The same absence. The same fading. But nobody here is using concentrated Mirathyn. The water went flat on its own. The fields stopped on their own. The children pulled away on their own." She held his gaze. "Two different causes. The same result. And nobody is asking why the Amarvel produces the same kind of death whether a man poisons himself with extract or a community just... goes quiet."

Vaelen didn't say he was sorry. He didn't offer comfort. He stood in a field that should have been leaning toward them and wasn't, and he held what she'd given him without flinching.

Then he said: "Show me the other communities."

"What?"

"You've been tracking this. The patterns, the similarities across different sites. I can feel what's wrong with the Amarvel here but I can't see the bigger picture — the connections between communities. You can. Show me."

She opened her journal. Her hands were steady. They were always steady.

She showed him everything.

---

The team ate at Torven's table that night. Jorath had conscripted the boy into his drainage project — they'd spent the afternoon hauling stone from the collapsed south wall, and the boy's hands were scraped raw and his face was lit with the particular satisfaction of someone who'd built something that would still be there tomorrow. Jorath had dirt on his knees for the first time since Drenna had known him. He was eating with the appetite of a man who'd used his body instead of his ledger, and he was explaining to the daughter — who was no longer arguing, who was watching the new channel with something approaching respect — exactly how to maintain the catch basin through the wet season.

Drenna ate quickly and went back to the wayhouse alone.

She lay on her bed. The wayhouse was cold — the hearth had been temperamental all evening, and the wind off the hills found every gap in the shutters. She pulled the blanket to her chin and stared at the ceiling, which was close and dark and stained from years of bad ventilation.

She could hear Jorath's voice carrying from the communal table. She could hear Sorvyn's silence, which was somehow louder. She could hear the settlement beneath all of it — the quiet that lived under the sounds of people eating and talking. The same quiet that lived in her father's house. Different source. Same shape.

Four communities. The same sequence in each. Water first. Then fields. Then the quiet — the people fading, the settlements emptying from the edges inward. And in every case, the help arriving after the community had already learned to live with the decline. Readings confirming the instruments. Instruments confirming the readings. The numbers closing a loop that left the people outside it.

Vaelen had used the word "gap." He was right. That was the center of it — the gap between what the Amarvel reported and what was happening to the people inside it. He'd asked to see her observations. He'd asked the right questions. He was the first person who had.

She thought about the note in her jacket. The assessment body at Veranthal. Three weeks after this deployment ended, she'd be in a room where the reports from deployments like this one were reviewed, filed, and acted on — or not acted on, depending on who was reading them and what else was on the table. She'd be the person with field hours and data and a Solcarin credential and a frontier accent she could turn on or off depending on what the room needed. She'd be the youngest person at the intake. She intended to be the most prepared.

She thought about her father's hand. The reach for the clay vessel that wasn't there. Even now. Even after everything. The body still wanting the thing that had destroyed it.

She thought about the girl who stopped playing in the field because the field felt wrong.

She thought about Torven's grandmother, who'd said the quiet lasted three years in one valley and came back on its own. And about Torven, who'd looked at his hands and said *this feels different* and hadn't been able to finish the sentence. The old stories were about wounds that healed. A node failed, you waited, it recovered. But this wasn't one node. This was four communities, maybe more, and the old stories didn't account for what she was seeing — the same shape of absence appearing everywhere at once, produced by something nobody had a name for yet.

She would find the name. She'd find it the way she found everything — by being there, by looking, by refusing to accept the version of events that the numbers told when the ground told a different story. And when she had it, she would carry it into the rooms where the decisions were made, and she would be the one speaking, not the one filing a report that would be received and acknowledged and forgotten. She hadn't come this far to be acknowledged. She'd come this far to be the person whose voice couldn't be ignored.

She'd known the shape of this since she was twelve years old, sitting in a dwelling with a father who was disappearing and a world that wasn't coming to save him:

*Nobody is coming. If we are going to be saved, it will have to be us.*

She closed her eyes. Sleep came slowly. When it came, she dreamed of Mirathyn stalks leaning toward a child who was no longer there.
