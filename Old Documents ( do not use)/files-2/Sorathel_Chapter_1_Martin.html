Sorathel
Chapter One
Caeleth
First Draft — Martin Version

The runner hit a root and the world went sideways.

Caeleth's shoulder struck the railing hard enough to send a bright shock up his neck and into his jaw, and for one terrible instant he was certain the whole carriage was going over. His field journal — three weeks of careful notes, a gift from his mother, the binding still stiff because he hadn't filled enough pages to break it in — slid off his knee and disappeared under the bench where Sorvyn was sleeping. Or pretending to sleep. Caeleth had not yet determined which. The man had been motionless since the second hour, his hat tipped over a face that had more creases than a person should earn honestly, his broad shoulders settled into the bench like stone that had found its final resting place. He breathed the way old walls breathe — slowly, with the certainty of things that have stopped needing to prove they're still standing.

"Grab something," Jorath said.

He didn't look up. He never looked up. Jorath had his coins arranged in rows along his thigh — twelve silver, four copper, and a single tarnished half-mark with the face of a Hearthlands governor whom Caeleth didn't recognize — and he was holding them steady against the carriage's shaking with three fingers, the way a musician holds strings. His other hand was making small notes in a ledger balanced on his knee, figures so small and precise that Caeleth couldn't read them from across the carriage. He was compact, Jorath. Sharp-jawed. Narrow in the shoulders and quick in the hands. Everything about him suggested a man who had learned early that the world would not give him anything he didn't count, record, and verify for himself.

"Hathren takes the western ridge at speed," Jorath said. "Every time. You'd think a man whose livelihood depends on delivering practitioners alive would care about the alive part."

Caeleth grabbed the railing. His knuckles were white. He noticed this and tried to relax them and couldn't.

Outside, the landscape blurred. Rolling green hills cut with dark stone outcroppings, stands of silver-barked trees whipping past close enough that their branches scraped the carriage slats with a sound like fingernails on wood. The air that pushed through the gaps was cold and tasted of crushed leaves and something else — something mineral and sharp that settled at the back of the throat like a word he'd forgotten. Through the slats he could see the ground passing beneath them at an angle that felt wrong, tilted thirty degrees from where the ground ought to be, and his stomach lurched with the realization that either the world was slanted or they were, and neither possibility was comforting.

Then the carriage leveled and the speed eased. The trees fell away into open meadow. Caeleth remembered to breathe.

He was three weeks out of the academy. This was his second deployment. At the academy, Instructor Halveth had told them that the mark of a good practitioner was the ability to appear calm in conditions that warranted panic. Caeleth was failing this test comprehensively and could only hope that nobody was grading him.

"First time on Korveth?"

That was Vaelen.

He sat across the carriage with one arm slung over the side and his legs crossed at the ankle, occupying the space the way certain men occupy every space they enter — not with size, though he was tall enough to have to angle his shoulders through standard doorways, but with a quality of ease that made the shaking carriage seem like something that was happening to other people. He was older than Caeleth by perhaps a decade, dark-eyed, with a long face that was handsome the way certain landscapes are handsome — not pretty, not symmetrical, but arranged in a way that made you want to keep looking. There were lines at the corners of his mouth that deepened when he spoke and faint shadows under his eyes that might have been fatigue or might have been permanent. He had a piece of Ashara smokebread in his hand — dark crust, flecked with volcanic salt that glinted in the light — and he'd somehow kept hold of it through the lurch that had sent Caeleth's journal flying and Caeleth's dignity along with it.

His eyes were warm. That was the first thing you noticed about Vaelen, before the height, before the intelligence that moved behind the warmth like a current beneath still water. His eyes said: I see you. You're safe here. I'm not going to make you feel small.

Caeleth had been watching for mockery since he'd been assigned to this team. He was the youngest, the least experienced, the most obviously out of his depth. In every other group he'd been part of — at the academy, in his home settlement, in the brief miserable apprenticeship with the regional assessor who'd told him he lacked instinct — the dynamic had been the same. The established members closed ranks. The new one was tolerated until they proved themselves or didn't. Vaelen's team didn't work that way, and the absence of the expected cruelty made Caeleth more nervous, not less. He kept waiting for the warmth to reveal itself as a performance. It hadn't yet. He didn't know what to do with that.

"Second time on a runner," Caeleth said, and was relieved that his voice came out steady. "But the first was Ylveth. She's... calmer."

"Ylveth is a hundred and forty years old and has the temperament of a grandmother who's seen everything and decided none of it warrants rushing," Vaelen said. "Korveth is nineteen and runs like he's being chased by something only he can see. Different experience entirely."

Jorath made a sound that might have been a laugh. "Korveth runs like that because Hathren lets him. That man has no interest in arriving with passengers who can still walk. The deployment rate for runner transport is one-point-four marks per hour. We've been on this creature for six hours. That's eight-point-four marks billed to the regional office for a journey that should have taken nine hours at standard pace, which means Hathren is saving himself two-point-six marks in creature feed costs by running Korveth at a speed that—"

"Jorath," Vaelen said.

"I'm just observing."

"You're auditing a man who can't hear you while traveling at speed on a creature that could crush all of us by sitting down. Save it for the expense report."

Jorath held a coin up to the light, turning it between his fingers. "The expense report is going to be a work of art."

Vaelen grinned.

Caeleth had seen the grin before — in the briefing room when a logistics officer had asked a stupid question, in the mess hall when Sorvyn had said something so dry it took three seconds to land, on the road when a child in a passing settlement had waved and Vaelen had waved back with a solemnity that made the child shriek with laughter. Each time it transformed him. The sharp angles of his face softened. The intelligence in his eyes became something warmer, something that invited you in. The grin said: we're in on something together, you and I. The supply clerk at the regional depot had walked into a door frame after receiving it. Caeleth had pretended not to see. He understood the impulse.

"Jorath's skeleton has survived worse," Vaelen said. "Remember the Veldryn deployment? The ice shelf?"

"I try not to."

"He fell forty feet into a crevasse and his first words, his very first words at the bottom, were 'does this count as hazard pay.'"

Sorvyn lifted the hat from his face. His eyes were pale and steady, the color of winter sky, and they held the particular patience of a man who had heard every story worth hearing and most that weren't. "His first words were considerably worse than that. You're editing for the new recruit."

"I'm curating," Vaelen said.

Sorvyn looked at Caeleth. There was nothing performative in the look — no wink, no warmth deployed as a tool. Just a plain, honest assessment, the way you'd look at a horse to see if it was sound. Whatever he saw, it apparently satisfied him.

"He's looking out for you," Sorvyn said. "He does that."

He put the hat back over his face. The conversation, as far as Sorvyn was concerned, was finished. But Caeleth had heard something in the old man's voice — a certainty so deep it didn't need emphasis. Not I think he's looking out for you. Not he probably means well. A statement of fact about the world, delivered with the same conviction another man might say the sun rises in the east. Sorvyn believed in Vaelen the way some people believed in the root network — as a given, as a foundation, as something too fundamental to require justification.

Vaelen glanced at Sorvyn's hat. Just for a half-second. Something moved across his face that Caeleth couldn't name — not discomfort, not gratitude, something faster and stranger, like a shadow passing behind a lit window. Then the grin was back, easy and warm.

"Sorvyn thinks I'm better than I am," Vaelen said. "It's his one consistent belief."

"Two consistent beliefs," Sorvyn said from under the hat. "That, and that Jorath will find a way to bill us for this conversation."

"Only if it goes over ten minutes," Jorath said. "I'm reasonable."

Caeleth laughed. It came out louder than he'd intended and he felt his neck flush hot, but nobody looked at him strangely, nobody paused, nobody exchanged the glance that said the new one is trying too hard. The joke simply absorbed him into its warmth, the way a campfire absorbs a person who sits down at its edge. He was included. The inclusion cost nothing and demanded nothing. That was the thing about Vaelen's team — three deployments together, and they had the rhythms of people who'd been sharing meals and silences and fear for years. Sorvyn's faith. Jorath's perpetual accounting. Vaelen's warmth holding it all in orbit. Even the frictions felt old and loved, like furniture worn smooth by use.

Caeleth wanted to belong here with a sharpness that sat in his chest like a stone. He'd felt it before — at the academy, in the first week, watching the older students move through the halls with the easy confidence of people who knew where they were going. He'd spent his whole life watching from the edge of circles he wanted to enter. This circle was warmer than most. That made the wanting worse.

· · ·

The carriage began to slow, and the light changed.

Caeleth noticed the light before he noticed the landscape — a subtle shift in quality, as if someone had placed a thin cloth over the sun. The meadow they were passing through was green, undeniably green, but the green was somehow less than it should have been. He couldn't have explained it to anyone. The grass was the right color. The sky was clear. Nothing was visibly wrong. But the light that fell on the hills ahead had a flatness to it, a quality of arriving from very far away and being tired by the journey.

He wondered if he was imagining it. He said nothing.

Through the slats, the terrain was resolving into something inhabited. Terraced fields climbing low hillsides. Stone walls, old but maintained. Clusters of trees with canopies so dark they looked almost black against the afternoon sky. And structures — pale wood, darker stone, arranged in the organic pattern of a settlement that had grown around a center point rather than being planned. A Mirathyn farming community. Caeleth had grown up near one, though smaller than this, and the sight of the familiar architecture — the long communal tables visible through open doorways, the thread-drying racks along the southern walls — produced a pang of homesickness so specific it surprised him.

The center point was visible from here: a network node. Caeleth had learned to identify them at the academy — the faint shimmer in the air above the place where Amarwen's roots concentrated, a distortion like heat rising from stone. The shimmer was supposed to pulse with a gold-green rhythm that matched the network's breath. This one pulsed. But the rhythm was irregular, the way a sleeping person's breath becomes irregular when their dreams turn bad.

Vaelen had stopped eating.

The smokebread was forgotten in his hand. His eyes had gone to a place Caeleth couldn't follow — distant, unfocused, his head tilting slightly to one side as if listening to a sound at the very edge of hearing. Caeleth had seen this twice before and it still made the hair on his forearms rise. When Vaelen read the network, his face emptied. The warmth, the intelligence, the charisma that made rooms rearrange themselves around him — all of it went away, and what was left was something mechanical and cold, a precision instrument wearing a person's face. It lasted three seconds, perhaps four. Then Vaelen blinked and was Vaelen again, and the transition was so seamless that Caeleth almost doubted what he'd seen.

Almost.

Vaelen looked at the smokebread in his hand with mild surprise and ate the rest of it in two bites. He brushed the dark crumbs from his chest. "The node's stressed but functional," he said. "Soil depletion pattern — probably eighteen months of gradual decline. The Mirathyn crops are pulling more than the network can replenish."

He said it the way someone would say it's going to rain later. Caeleth was still trying to process the fact that Vaelen had just extracted eighteen months of agricultural and network data from a three-second stare at a shimmer in the air two hundred meters away.

"How bad?" Jorath's coins were put away. His ledger was closed. The shift was instant — a different man, alert and professional, the accounting forgotten.

"Not emergency. Not comfortable either. The community will have noticed it in the yields. Smaller harvests. Lower Mirathyn potency. Bond creatures a little more sluggish than usual. They'll have attributed it to weather, or season, or bad luck. People always do."

"And the real cause?"

Vaelen paused. Caeleth was learning to read Vaelen's pauses. The short pause meant he was organizing information. The medium pause meant he was deciding how much to share. This was a medium pause.

"The node's compensating for something downstream," Vaelen said. "Drawing resources from the local network to feed a deficit further along the line. Like a body pulling blood from the extremities to protect the core."

Sorvyn's hat was off. "What's downstream?"

"I don't know yet."

There was a silence after that which Caeleth didn't like. Vaelen always had the next sentence ready. The absence of one felt like a gap in a wall where a stone should be.

The carriage shuddered and dipped. A sharp whistle from above — Hathren, the runner's handler, calling out a command in a low guttural tone that vibrated strangely in the air, more felt than heard. Caeleth felt it in his teeth. The creature beneath them groaned in answer, a sound so deep it traveled up through the carriage floor and into Caeleth's spine, and the descent smoothed from terrifying to merely unpleasant. The ground rushed up. There was a final jolt that knocked Jorath's ledger from his hands — "Three marks for a new binding if that's damaged," Jorath said to nobody — and then they were still.

Vaelen swung over the side and dropped to the ground with the ease of a man stepping off a porch. Caeleth climbed down after him, his legs shaking in a way he hoped nobody noticed, and planted his feet on solid earth that didn't move and didn't tilt and was blessedly, wonderfully flat.

Behind them, Hathren was already descending from the driving perch — a wiry, sun-darkened man with a face like dried fruit and the permanent squint of someone who'd spent decades looking into wind. He moved down the creature's neck with a familiarity that bordered on contempt, as if the massive animal beneath him were a particularly stubborn piece of furniture.

And now Caeleth saw Korveth properly, from the ground, at rest, and the scale of the thing stopped his breath.

The creature was broader than a house. Low-slung, with six legs folded beneath a body plated in something that was neither stone nor bark but shifted between the two depending on how the light struck it — gray-brown, mottled, alive in a way that mineral shouldn't be. Its head was tucked against its chest, each exhalation a slow mist that flattened the grass in a circle wide enough to build a dwelling in. Along its spine, where the carriage had been bolted to a natural ridge of plating, pale lichens grew in spiraling patterns — root network signatures, Caeleth knew from his textbooks, the mark of the bond between creature and handler made visible on the body like a living tattoo. Hathren's bond. Hathren's life, mapped in lichen on the back of something that could have crushed them all by rolling over in its sleep.

Hathren put his hand on the creature's jaw without looking, the way you'd touch a wall you'd leaned against a thousand times. Korveth's eye — one enormous amber disc, flecked with green, more intelligent than any animal eye had a right to be — tracked him with a patience that made Caeleth think of old married couples.

The lichens pulsed. A single wave of gold-green light rippled out from Hathren's hand across the creature's plated flank and faded into the gray afternoon.

"Two hours," Hathren said to Vaelen. "Then he grazes. I'm gone by dusk."

"Understood. Thank you, Hathren."

Hathren grunted and turned back to his creature. It was, Caeleth suspected, the most extensive social interaction the man had engaged in all week.

· · ·

She was already walking toward them when Caeleth turned around.

His first thought was that she was young — younger than him, which startled him, because he was accustomed to being the youngest person in every professional context he entered. She was perhaps twenty, twenty-one at most, with long dark hair pulled back in a way that was practical without being severe, loose strands falling around a face that was — Caeleth noticed and immediately felt foolish for noticing — striking. Wide brown eyes set above sharp cheekbones. A mouth that looked like it was perpetually considering whether to say something cutting. She was small — not short, but built with a compactness that made her movements seem precise, deliberate, as if she'd never in her life taken a step she hadn't thought about first.

She wore Solvarei field markings. New ones — the fabric still stiff at the shoulders, not yet shaped to her body, the insignia too bright. A rotation practitioner, fresh from her Solcarin assignment. But below the new markings, at her wrist, Caeleth caught a glimpse of threadwork — a thin band woven in colors he recognized. Frontier work. The kind of threadwork you saw in settlements on the network's edge, where the dyes came from local plants and the patterns were passed down through families, not taught in institutions. She'd come from out here. Or somewhere very like it.

Her boots were already caked with the red-brown soil of the Mirathyn fields. She'd been here since the night before, Caeleth would learn later, and had walked the community's fields that morning before dawn. She carried a field journal under her arm that was thick with loose pages and handwritten annotations — the journal of someone who recorded everything, who trusted paper more than memory and memory more than institutions.

She stopped a few paces from the team. Her eyes moved across them with a frankness that reminded Caeleth of Instructor Halveth conducting assessments — thorough, unhurried, uninterested in making anyone comfortable. She took in Jorath pulling equipment from the storage compartment, Sorvyn stretching his back with the slow care of a man managing old complaints, and Caeleth standing beside a kit he wasn't sure he was supposed to carry. She catalogued them, filed them, and turned to Vaelen.

"You're the pattern reader," she said.

It wasn't a question. She'd done her preparation. She knew who was coming and what they could do. Caeleth watched Vaelen receive the directness, and for the first time since joining the team, he saw something he didn't expect: a beat of hesitation. Not long — a fraction of a second, less than a breath — where Vaelen's ready warmth didn't arrive on schedule. As if the grin had been reaching for its usual place and found, briefly, that the face it lived on was occupied by something else. Attention, maybe. Recognition. The look of a man who'd expected a junior practitioner and found something that didn't fit the category.

Then the warmth came, and if it arrived a half-beat late, Caeleth was probably the only one who noticed. Probably.

"I am," Vaelen said, and extended his hand. "Vaelen."

She shook it. Firm grip, brief contact. "Drenna."

"Drenna." He held the name for a moment, the way he held data — feeling its weight. Then he turned to the team and the warmth broadened, became the inclusive thing it always was. "Let me introduce everyone. Sorvyn — my second. He'll tell you the most important thing he does is nap, and he's wrong about that but right about most other things. Jorath handles logistics, equipment, and finance. If you need something, ask him. If you need something that costs money, ask him twice. And Caeleth is our newest — two weeks ahead of you on the experience of not knowing where anything is."

The introductions were easy and generous. They gave Drenna a shape of the team without demanding anything in return. Caeleth noticed that Vaelen had described each of them by what they contributed, not by their rank — a kindness so precise it had to be deliberate. He filed it away as another piece of evidence that Vaelen was either genuinely good or very, very skilled at appearing so.

"I'm your rotation practitioner for this deployment," Drenna said. She nodded at each of them — Sorvyn, Jorath, Caeleth — with a precision that suggested she'd remember their names, faces, and Vaelen's descriptions of them long after this conversation ended. "I arrived last night."

"You're from this region," Vaelen said.

Again, not a question. He'd read it somewhere — in the threadwork at her wrist, in the soil on her boots, in the way she'd walked toward them from the settlement's interior rather than waiting at its edge. She'd walked the way someone walks through a place they know — not checking the path, not looking for landmarks. Moving through memory.

Something shifted in Drenna's expression. A flicker of — not surprise. Reassessment. She hadn't expected him to see that so quickly.

"I grew up forty minutes east," she said. "I know this community. I know the families. I know the node. I walked the eastern fields this morning." She opened her journal to a tabbed page. "The eastern Mirathyn fields stopped producing six weeks ago. Three families have relocated to the inner Hearthlands. The node specialist who came through last season called it cyclical." She looked at him directly. "It's not cyclical."

She said this without anger, without performance. The way you report damage after a storm. Here is what is broken. Here is what I know. Are you going to do something about it.

Vaelen took the journal. He studied her measurements — not glancing, not skimming. Reading, the way he read the network. His eyes moved across her notations and the focus was absolute, and Caeleth saw something happen that he didn't fully understand: Vaelen's body changed. The easy posture, the warmth, the looseness that said nothing here is urgent — all of it went away, replaced by something straighter and colder. Not hostile. Functional. A man shelving everything that wasn't necessary.

"Your observations are better than what we'd have gotten from the regional office," he said. Simply. Without flattery.

"I know," Drenna said. Without vanity. A fact measured and recorded, like the node's decline curve.

"What else?"

"Something's wrong with the water. It's not contaminated — I've tested it. But it tastes like nothing. Started four weeks ago." She paused. "People are used to it already. That's the part that concerns me."

Caeleth didn't understand why that detail — people are used to it — made Vaelen's left hand close into a fist. He saw it happen. The fingers curling slowly, one at a time, until the knuckles were white. The rest of Vaelen's body remained perfectly composed — the face attentive, the voice steady, the posture professional. But the hand had made its own decision.

"Right," Vaelen said, and his voice had shifted register. Lower, quieter, stripped of the warmth that made people feel at ease. This was the voice that gave orders. "Here's what we'll do. Sorvyn — perimeter walk, full settlement, not just the node radius. I want you talking to the elders. Listen more than you ask. Jorath — field station at the node, standard readings. I want numbers on paper before I go in for a direct read." He looked at Caeleth. "You're with Jorath. Watch everything. Write everything down."

Then he looked at Drenna.

"You're with me. The community elder will want the official meeting — the speech, the reassurances. We'll give him that. He's earned it. He's the elder of a place that's frightened and hasn't been listened to, and we're going to listen before we do anything else." He handed the journal back to her. "After that, you take me to the eastern fields. And you tell me everything you know about this place that isn't in any report. The things you know because you grew up here."

Drenna looked at him. The assessment was visible — Caeleth could almost see the calculations adjusting behind her eyes. She'd arrived with an argument prepared. A case for urgency, built from data and frustration and the specific fury of someone who'd watched institutions fail her home. Vaelen had taken the argument out of her hands by agreeing with it before she could make it — and then he'd added something she hadn't accounted for: respect for the elder. The political instinct to honor the community's own authority before asserting his.

She'd expected competence. She hadn't expected that.

"Twenty minutes," she said.

"Twenty minutes," Vaelen agreed.

She turned toward the settlement. Vaelen fell into step beside her — not behind, not ahead, beside — and they walked together toward the elder's house, and within ten paces they were talking. Caeleth watched them go: the tall man with the easy stride and the young woman with the precise one, her journal between them, her hands moving when words weren't enough, his head inclined toward her in the posture of someone who was listening with everything he had.

Jorath appeared at Caeleth's shoulder. "Secondary kit," he said. "On the carriage. Move."

Caeleth moved. He hefted the kit — heavier than expected — and followed Jorath toward the node, and as they walked through the settlement's main path he noticed things that he would spend the evening trying to articulate in the margins of his journal.

A child was sitting on a wall, swinging her legs. She waved at them. Caeleth waved back. The child smiled. It was a normal smile. There was nothing wrong with it. But it didn't travel — it reached the child's mouth and stopped there, as if the muscles of the face had done their part and the rest of the body hadn't received the message.

A man was repairing a fence. He worked steadily, competently. His hands knew the wood. But he'd been repairing the same section since they arrived — Caeleth could see the fresh-cut timber — and his movements had a quality of repetition that was too even, too regular, like a wheel turning because it had been turned, not because anyone needed it to.

The communal table outside the nearest house was set for a meal. The plates were arranged with care. The food looked adequate. Caeleth couldn't have said why the word that came to mind was adequate rather than good — the bread was bread, the stew was stew — but something in the arrangement suggested a meal that would be eaten because meals were eaten, not because anyone was hungry for it.

And the flowers. In every window box, blooming. Well-tended. Bright. But the colors were wrong in a way that Caeleth couldn't articulate and wouldn't have noticed if he hadn't already been looking. The reds were present but not vivid. The yellows were there but didn't catch the light. As if someone had painted a perfect copy of a garden from memory, and the memory was beginning to fade, and nobody had noticed because the fading happened so slowly that each day looked the same as the last.

He thought about mentioning it to Jorath. He didn't. Jorath was already setting up the field station with movements that suggested he'd stopped thinking about flowers a long time ago.

· · ·

That evening they ate at the elder's table.

The elder was a man named Torven — heavy, white-haired, with the broad hands of someone who'd farmed Mirathyn his entire life. He served them a stew of root vegetables and smoked grain that was hot and filling and tasted, to Caeleth, like it should have tasted like more than it did. He drank a cup of water from the community well and tried not to think about the fact that Drenna was right. The water tasted like nothing. Not bad. Not mineral. Not cold. Nothing. Like drinking the memory of water.

Vaelen and Drenna sat across from each other. They'd been in the eastern fields for three hours. Drenna's boots were caked to the ankle now, and her hands were dirty, and there was a smear of red soil on her cheek that she either hadn't noticed or didn't care about. She was explaining something to Vaelen — gesturing with the intensity of someone who'd been holding this information alone for too long — and Vaelen was listening with the absolute focus that Caeleth was beginning to understand was his rarest and most genuine mode. Not the warmth. Not the grin. This. The listening. The way he turned his whole body toward the person speaking and made the rest of the world go quiet.

Sorvyn sat beside Caeleth, eating slowly. He'd completed his perimeter walk. He'd spoken with the elder and three other families. He hadn't shared his findings with anyone except Vaelen, and that conversation had lasted two minutes — a look, a few murmured words, a nod. Whatever Sorvyn had found, Vaelen now carried it, and Sorvyn was content to let him carry it. The trust was so complete it felt like gravity.

"He's worried," Sorvyn said quietly, not looking up from his stew.

"How can you tell?"

"The hand."

Caeleth looked. Vaelen's left hand rested on the table beside his untouched water cup. It was closed. A fist. Tight. Still. The rest of him was animated — nodding, asking questions, his voice measured and professional. But the hand had its own opinion, and the opinion was fear.

"What does it mean?" Caeleth asked.

"It means he's sensed something he doesn't have a name for yet," Sorvyn said. He spooned his stew without hurry. "But he will. He always figures it out."

Caeleth wanted to believe that. Sorvyn clearly did — his faith was a living thing, steady and warm and absolute. But Caeleth looked at Vaelen's fist, and then he looked at the stew in his bowl that should have tasted like something, and then he looked at the window box behind Vaelen's head where the flowers were blooming in colors that were almost right.

Almost.

He went to bed thinking about the water. About how quickly the people here had stopped noticing it tasted like nothing. About what else might be fading that nobody had noticed yet.

He slept badly.

Sometime deep in the night — he couldn't have said the hour — he woke needing to relieve himself and stepped outside the guest quarters into air that was colder than it should have been for the season. The settlement was quiet. Moonlight turned the pale wood of the houses silver-blue. Somewhere distantly, a bond creature made a low sound — not distressed, but watchful. The kind of sound an animal makes when it senses something its body knows about before its mind does.

He saw Drenna.

She was standing at the eastern edge of the settlement, where the last houses gave way to the Mirathyn fields and the fields gave way to open land. Her back was to him. She was very still. Her arms were at her sides, her hair loose now, black against the silver-blue of the moonlit air. She was looking east, toward where she'd grown up, toward the forty minutes of road between this community and whatever was left of hers.

Caeleth almost called out. He didn't. Something in her stillness stopped him — a rigidity in her shoulders, a tension in the way she held her head. This was not a woman enjoying the night air. This was not homesickness, or nostalgia, or the quiet contemplation of a young practitioner on her first rotation.

This was someone who was afraid.

She knew something. About this place, about the water, about the fields, about whatever was downstream that Vaelen couldn't yet name. She knew it the way you know things about the place you grew up — in the body, in the bones, in the part of the mind that doesn't bother with reports and data because it was there first, before the institutions, before the training, before the words.

She stood there for a long time, looking east into the dark. The moon moved. The bond creature called again, farther away now. The cold deepened.

Caeleth went back inside. He did not sleep again that night.
