Sorathel
Chapter One
The Mirathyn Road
In which a team arrives, a practitioner is admired, and the colors are wrong.
First Draft — v2

The runner hit a root and the whole carriage lurched sideways, slamming Caeleth's shoulder into the railing hard enough to make his teeth click. His field journal slid off his knee and vanished under the bench where Sorvyn was sleeping — or pretending to sleep — with his hat over his face and his boots crossed at the ankle like a man who'd decided the world could sort itself out for an hour.

"Grab something," Jorath said, not looking up from the purse he was counting. He had the coins arranged in neat rows on his thigh, three fingers holding them steady against the shaking with the absent precision of someone who'd done this in worse conditions. "Hathren takes the western ridge at speed. Every time."

Caeleth grabbed the railing. Outside, the landscape blurred — rolling green hills cut with dark stone, stands of silver-barked trees whipping past close enough to touch. The air tasted of crushed leaves and something mineral, sharp at the back of the throat. Through the open slats of the carriage wall, he could see the ground passing beneath them at an impossible angle, tilted thirty degrees, and for a disorienting moment he couldn't tell if the world was slanted or they were.

Then the carriage leveled and the speed eased, and Caeleth remembered to breathe.

He was three weeks out of the academy and this was his second deployment. He was trying very hard not to look like someone for whom this was his second deployment.

"First time on Korveth?" That was Vaelen, sitting across from him, one arm slung over the side of the carriage with the easy looseness of a man in a tavern chair. He had a half-eaten piece of flatbread in his other hand, which he'd somehow kept hold of through the lurch. His eyes were warm. Not mocking. Caeleth had been watching for mockery since he'd been assigned to this team and hadn't found any yet, which made him more nervous, not less.

"Second time on a runner," Caeleth said. "But the first one was Ylveth. She's... calmer."

"Ylveth is a hundred and forty years old and has the temperament of a grandmother who's seen everything," Vaelen said. "Korveth is nineteen and runs like he's being chased by something only he can see. Different experience."

Jorath snorted without looking up from his coins. "Korveth runs like that because Hathren lets him. That man has no interest in arriving with passengers who can still walk."

"Hathren's reliable," Vaelen said.

"Hathren is a lunatic who happens to own a very fast creature. Those aren't the same thing." Jorath held a coin up to the light, squinting at it. "I'd like to arrive with my skeleton inside my body, if that's not too much to ask."

Vaelen grinned. It changed his whole face — the sharp angles softened, the assessing intelligence in his eyes became something warmer, conspiratorial. Caeleth had seen him do this in the briefing room, in the mess hall, on the road. Each time, whoever was on the receiving end of that grin felt, for a moment, like they were the only person who mattered. Caeleth had watched a supply clerk trip over her own boots after Vaelen smiled at her while asking for replacement bandages.

"Jorath's skeleton has survived worse," Vaelen said. "Remember the Veldryn deployment? The ice shelf?"

"I try not to."

"He fell forty feet into a crevasse and his first words were 'does this count as hazard pay.'"

Sorvyn lifted the hat from his face. He was older than the rest of them, with a stillness about him that Caeleth associated with people who'd decided what they thought about everything a long time ago. "His first words were considerably worse than that. You're editing for the new recruit."

"I'm curating," Vaelen said.

"He's looking out for you," Sorvyn said to Caeleth, and something in his voice — quiet, certain, unperformative — made it sound less like a joke and more like a statement of faith. The hat went back over his face. "He does that."

Caeleth saw Vaelen glance at Sorvyn — just for a half-second, something quick and unguarded — and then the easy grin was back. "Sorvyn thinks I'm better than I am. It's his one consistent belief."

"Two consistent beliefs," Sorvyn said from under the hat. "That, and that Jorath will find a way to bill us for this conversation."

Jorath pocketed his coins. "Only if it goes over ten minutes. I'm reasonable."

Caeleth laughed before he could stop himself. It came out louder than he intended and he felt his neck flush, but nobody reacted like it was strange. That was the thing about Vaelen's team. Three deployments together — Caeleth had looked it up — and they had the rhythms of people who'd been sharing meals for years. The jokes were lived-in. The silences were comfortable. Sorvyn's faith in Vaelen, Jorath's constant accounting, Vaelen holding it all together with warmth that felt effortless — even the friction had the texture of something old and affectionate, like brothers who'd argued the same argument so many times it had become a form of love.

He wanted to belong here with a sharpness that embarrassed him.

· · ·

The carriage began to slow. The wild tilting of the landscape settled into a gentler sway, and through the slats Caeleth could see the ground leveling out — terraced fields, low stone walls, clusters of trees with deep green canopies that looked almost black against the afternoon sky. A Mirathyn farming community. Structures of pale wood and darker stone, arranged in the loose, organic pattern of a settlement that had grown around a central point rather than being planned.

The central point was visible from here: a Rootsong node, marked by the faint shimmer in the air above it that Caeleth had learned to identify at the academy. The shimmer was supposed to be gold-green, pulsing gently with Amarwen's rhythm. This one was gold-green. But the pulse was irregular. Caeleth noticed it the way you notice a person breathing oddly — not alarming, exactly, but not right.

Vaelen noticed it too. He'd stopped eating. The flatbread was forgotten in his hand, and his eyes had gone distant in the way they did when he was reading — not words, not maps. The network. Caeleth had seen it twice now and it still unnerved him: the way Vaelen's attention would shift to something invisible, his expression going flat and focused, his head tilting slightly as if listening to a frequency nobody else could hear. The team knew not to interrupt him when he went like this. Even Jorath went quiet.

After a moment, Vaelen blinked. He looked at the flatbread in his hand with mild surprise, as if he'd forgotten he was holding it. Then he ate the rest of it in two bites, brushed the crumbs off his chest, and said, "The node's stressed but functional. Soil depletion pattern, probably eighteen months of gradual decline. The Mirathyn crops are pulling more than the network can replenish."

He said it the way someone would say it's going to rain later. Casual. Already moving to the next thing. Caeleth was still trying to process the fact that Vaelen had just extracted eighteen months of agricultural and network data from a three-second unfocused stare.

"How bad?" Jorath asked. The coins were put away. His posture had shifted. Deployment mode.

"Not emergency. Not comfortable either. The community's probably noticed it in the yields — smaller harvests, lower Mirathyn potency, bond creatures a little more sluggish than usual. They'll have attributed it to weather or season. People always do."

"And the real cause?"

Vaelen paused. It was a specific kind of pause — Caeleth was learning to read them. This one meant he had an answer but was deciding how much to share. "The node's compensating for something downstream. Drawing resources from the local network to feed a deficit further along the line. Like a body pulling blood from the extremities to protect the core."

Sorvyn's hat was off now. "What's downstream?"

"I don't know yet. Could be nothing — a temporary disruption that'll resolve itself. Could be a cascade starting. We'll know more once I can read the node directly."

The carriage shuddered and dipped, and suddenly the ground was much closer. Caeleth gripped the railing again as the whole structure tilted forward at an angle that felt deeply incompatible with survival. A sharp whistle from above — Hathren, the runner's handler, calling out a command in a guttural tone that was half-voice, half-Rootsong. The creature beneath them groaned, a sound so deep Caeleth felt it in his ribs, and the descent smoothed into something merely alarming rather than lethal.

They settled. The ground stopped moving. Caeleth's hands took several seconds to believe it.

Hathren swung down from the driving perch at the creature's neck — a wiry man with sun-darkened skin and the permanently squinted expression of someone who spent his life looking into wind. He slapped Korveth's flank twice, muttered something that might have been affectionate or might have been a curse, and began unfastening the carriage rigging with the efficient disinterest of a man who'd done this four thousand times.

Vaelen dropped over the side. Caeleth followed, less gracefully, and for the first time saw what had been carrying them.

Korveth was massive. Broader than a house, low-slung, with six legs folded beneath a body covered in plates of something that wasn't quite stone and wasn't quite bark — a mottled gray-brown surface that seemed to shift in the light, as if the creature's skin couldn't decide whether it was mineral or organic. Its head was tucked against its chest, breath misting in slow, vast exhalations that stirred the grass flat in a circle around it. Along its spine, where the carriage had been secured to a natural ridge of plating, pale lichens grew in spiraling patterns that Caeleth recognized from his textbooks as root network signatures — the bond between Korveth and Hathren made visible on the creature's body.

Hathren put a hand on the creature's jaw without looking, the way you'd rest your hand on a table. Korveth's enormous eye — amber, flecked with green, disturbingly intelligent — tracked him with an expression that Caeleth could only describe as tolerant affection. The spiraling lichens pulsed once, a wave of faint gold-green that traveled from the point of contact outward and faded.

"Two hours," Hathren said to Vaelen. "Then he needs to graze. I'm not waiting past dusk."

"Understood," Vaelen said. "Thank you, Hathren."

Hathren grunted, which apparently concluded the social portion of their interaction, and turned back to his creature.

· · ·

A woman was walking toward them from the settlement.

She moved with purpose — not rushing, not dawdling. The walk of someone who had been expecting them and had used the time to prepare. She wore Solvarei field markings, the same as theirs, though hers looked more broken-in, the insignia on her shoulder faded from sun and use. Her dark hair was cut short in the practical way of someone who didn't have time for it. Her hands were clean but her boots were caked with soil from the Mirathyn fields, and Caeleth noticed that she carried a field journal under one arm that was thick with loose pages and handwritten tabs — the journal of someone who documented everything.

She stopped in front of them and her eyes moved across the team — Jorath already pulling equipment from the storage compartment, Sorvyn stretching his back slowly, Caeleth trying to look like he had a purpose — and settled on Vaelen.

"You're the pattern reader," she said. Not a question.

"Vaelen," he said. He extended a hand. "You're the rotation practitioner assigned here?"

"Drenna." She shook his hand — firm, brief, no performance in it. "I've been here three weeks. I've filed two reports with the regional office. Neither was acknowledged. I requested specialist support nine days ago. You're nine days late, but you're here." She opened the field journal to a tabbed page and held it out. "This is the node's decline curve as I've measured it. The eastern Mirathyn fields stopped producing six weeks ago. Three families have relocated to the inner Hearthlands because they couldn't feed themselves on what the yields were giving them. The node specialist who came through last season said it was cyclical. It's not cyclical."

Caeleth watched Vaelen take the journal. He expected the grin, the warmth, the thing he did that made people feel seen. It didn't come. Instead Vaelen looked at the data — really looked, his eyes moving across her measurements with the same focused intensity he'd had when reading the node from the carriage — and what came over his face was something Caeleth hadn't seen before. Not charm. Not professional assessment. Something closer to recognition.

"Your measurements are better than what we'd have gotten from the regional office," Vaelen said. He said it simply, without flattery.

"I know," Drenna said. She said it the same way — without vanity. A fact.

Jorath looked up from his equipment check and caught Caeleth's eye. He raised one eyebrow a fraction of an inch. Caeleth wasn't sure what it meant, but it felt like a private commentary on the fact that two people had just sized each other up in ten seconds and come to a mutual conclusion that the rest of them hadn't been invited into.

"The community elder wanted to meet you first," Drenna continued. "The prepared speech. The tour of the healthy fields. The reassurances."

"And?"

"I told him you'd want to see the node first." A pause. "I'm assuming I'm right about that."

Something crossed Vaelen's face — not the grin, something realer and less practiced. "You're right about that."

"Good. There's also something wrong with the water. It's not contaminated — I've tested it. But it tastes like nothing. Started about four weeks ago. People are used to it already. That's the part that concerns me."

The smallest shift in Vaelen's posture. Caeleth almost missed it. A straightening of the spine, a stillness that replaced the easy looseness. Sorvyn caught it too — Caeleth saw his eyes flick to Vaelen, trusting, waiting for the read. Whatever Vaelen saw in the data, whatever he sensed in Drenna's observation about the water, Sorvyn would follow it. Caeleth found that certainty — that absolute faith in another person's judgment — both comforting and faintly terrifying.

"Show us," Vaelen said.

Drenna turned and walked toward the settlement. She didn't look back to see if they were following. She didn't need to.

Caeleth gathered his field journal from the carriage and hurried after them. As he passed the first row of houses he noticed something that he would write about later, in the margins of his report, in handwriting that had gone unsteady:

The flowers in the window boxes were blooming. Every house had them — bright, well-tended, cheerful. But the colors were slightly wrong. Not dead. Not wilted. Just... muted. As if someone had painted a perfect copy of a garden from memory, and the memory was beginning to fade.

He looked at Vaelen, walking ahead beside Drenna, listening as she talked him through three weeks of observations with the brisk efficiency of someone who'd been waiting to give this briefing to a person capable of hearing it. Vaelen was nodding, asking short questions, his attention fully locked on her in a way that Caeleth recognized — the same focus he gave the root network when he was reading. Except this was directed at a person. He was reading her. Her data, her instincts, the things she'd noticed that weren't in the journal. And she was letting him, because she'd decided in the first thirty seconds that he was worth the information.

Behind them, Sorvyn walked at an even pace, watching Vaelen with an expression that was hard to name. It wasn't worry, exactly. It was the look of someone who believed completely in another person and was watching that person encounter something that belief alone might not be enough for.

Jorath fell into step beside Caeleth. "First deployment with a rotation practitioner?" he asked, quiet enough that the others wouldn't hear.

Caeleth nodded.

"That one knows more than she's in her reports," Jorath said. He adjusted the strap of his equipment pack. "Which means either the reports are bad or she's saving the good material for something. Either way—" He patted his coin purse, a gesture Caeleth was beginning to understand as Jorath's version of a shrug. "Pay attention."

Caeleth paid attention. And as they walked deeper into the community, past the muted flowers and the too-quiet streets and the people who smiled and waved with a warmth that somehow didn't reach the air around them, he noticed one more thing that he didn't write in the report, because he didn't have words for it yet:

Vaelen's hand — the one that had held the flatbread through every lurch and jolt on Korveth's back, the one that had taken Drenna's journal with such steady precision — was closed into a fist at his side.

He asked Sorvyn about it later that evening, by the fire, because Sorvyn seemed like the person who would know.

"It means he's worried," Sorvyn said. He was watching Vaelen across the fire, where Vaelen and Drenna had been talking for over an hour, her journal open between them, their voices too low to hear. "But you won't see it anywhere else. Just the hand." Sorvyn took a sip of his drink. "He's the best of us, Caeleth. Whatever he's sensing here — trust it. I always do."

Caeleth looked at Vaelen's fist, still closed, still tight. He looked at Sorvyn's face, calm and certain and devoted. He looked at Drenna, who was pointing at something in her journal and speaking with the controlled urgency of someone who'd been carrying a warning for three weeks and had finally found someone who could hear it.

And he looked at the flowers in the window box nearest the fire, their petals open to the evening light, their colors almost right, almost beautiful, almost what they were supposed to be.

Almost.

End of Chapter One
