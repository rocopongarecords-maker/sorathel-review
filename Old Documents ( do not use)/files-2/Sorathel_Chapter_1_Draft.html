Sorathel
Chapter One
The Mirathyn Road
In which a team arrives, a practitioner is admired, and the colors are wrong.
First Draft

The runner hit a root and the whole carriage lurched sideways, slamming Torvyn's shoulder into the railing hard enough to make his teeth click. His field journal slid off his knee and vanished under the bench where Sorvyn was sleeping, or pretending to sleep, with his hat over his face and his boots crossed at the ankle like a man who'd decided the world could sort itself out for an hour.

"Grab something," Jorath said, not looking up from the purse he was counting. He had the coins arranged in neat rows on his thigh, three fingers holding them steady against the shaking with the absent precision of someone who'd done this in worse conditions. "Korveth takes the western ridge at speed. Every time."

Torvyn grabbed the railing. Outside, the landscape blurred — rolling green hills cut with dark stone, stands of silver-barked trees whipping past close enough to touch. The air tasted of crushed leaves and something mineral, sharp at the back of the throat. Through the open slats of the carriage wall, he could see the ground passing beneath them at an impossible angle, tilted thirty degrees, and for a disorienting moment he couldn't tell if the world was slanted or they were.

Then the carriage leveled and the speed eased, and Torvyn remembered to breathe.

He was three weeks out of the academy and this was his second deployment. He was trying very hard not to look like someone for whom this was his second deployment.

"First time on a runner?" That was Vaelen, sitting across from him, one arm slung over the side of the carriage with the easy looseness of a man in a tavern chair. He had a half-eaten piece of flatbread in his other hand, which he'd somehow kept hold of through the lurch. His eyes were warm. Not mocking. Torvyn had been watching for mockery since he'd been assigned to this team and hadn't found any yet, which made him more nervous, not less.

"Second," Torvyn said. "But the first one was Ylveth. She's... calmer."

"Ylveth is a hundred and forty years old and has the temperament of a grandmother who's seen everything," Vaelen said. "Korveth is nineteen and runs like he's being chased by something only he can see. Different experience."

Jorath snorted without looking up from his coins. "Korveth runs like that because Vaelen asked him to."

"I asked him to make good time."

"You asked him in Rootsong. To a nineteen-year-old bond-runner, 'make good time' in Rootsong sounds like 'the world is ending, go.'" Jorath held a coin up to the light, squinting at it. "I'd like to arrive with my skeleton inside my body, if that's not too much to ask."

Vaelen grinned. It changed his whole face — the sharp angles softened, the assessing intelligence in his eyes became something warmer, conspiratorial. Torvyn had seen him do this in the briefing room, in the mess hall, on the road. Each time, whoever was on the receiving end of that grin felt, for a moment, like they were the only person who mattered. Torvyn had watched a supply clerk trip over her own boots after Vaelen smiled at her while asking for replacement bandages.

"Jorath's skeleton has survived worse," Vaelen said. "Remember the Veldryn deployment? The ice shelf?"

"I try not to."

"He fell forty feet into a crevasse and his first words were 'does this count as hazard pay.'"

Sorvyn lifted the hat from his face. He was older than the rest of them, with a stillness about him that Torvyn associated with people who'd decided what they thought about everything a long time ago. "His first words were considerably worse than that. You're editing for the new recruit."

"I'm curating," Vaelen said.

"He's performing," Sorvyn said, and put the hat back.

There was a half-second where something crossed Vaelen's face — not hurt, not offense, something faster and less identifiable — and then it was gone, replaced by an easy laugh. "Sorvyn thinks everything I do is a performance. It's his one consistent belief."

"Two consistent beliefs," Sorvyn said from under the hat. "That, and that Jorath will find a way to bill us for this conversation."

Jorath pocketed his coins. "Only if it goes over ten minutes. I'm reasonable."

Torvyn laughed before he could stop himself. It came out louder than he intended and he felt his neck flush, but nobody reacted like it was strange. That was the thing about Vaelen's team. Three deployments together — Torvyn had looked it up — and they had the rhythms of people who'd been sharing meals for years. The jokes were lived-in. The silences were comfortable. Even the friction between Sorvyn and Vaelen had the texture of something old and affectionate, like brothers who'd argued the same argument so many times it had become a form of love.

He wanted to belong here with a sharpness that embarrassed him.

· · ·

The carriage began to slow. The wild tilting of the landscape settled into a gentler sway, and through the slats Torvyn could see the ground leveling out — terraced fields, low stone walls, clusters of trees with deep green canopies that looked almost black against the afternoon sky. A community. Structures of pale wood and darker stone, arranged in the loose, organic pattern of a settlement that had grown around a central point rather than being planned.

The central point was visible from here: a Rootsong node, marked by the faint shimmer in the air above it that Torvyn had learned to identify at the academy. The shimmer was supposed to be gold-green, pulsing gently with Amarwen's rhythm. This one was gold-green. But the pulse was irregular. Torvyn noticed it the way you notice a person breathing oddly — not alarming, exactly, but not right.

Vaelen noticed it too. He'd stopped eating. The flatbread was forgotten in his hand, and his eyes had gone distant in the way they did when he was reading — not words, not maps. The network. Torvyn had seen it twice now and it still unnerved him: the way Vaelen's attention would shift to something invisible, his expression going flat and focused, his head tilting slightly as if listening to a frequency nobody else could hear. The team knew not to interrupt him when he went like this. Even Jorath went quiet.

After a moment, Vaelen blinked. He looked at the flatbread in his hand with mild surprise, as if he'd forgotten he was holding it. Then he ate the rest of it in two bites, brushed the crumbs off his chest, and said, "The node's stressed but functional. Soil depletion pattern, probably eighteen months of gradual decline. The Mirathyn crops are pulling more than the network can replenish."

He said it the way someone would say it's going to rain later. Casual. Already moving to the next thing. Torvyn was still trying to process the fact that Vaelen had just extracted eighteen months of agricultural and network data from a three-second unfocused stare.

"How bad?" Jorath asked. The coins were put away. His posture had shifted. Deployment mode.

"Not emergency. Not comfortable either. The community's probably noticed it in the yields — smaller harvests, lower Mirathyn potency, bond creatures a little more sluggish than usual. They'll have attributed it to weather or season. People always do."

"And the real cause?"

Vaelen paused. It was a specific kind of pause — Torvyn was learning to read them. This one meant he had an answer but was deciding how much to share. "The node's compensating for something downstream. Drawing resources from the local network to feed a deficit further along the line. Like a body pulling blood from the extremities to protect the core."

Sorvyn's hat was off now. "What's downstream?"

"I don't know yet. Could be nothing — a temporary disruption that'll resolve itself. Could be a cascade starting. We'll know more once I can read the node directly."

The carriage shuddered and dipped, and suddenly the ground was much closer. Torvyn gripped the railing again as the whole structure tilted forward at an angle that felt deeply incompatible with survival. He heard a sound — deep, resonant, somewhere between a purr and a groan — and the carriage settled with a final lurch that sent Jorath's neatly arranged pack sliding into Sorvyn's boots.

They had stopped.

Vaelen swung over the side of the carriage and dropped to the ground with a lightness that looked practiced. Torvyn peered over the edge and saw, for the first time, what had been carrying them.

Korveth was massive. Broader than a house, low-slung, with six legs folded beneath a body covered in plates of something that wasn't quite stone and wasn't quite bark — a mottled gray-brown surface that seemed to shift in the light, as if the creature's skin couldn't decide whether it was mineral or organic. Its head was tucked against its chest, breath misting in slow, vast exhalations that stirred the grass flat in a circle around it. Along its spine, where the carriage had been secured to a natural ridge of plating, pale lichens grew in spiraling patterns that Torvyn recognized as root network signatures — the creature's bond made visible on its body.

Vaelen put a hand on Korveth's flank. The creature made that deep sound again, and the spiraling lichens pulsed once — a wave of faint gold-green that traveled from the point of contact across the creature's entire body and faded.

"Good run," Vaelen murmured. Something in his voice was different. Softer. Less performed. The creature's enormous eye — amber, flecked with green, disturbingly intelligent — tracked him as he moved along its side.

Torvyn climbed down after the others, trying to look like someone who'd dismounted a bond-runner before. His legs were shaking. Jorath was already pulling equipment from the storage compartment with efficient, irritated movements, muttering about an inventory discrepancy that no one but him had noticed or would ever care about. Sorvyn stretched his back slowly, squinting at the community ahead with an expression that gave away nothing.

And at the edge of the settlement, watching them arrive, a woman stood with her arms crossed.

She was perhaps thirty, with dark hair cut short in the practical way of someone who didn't have time for it and hands that looked like they'd spent more time in soil than in anything else. She wore no faction markings, no practitioner insignia. She watched the Solvarei team unload with the patient, measuring expression of someone assessing whether the help that had arrived was actually going to help.

Her eyes moved across them — Jorath and his inventory, Sorvyn and his silence, Torvyn and his obvious newness — and settled on Vaelen, who was still at Korveth's side, murmuring something to the bond-runner that made the creature's breath slow and its eye half-close.

She waited until he turned toward her. Then she walked forward, and her first words were not a greeting.

"The eastern fields stopped producing six weeks ago. The node specialist you sent last season said it was seasonal. It wasn't seasonal. Three families have relocated to the inner Hearthlands because they couldn't feed themselves on what the Mirathyn was yielding. And something is wrong with the water."

She said this without anger, without performance, without any apparent interest in making an impression. She said it the way someone reports damage after a storm — here is what is broken, here is what we need, are you going to fix it or not.

Vaelen looked at her for a long moment. Torvyn expected the grin, the warmth, the thing he did that made people feel seen. It didn't come. Instead, Vaelen's expression was quiet and focused — the same expression he'd worn when reading the node from the carriage, except directed at a person.

"What's wrong with the water?" he asked.

"It tastes like nothing."

The smallest shift in Vaelen's posture. Torvyn almost missed it. A straightening of the spine, a stillness that replaced the easy looseness. Sorvyn caught it too — Torvyn saw his eyes flick to Vaelen and then away, a conversation in a glance.

"I'm Vaelen," he said. "This is my team. We're going to take a look."

"Drenna," she said. She didn't extend her hand. "I'll take you to the node. The community elder wanted to meet you first — the polite version, the prepared speech, the tour of the healthy fields before you see the bad ones. I told him you'd want to see the bad ones first."

Something crossed Vaelen's face — not the grin, something realer and less practiced. A flash of recognition, maybe. Respect.

"You told him right," Vaelen said.

Drenna turned and walked toward the settlement. She didn't look back to see if they were following. She didn't need to.

Torvyn gathered his field journal from the carriage and hurried after them, and as he passed the first row of houses he noticed something that he would write about later, in the margins of his report, in handwriting that had gone unsteady:

The flowers in the window boxes were blooming. Every house had them — bright, well-tended, cheerful. But the colors were slightly wrong. Not dead. Not wilted. Just... muted. As if someone had painted a perfect copy of a garden from memory, and the memory was beginning to fade.

He looked at Vaelen, walking ahead beside Drenna, and saw that Vaelen had noticed the flowers too. Vaelen hadn't said anything. But his hand — the one that had rested so easily on Korveth's flank, the one that had held the flatbread through every lurch and jolt — was closed into a fist at his side.

Torvyn didn't know what that meant yet.

He would.
