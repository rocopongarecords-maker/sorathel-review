Sorathel · Season 1 · Editorial Assessment

George R.R. Martin

On character, prose, world, and the things that make a reader stay

What's Working

I'll start with the compliment because I don't give them often. Three chapters in, and you've done something most debut fantasy writers can't do in thirty: you've created three distinct human beings. Caeleth, Drenna, Vaelen — I can hear them. They think differently. They notice different things. They want different things. The multi-POV structure isn't a gimmick; it's earning its complexity because each head the reader enters genuinely changes what the world looks like.

Vaelen's Chapter 3 is the strongest piece of writing so far. The pattern-reading as sensory experience — trunk bass, branch midrange, capillary chatter — that's not a magic system description. That's a man in love. When he slips and the reader sees the raw feeling for the Amarvel, and then the control returns "like a door closing" — that's the character in one image. You told me more about Vaelen in that half-second of vulnerability than in all of Caeleth's careful observations combined.

The Mirathyn-abuse father is the right call. A man destroyed by the substance that was supposed to restore what the world took from him. The creature lying outside with dead lichen. The hand reaching for the dose vessel even now. That's real horror — not the gothic kind, the human kind. The kind that makes people put the book down and stare at the wall.

The girl who stopped playing in the field. That's your best Ethura detail yet. Better than the muted flowers. Better than the tasteless water. Because a seven-year-old who feels the wrongness before the instruments — that's not creepy, it's heartbreaking. She lost something she can't name. That's your whole book.

What's Concerning

1. The Season Is Too Long

Ten episodes. Seven chapters each. Three hundred thousand words. You know what else is three hundred thousand words? A Storm of Swords. I wrote that book and it nearly killed me, and I'd been doing this for twenty years.

You have three excellent chapters. You have a beautiful world, a strong central character, a magic system that sings. What you don't have is the proof that this engine can run for three hundred thousand words without repeating itself. Episodes 2 and 3 in your outline — second deployment, third deployment — are essentially the same narrative move: go somewhere, find the wrongness, Vaelen reads the Amarvel, Drenna observes the people, something's worse than they thought. How many times can you run that pattern before the reader starts skimming?

My advice: Consider whether Season 1 needs ten episodes. Seven might be sharper. Cut Episodes 2 and 3 into a single episode — one more deployment that accomplishes both objectives (deeper world-building AND the ceiling becoming visible). You get to the Surge faster. The reader's patience for "things getting worse" is limited. Give them beauty, give them dread, then break the world.

2. Drenna's Arc Is Underpowered for the Season

Drenna's chapters are strong. She's a genuine person on the page — the envy watching Jorath, the father's hand, the girl with dirt under her fingernails she sometimes misses. Good. But look at her Season 1 arc: Arrival → Partnership → Respect → Separation → Political path begins. That's a supporting character arc. That's Brienne in Book One — interesting, capable, but not driving anything.

Your Episode 1 Drenna is the most interesting character on the page. She has the observations that unlock Vaelen's discovery. She has the personal stakes (the father). She has the cross-community data that nobody else has assembled. And then she... leaves? Goes off to become political somewhere we don't see? That's a waste.

My advice: Drenna needs a Season 1 decision that costs her something. Not just "rotation ends, she leaves." She needs to choose — between the team and her ambition, between Vaelen and her path, between saying the truth and protecting something she's built. If Vaelen's apex is choosing silence, Drenna's should be choosing voice — but directed at the wrong target, or at the right target for the wrong reason. Give her a moment in Episodes 8-9 that's as morally complicated as Vaelen's silence.

3. The Three Deployments Problem

Act 1 is three deployments. Three new locations. Three sets of local characters. Three communities with their own problems. That's a lot of world to build and then abandon. Torven's community is alive to me now — Torven, Maret, the girl, the boy who bet Jorath, the grandmother's story. I care about these people. If the team leaves in Chapter 5 and I never see them again until the Dread destroys their region, I haven't lost characters I love. I've lost names I vaguely remember.

My advice: Either stay in Torven's community longer — make it the community the reader loves, the one they mourn when the Dread comes — or condense the deployments so the reader doesn't invest in characters who disappear. The Red Wedding works because we spent hundreds of pages at Robb's side. If we'd met him once and then he died ten chapters later, nobody would have cried.

4. Who Sits Down and Doesn't Get Up?

Episode 5 — someone on the team is lost. Becomes Hushed. Who? You haven't decided, or if you have, you haven't told me. This matters NOW, not in Episode 5. Because the character who's going to be lost needs to be the reader's favorite team member. Not Vaelen's favorite — the READER'S favorite. That means I need to love them by the end of Episode 1. I need their specific joke, their specific fear, their specific kindness. Right now, Sorvyn and Jorath are sketched but not beloved. Caeleth is sympathetic but he's the lens, not the heart. Who is the reader going to cry for?

My advice: Pick the person who sits down. Build them starting now. Give them the funniest line, the warmest moment, the scene that makes the reader think "this is my favorite character." Then take them away. That's the contract with the reader: love costs something in this world. If you can't make me cry in Episode 5, your Dread is just spectacle.

5. Vardo's Introduction Is Too Thin

Your outline says Vardo appears in Episode 1, Chapter 4. Surprise POV. The three Solvarei scene. I understand the plan: plant him, let him fade, bring him back when it matters. But one chapter in Episode 1 isn't enough to plant a character who's going to carry weight later. If the reader barely remembers him, his return doesn't land. He needs at least two strong scenes in Act 1 — and the scenes need to show something the reader won't get from anyone else. What does Vardo see that Vaelen doesn't? What does he say that nobody else would say?

The Prose

The writing is good. It's atmospheric, it's sensory, the voices are distinct. But you're still doing a thing I warned about: the tidy sentence. "The silence between the Amarvel's pulses had a feel." That's fine. "He tried to hold onto it — to examine it, to give it shape — and it slipped away, the way a dream dissolves when you try to look at it directly." That's better. The second sentence does work. The first one announces the work is coming. Cut the announcement. Start with the work.

Chapter 1 is the loosest — it was written first and it shows. The Vaelen introduction scene could lose three paragraphs and be stronger. Not every thought Caeleth has about Vaelen needs to be on the page. Let the reader fill in the gaps. The carriage scene is too long for what it accomplishes. Get them to the community faster.

Chapter 3 is the tightest. The pattern-reading has genuine beauty. Keep writing at that level.

Brandon Sanderson

On structure, magic system, pacing, and the promises you're making to the reader

What's Working

Your magic system is excellent. I mean that specifically. The pattern-reading has clear rules: it's sensory (three layers), it has costs (headache, metallic taste, tremors that worsen), it has range limitations, and it operates through coaxing rather than commanding. Branch work — creating a signal of need to redirect flow — is a mechanism I can understand and predict, which means when you break the rules later, I'll feel it. That's what a magic system should do: train the reader's expectations so you can subvert them.

The three-layer structure (trunk/branch/capillary) gives you a built-in escalation ladder. Surface reads are easy and cheap. Branch work is harder and costly. Deep reads are dangerous and physically damaging. The anomaly — the thing in the silence between pulses — sits OUTSIDE this system, which is exactly where your mystery should live: in the space your established rules can't explain.

Your "promise, progress, payoff" structure across the three chapters is clean. Chapter 1 promises: something is wrong (the child, the flowers, the water). Chapter 2 progresses: the wrong has a shape (four communities, the gap, Drenna's father as a dark mirror). Chapter 3 begins the payoff: Vaelen enters the Amarvel and discovers the wrongness is UPSTREAM of everything the instruments can measure. Each chapter raises the stakes while answering a question from the previous chapter. That's good serial structure.

What's Concerning

1. Your Magic System Has a Sanderson's First Law Problem

Sanderson's First Law: an author's ability to solve problems with magic is directly proportional to how well the reader understands that magic. Your pattern-reading is well-defined for DIAGNOSIS — Vaelen can feel what's wrong, trace its source, identify its timeline. That's clear. But what can he actually DO about it? Branch work redirected flow temporarily. What else? What are the limits of intervention? If Vaelen can coax the Amarvel to redirect, why can't he coax it to stop retreating? What makes the anomaly resistant to his methods?

Right now, the reader understands the input (reading) but not the output (action). That's fine for Episode 1 — mystery is appropriate. But by Episode 3, the reader needs to understand what pattern-readers CAN do so that what they CAN'T do in Episodes 5-9 feels earned rather than arbitrary.

Action item: Define three tiers of practitioner intervention, each with clear costs and limitations. The reader should be able to predict, roughly, what Vaelen can attempt and what will happen if he tries. Then the Dread becomes terrifying because it invalidates all three tiers.

2. The Pacing Between Acts Is Unbalanced

Act 1: Three episodes of discovery (30% of the season). Act 2: Three episodes of crisis (30%). Act 3: Four episodes of aftermath and choice (40%). That's back-heavy. The longest section of the season is the political maneuvering and the Amarith joint operation. In a visual medium (TV), that works because you can cut between action and conversation. In prose, four episodes of institutional politics after the spectacle of the Dread is a pacing risk. The reader's adrenaline from Episode 5 will dissipate long before Episode 9.

Action item: Either compress Act 3 (three episodes instead of four — combine The Elite and The Insight) or inject a secondary action sequence into Act 3 that keeps the physical stakes present alongside the political ones. The Amarith operation shouldn't be just politics — it should involve fieldwork, danger, the Amarvel resisting their methods in real time.

3. The Anomaly Needs Rules Even If the Characters Don't Know Them

Your anomaly — the texture in the silence, the simulation of normalcy — is a great mystery. But you, the author, need to know exactly what it is, how it works, and what its limitations are. Not the characters. You. Because if you don't know, the anomaly will become whatever the plot needs it to be, and the reader will sense the arbitrariness even if they can't articulate it.

Questions you need answers to (even if the reader never learns them in Season 1): What is filling the space the Amarvel retreats from? Is it alive? Is it conscious? Does it have goals? Can it be resisted? What determines who becomes Hushed versus who destroys versus who rebuilds? Is the seven-year-old girl's sensitivity a clue to how it's fought? Is Vaelen's inability to sense it directly a limitation of his training, his gift, or his psychology?

Action item: Write a one-page "Anomaly Bible" that answers these questions for yourself. It never goes in the book. But it ensures consistency.

4. The Two-Peak Structure Needs a Bridge

Peak 1 (Episode 5, the Dread) is spectacle and horror. Peak 2 (Episode 9, the Silence) is character and moral failure. The gap between them is Episodes 6-8 — mobilization, the elite, the insight. That's three episodes (~21 chapters, ~90,000 words) between your two biggest moments. In that space, the reader needs to feel that something is escalating, not just that the situation is complicated. The Amarith operation needs its own internal tension arc — not just "Vaelen sees them and is awed," but a rising series of conflicts where his loyalty to his own perception battles his desire for their approval. Each episode in the bridge should feel like the pressure is increasing, not like we're waiting for Episode 9 to arrive.

5. Caeleth's Season Arc Is Missing

George mentioned this too, but from a structural standpoint: Caeleth is your entry lens. He opened the book. The reader bonded with him first. His sensitivity is better than half the practitioners Vaelen has worked with. And then... what? "Junior → Awakening sensitivity → Growing competence → Witnesses the loss → Inherits the question" is not a character arc. It's a list of things that happen to him. What does Caeleth WANT? What does he CHOOSE? What does he LOSE? If the answer is "he wants to belong and he loses the team member he loved," that's a start — but he needs a decision, a moment where he acts rather than witnesses.

Action item: Give Caeleth one moment in Season 1 where his sensitivity reveals something that Vaelen's reading doesn't. Something that only the "bees behind a wall" perception could catch. Make it matter. Make it change the outcome of a scene. The junior who sees what the master misses — that's a promise the reader will hold you to in Season 2.

6. Your Estimated Word Count Is Probably Wrong

You've estimated 7 chapters per episode, 4,000-5,000 words per chapter, 70 chapters total. Based on your first three chapters (averaging ~5,000 words each), and the complexity of your outlines for later episodes, I'd estimate 4-5 chapters for lighter episodes and 8-10 for peak episodes. Your actual range is probably 45,000-60,000 words per act, putting the full season at 150,000-200,000 words. That's a more reasonable first novel. If you try to hit 300,000, you'll pad. Write the story that exists, not the word count you planned.

The Promises You've Made

By the end of Chapter 3, you've made these promises to the reader. Every one must be kept:

Promise 1: The anomaly will be explained. Not fully — but the reader will understand more about the silence between the pulses by season's end than they do now.

Promise 2: Vaelen's control will break. The reader has seen the fist. They've seen the door close after the unguarded moment. They're waiting for the moment it doesn't close. You must deliver that moment, and it must cost him something real.

Promise 3: Caeleth's sensitivity matters. You gave a junior practitioner a gift that even Vaelen noticed. If that gift doesn't affect the plot, you've broken the promise.

Promise 4: Drenna's father's condition connects to the larger mystery. You drew the parallel explicitly — same symptoms, different causes. The reader is waiting for someone to explain why. If the parallel is just thematic and never becomes plot-relevant, it will feel like a cheat.

Promise 5: The girl in the field knows something nobody else does. Barefoot, seven years old, five weeks ahead of the instruments. That's not a detail — that's a Chekhov's gun. She, or someone like her, needs to matter.

Promise 6: Torven's grandmother's story is incomplete. The old frameworks don't apply. But they're not worthless either — they contain partial truth. The reader expects the old stories to turn out to be more right than the modern practitioners assumed, just in ways nobody expected.

Overall Assessment

The foundation is strong. The prose is good and getting better. The magic system is one of the best I've seen in a debut — grounded, sensory, with clear costs and a mystery that sits outside its rules. The characters are human. The world is alive.

The risk is scope. You've planned a 300,000-word season with three acts and two peaks and ten episodes, and you've written 15,000 words. The distance between where you are and where you're going is enormous. The episodes between the peaks are undercooked — they have bullet points where they need chapters, and the pacing in Act 3 is a genuine structural problem.

My recommendation: outline Episodes 4-6 at chapter level before writing Episode 1's remaining chapters. The Dread is the most important sequence in the season — if you don't know exactly how it works, how it feels, and who is lost, everything before it is just prelude. Build toward the Dread. Make us dread the Dread. Then deliver it.

Joint Recommendation

We don't agree on much, but we agree on this:

1. Decide who dies. The lost team member needs a name, a face, and the reader's love before Episode 5. Start building them in Episode 1, Chapter 4.

2. Compress the season. Seven or eight episodes, not ten. Cut one deployment from Act 1. Cut one episode from Act 3. The story you're telling doesn't need 70 chapters. It needs 45 good ones.

3. Give Drenna a choice. Her Season 1 arc can't just be "arrives, contributes, leaves." She needs a moment that defines her — a decision that reveals who she is when the cost is real.

4. Write the Dread before you write anything else. Episode 5 is the hinge. If you don't know how it works, you don't know what you're building toward. Outline it now. Write a test chapter. Feel the horror on the page. Then go back and make sure Episodes 1-4 are pointed at it like arrows.

5. Trust your prose. Your Chapter 3 is better than your Chapter 1. You're learning as you write. That's correct. That's what first drafts are for. Don't go back and polish Episode 1 until the season is drafted. Move forward. The revision will be better when you know where the story actually went.
