Sorathel
Chapter One
The Mirathyn Road
In which a team arrives, a practitioner is admired, and the colors are wrong.
First Draft — v3

The runner hit a root and the whole carriage lurched sideways, slamming Caeleth's shoulder into the railing hard enough to make his teeth click. His field journal slid off his knee and vanished under the bench where Sorvyn was sleeping — or pretending to sleep — with his hat tipped over a face that had settled into its creases the way old leather settles into its folds. He was the oldest on the team by a decade, shoulders broad but unhurried, the kind of man whose body had decided long ago that efficiency mattered more than speed.

"Grab something," Jorath said, not looking up from the purse he was counting. He had the coins arranged in neat rows on his thigh, three fingers holding them steady against the shaking with the absent precision of someone who'd done this in worse conditions. He was compact, sharp-featured, with the restless hands of a person who needed something to organize at all times. "Hathren takes the western ridge at speed. Every time."

Caeleth grabbed the railing. Outside, the landscape blurred — rolling green hills cut with dark stone, stands of silver-barked trees whipping past close enough to touch. The air tasted of crushed leaves and something mineral, sharp at the back of the throat. Through the open slats of the carriage wall, he could see the ground passing beneath them at an impossible angle, tilted thirty degrees, and for a disorienting moment he couldn't tell if the world was slanted or they were.

Then the carriage leveled and the speed eased, and Caeleth remembered to breathe.

He was three weeks out of the academy and this was his second deployment. He was trying very hard not to look like someone for whom this was his second deployment.

"First time on Korveth?" That was Vaelen, sitting across from him, one arm slung over the side of the carriage with the easy looseness of a man in a tavern chair. He was tall — noticeably so, even seated — with dark eyes that held a kind of warm intelligence, the face of someone who'd been told he was striking often enough to have learned what to do with it. There were faint lines at the corners of his mouth that deepened when he smiled, placing him somewhere past thirty, though the energy he carried — quick, magnetic, slightly restless — made him seem younger. He had a piece of Ashara smokebread in his other hand, the dark crust flecked with volcanic salt, which he'd somehow kept hold of through the lurch.

"Second time on a runner," Caeleth said. "But the first one was Ylveth. She's... calmer."

"Ylveth is a hundred and forty years old and has the temperament of a grandmother who's seen everything," Vaelen said. "Korveth is nineteen and runs like he's being chased by something only he can see. Different experience."

Jorath snorted without looking up from his coins. "Korveth runs like that because Hathren lets him. That man has no interest in arriving with passengers who can still walk."

"Hathren's reliable," Vaelen said.

"Hathren is a lunatic who happens to own a very fast creature. Those aren't the same thing." Jorath held a coin up to the light, squinting at it. "I'd like to arrive with my skeleton inside my body, if that's not too much to ask."

Vaelen grinned. It changed his whole face — the sharp angles softened, the assessing intelligence in his eyes became something warmer, conspiratorial. Caeleth had seen him do this in the briefing room, in the mess hall, on the road. Each time, whoever was on the receiving end of that grin felt, for a moment, like they were the only person who mattered. Caeleth had watched a supply clerk trip over her own boots after Vaelen smiled at her while asking for replacement bandages.

"Jorath's skeleton has survived worse," Vaelen said. "Remember the Veldryn deployment? The ice shelf?"

"I try not to."

"He fell forty feet into a crevasse and his first words were 'does this count as hazard pay.'"

Sorvyn lifted the hat from his face. His eyes were pale, steady, the eyes of a man who measured everything twice and said it once. "His first words were considerably worse than that. You're editing for the new recruit."

"I'm curating," Vaelen said.

"He's looking out for you," Sorvyn said to Caeleth, and something in his voice — quiet, certain, without any need to perform — made it sound less like a joke and more like a statement of faith. The hat went back over his face. "He does that."

Vaelen glanced at Sorvyn — just for a half-second, something quick and hard to read — and then the easy grin was back. "Sorvyn thinks I'm better than I am. It's his one consistent belief."

"Two consistent beliefs," Sorvyn said from under the hat. "That, and that Jorath will find a way to bill us for this conversation."

Jorath pocketed his coins. "Only if it goes over ten minutes. I'm reasonable."

Caeleth laughed before he could stop himself. It came out louder than he intended and he felt his neck flush, but nobody reacted like it was strange. That was the thing about Vaelen's team. Three deployments together — Caeleth had looked it up — and they had the rhythms of people who'd been sharing meals for years. The jokes were lived-in. The silences were comfortable. Sorvyn's faith in Vaelen, Jorath's constant accounting, Vaelen holding it all together with warmth that felt effortless — even the friction had the texture of something old and affectionate, like brothers who'd argued the same argument so many times it had become a form of love.

He wanted to belong here with a sharpness that embarrassed him.

· · ·

The carriage began to slow. The wild tilting of the landscape settled into a gentler sway, and through the slats Caeleth could see the ground leveling out — terraced fields, low stone walls, clusters of trees with deep green canopies that looked almost black against the afternoon sky. A Mirathyn farming community. Structures of pale wood and darker stone, arranged in the loose, organic pattern of a settlement that had grown around a central point rather than being planned.

The central point was visible from here: a network node, marked by the faint shimmer in the air above it that Caeleth had learned to identify at the academy. The shimmer was supposed to be gold-green, pulsing gently with Amarwen's rhythm. This one was gold-green. But the pulse was irregular. Caeleth noticed it the way you notice a person breathing oddly — not alarming, exactly, but not right.

Vaelen noticed it too. He'd stopped eating. The smokebread was forgotten in his hand, and his eyes had gone distant in the way they did when he was reading — not words, not maps. The network. Caeleth had seen it twice now and it still unnerved him: the way Vaelen's attention would shift to something invisible, his expression going flat and focused, his head tilting slightly as if listening to a frequency nobody else could hear. The team knew not to interrupt him when he went like this. Even Jorath went quiet.

After a moment, Vaelen blinked. He looked at the smokebread in his hand with mild surprise, as if he'd forgotten he was holding it. Then he ate the rest of it in two bites, brushed the dark crumbs off his chest, and said, "The node's stressed but functional. Soil depletion pattern, probably eighteen months of gradual decline. The Mirathyn crops are pulling more than the network can replenish."

He said it the way someone would say it's going to rain later. Casual. Already moving to the next thing. Caeleth was still trying to process the fact that Vaelen had just extracted eighteen months of agricultural and network data from a three-second unfocused stare.

"How bad?" Jorath asked. The coins were put away. His posture had shifted. Deployment mode.

"Not emergency. Not comfortable either. The community's probably noticed it in the yields — smaller harvests, lower Mirathyn potency, bond creatures a little more sluggish than usual. They'll have attributed it to weather or season. People always do."

"And the real cause?"

Vaelen paused. It was a specific kind of pause — Caeleth was learning to read them. This one meant he had an answer but was deciding how much to share. "The node's compensating for something downstream. Drawing resources from the local network to feed a deficit further along the line. Like a body pulling blood from the extremities to protect the core."

Sorvyn's hat was off now. "What's downstream?"

"I don't know yet. Could be nothing — a temporary disruption that'll resolve itself. Could be a cascade starting. We'll know more once I can read the node directly."

The carriage shuddered and dipped. Caeleth gripped the railing as the whole structure tilted forward at a steep angle. A sharp whistle from above — Hathren calling out a command in a low, guttural tone that vibrated strangely in the air, more felt than heard. The creature beneath them groaned in response, a sound so deep it traveled through the carriage floor and into Caeleth's chest, and the descent smoothed into something merely alarming rather than lethal.

They settled. The ground stopped moving. Caeleth's hands took several seconds to believe it.

Vaelen swung over the side and dropped to the ground with a lightness that looked practiced. Caeleth followed, less gracefully, and steadied himself on solid earth. Behind them, Hathren was already climbing down from the driving perch at the creature's neck — a wiry man with sun-darkened skin and the permanently squinted expression of someone who spent his life looking into wind.

And now, standing at ground level, Caeleth saw Korveth properly.

The creature was massive. Broader than a house, low-slung, with six legs folded beneath a body covered in plates of something that wasn't quite stone and wasn't quite bark — a mottled gray-brown surface that shifted in the light, as if the creature's skin couldn't decide whether it was mineral or organic. Its head was tucked against its chest, breath misting in slow, vast exhalations that stirred the grass flat in a wide circle. Along its spine, where the carriage had been secured to a natural ridge of plating, pale lichens grew in spiraling patterns — root network signatures, the bond between Korveth and Hathren made visible on the creature's body like a map of their shared life.

Hathren put a hand on the creature's jaw without looking, the way you'd rest your hand on a familiar table. Korveth's enormous eye — amber, flecked with green, disturbingly intelligent — tracked him with what Caeleth could only describe as tolerant affection. The spiraling lichens pulsed once, a wave of faint gold-green that traveled from the point of contact across the creature's entire body and faded.

"Two hours," Hathren said to Vaelen. "Then he needs to graze. I'm not waiting past dusk."

"Understood," Vaelen said. "Thank you, Hathren."

Hathren grunted, which apparently concluded the social portion of their interaction, and turned back to his creature.

· · ·

A young woman was walking toward them from the settlement.

She was perhaps twenty or twenty-one, with long dark hair pulled back loosely, a few strands escaping around a face that was striking in its intensity — wide brown eyes, sharp cheekbones, the expression of someone whose mind was already three steps ahead of whatever her body was doing. She had the look of a person who'd arrived somewhere important very young and hadn't yet learned to hide how seriously she took everything. She wore Solvarei field markings like the rest of them, though hers were new — stiff at the shoulders, not yet shaped to her body. But her boots were already caked with the red-brown soil of the Mirathyn fields, and Caeleth noticed she carried a field journal under one arm, thick with handwritten tabs and loose pages, the journal of someone who documented everything.

She stopped a few paces from the team. Her eyes moved across them — Jorath pulling equipment from the storage compartment, Sorvyn stretching his back, Caeleth standing uncertainly — and settled on Vaelen with the focused recognition of someone who'd studied his deployment record.

"You're the pattern reader," she said. Not a question.

Vaelen looked at her. Caeleth expected the immediate grin, the warmth that made people feel welcome. Instead there was a beat — brief, assessing — and then something that wasn't quite the grin but was warmer than professional courtesy. "I am. Vaelen." He extended a hand, and when she shook it — firm, quick — he turned to the rest of the team. "Sorvyn, my second — he'll tell you he's retired from everything except napping, but don't believe him. Jorath handles logistics, equipment, and anything involving money; argue with him at your own expense. And Caeleth is two weeks ahead of you on the 'new to the team' experience, so he'll understand the confusion."

Caeleth felt a small flush of warmth at being included in the introduction as though he belonged. Vaelen did that — made you feel like a full member of the group while barely seeming to try.

"Drenna," the young woman said. She didn't smile, but she nodded at each of them with a precision that suggested she was filing them away. "I'm your rotation practitioner for this deployment. I arrived last night."

"You're from this region," Vaelen said. It wasn't a question either. He'd read something — in her boots, in her posture, in the way she'd walked toward them from the settlement rather than waiting at its edge.

"I grew up forty minutes east of here," Drenna said. "I know this community. I know the families. I know the node." A pause — not hesitant, deliberate. She was deciding how much to give him upfront. "I walked the eastern fields this morning. They stopped producing six weeks ago. Three families have already relocated to the inner Hearthlands. The node specialist who came through last season called it cyclical. It's not cyclical."

She said this without anger, without performance, without any apparent interest in making an impression. She said it the way someone reports damage after a storm — here is what is broken, here is what I know, are you going to do something about it or not.

Vaelen held her gaze for a moment longer than was strictly professional. Then he took the smokebread crumbs off his sleeve — a small, almost unconscious gesture that broke the formality — and said, "What else?"

"Something's wrong with the water. It tastes like nothing. Started about four weeks ago. People are used to it already." She looked at him directly. "That's the part that concerns me."

The smallest shift in Vaelen's posture. Caeleth almost missed it. A straightening of the spine, a stillness that replaced the easy looseness. He'd gone from charming senior practitioner to something else — something colder and more focused, like a lens adjusting.

"Right," he said. His voice had changed register — not harsh, but the warmth had been set aside, and what replaced it was the voice of someone who made decisions that other people followed. "Here's how we'll work. Sorvyn, I want a perimeter walk — the full settlement, not just the node radius. Talk to the elders, but listen more than you ask. Jorath, set up the field station at the node and run the standard readings. I want numbers before I go in for a direct read." He turned to Caeleth. "You're with Jorath. Watch what he does. Write everything down."

Then he turned back to Drenna. "You're with me. I want to see the eastern fields, and I want you to tell me everything you know about this place that isn't in any report — the things you know because you grew up here. The community elder will want the official meeting first. We'll give him twenty minutes. Then we go to the fields."

Drenna looked at him with an expression Caeleth couldn't fully parse. Not surprise — she'd expected competence. Something closer to recalibration. She'd arrived with a prepared argument for why the specialist team should take this seriously, and Vaelen had skipped past the need for it in thirty seconds.

"Twenty minutes is generous," she said.

"It's respectful," Vaelen said. "He's the elder of a community that's frightened and hasn't been listened to. We give him the respect, and then we do the work. Both matter."

Something shifted in Drenna's face — the faintest softening around the eyes, gone almost immediately, replaced by a nod. She'd learned something about him in that sentence. Something she hadn't expected.

"Twenty minutes," she said. "I'll take you to him."

She turned and walked toward the settlement. Vaelen fell into step beside her — not following, walking with her, matching her pace — and within seconds they were talking, her field journal open between them, Drenna pointing at something in her notes while Vaelen asked a question Caeleth couldn't hear.

Jorath appeared beside Caeleth, hoisting an equipment pack. "Come on, new blood. Grab the secondary kit."

Caeleth hefted the kit and followed, and as they passed the first row of houses he noticed something that he would write about later, in the margins of his report, in handwriting that had gone unsteady:

The flowers in the window boxes were blooming. Every house had them — bright, well-tended, cheerful. But the colors were slightly wrong. Not dead. Not wilted. Just... muted. As if someone had painted a perfect copy of a garden from memory, and the memory was beginning to fade.

He looked ahead at Vaelen and Drenna, walking side by side toward the elder's house. Drenna was talking — quick, precise, her hands moving when a word wasn't enough — and Vaelen was listening with the same focused stillness he gave the root network when he was reading. Except this was directed at a person. A twenty-year-old rotation practitioner who'd been on the team for five minutes and was briefing a senior pattern reader as though she'd been waiting her whole life to find someone capable of hearing what she had to say.

And Vaelen was hearing it. All of it. Caeleth could tell by the way he walked — the looseness was gone, the charm was shelved, and in its place was the version of Vaelen that Sorvyn believed in so completely: the man who, when something was genuinely wrong, became the sharpest instrument in the room.

Behind them, Sorvyn had already begun his perimeter walk, hands clasped behind his back, pace steady, asking nothing of anyone. He paused at a window box. Looked at the flowers. Looked away.

That evening, after the elder's meeting and the field survey and the node readings that confirmed what Vaelen had sensed from the carriage, Caeleth sat by the fire writing his notes. Across the flames, Vaelen and Drenna were still talking — her journal open, their voices low, the conversation now in its second hour. Whatever she knew about this place, about the network's behavior in this region, about the slow decline that the institutions had filed under "cyclical" — Vaelen was drawing it out of her with the patience of someone who understood that the best intelligence came from people, not instruments.

Sorvyn sat near Caeleth, cleaning his boots. Unhurried. Watching Vaelen the way he always watched Vaelen — with that quiet, unshakable faith.

"He's worried," Sorvyn said, not looking up.

"How can you tell?"

"The hand."

Caeleth looked. Vaelen's left hand — the one that had held the smokebread through every lurch on Korveth's back, the one that had shaken Drenna's so steadily — was closed into a fist at his side. Tight. Still. The only part of him that wasn't performing ease.

"But he'll figure it out," Sorvyn said. He put down his boot and picked up the other one. "He always does."

Caeleth looked at the fist. He looked at Sorvyn's face, calm and certain and devoted. He looked at Drenna, who was pointing at something in her journal and speaking with the controlled urgency of someone who'd been carrying a warning alone and had finally found a person capable of hearing it.

And he looked at the flowers in the window box nearest the fire, their petals open to the evening light, their colors almost right, almost beautiful, almost what they were supposed to be.

Almost.

End of Chapter One
