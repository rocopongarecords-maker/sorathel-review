Sorathel
Chapter Two
Drenna
First Draft — Martin Version

She left before dawn.

The team was sleeping. Sorvyn in the chair by the door, his hat over his face, his boots still on — he slept like a man who'd spent years expecting to be woken badly. Jorath in the corner, his equipment pack arranged within arm's reach, his ledger under his pillow. The young one — Caeleth — curled on his side with his field journal clutched against his chest like a child holding a stuffed animal. He'd been awake when she came back in the night. She'd heard him shift. She hadn't turned around.

Vaelen was not in the main quarters. She didn't know where he slept. She hadn't asked.

The path east was forty minutes on foot, longer in the dark, but Drenna had walked it when she was eleven and twelve and fourteen and she didn't need light for it. Her feet knew the stones. The dip where the irrigation channel crossed under the road. The place where the silver-bark roots had buckled the ground and you had to step wide or twist your ankle. Her body carried the map the way bodies carry the maps of the places they were made.

The air was cold. Colder than it should have been for the season. She noticed this the way she noticed everything — precisely, without sentiment, filed under a heading she'd use later. Four weeks ago, the water. Six weeks, the fields. Now the temperature. The decline had a sequence. She was learning its grammar.

· · ·

Her father's dwelling sat at the edge of a settlement smaller than the one she'd left — eight houses, a communal well, a node so faint that its shimmer was barely visible even at midday. The house was well-maintained. Not by him. By Maret, the neighbor, who had taken on the maintenance with the resigned efficiency of a woman who'd been doing favors so long they'd become obligations.

Maret's light was already on. It was always on when Drenna came. She suspected Maret heard her on the path — or heard the absence on the path, the particular silence that meant someone was approaching who knew the ground well enough not to make noise.

Drenna didn't knock at her father's door. She opened it and went in.

The dwelling was clean. Maret's work. The fire had been kept, the bedding changed, the plates washed and stacked. There was food on the table — a half-eaten bowl of something that had gone cold, a piece of bread with one bite missing. Maret's cooking. Her father had eaten some of it, probably because it was placed in front of him, the way a body continues to eat when the person inside it has stopped caring whether it does.

He was sitting by the window.

Not looking out. Not looking at anything. His hands were in his lap, palms up, fingers slightly curled, the hands of a man who had put something down a long time ago and forgotten that he'd ever held it. He was thin. Thinner than the last time, which had been thinner than the time before. His hair had gone entirely gray, which was wrong — he was forty-seven. She remembered him at forty: broad-shouldered, dark-haired, with a laugh that carried across fields. A man whose hands were always holding something — a tool, a rope, the scruff of his bond-creature's neck. Hands that moved because they belonged to a person who was alive in the full, restless, unsatisfied way that alive people are alive.

The bond-creature was outside. She'd passed it on the path. It lay in the grass beside the house, where it had lain every day for three years, waiting. A medium-sized thing — furred, broad-headed, with amber eyes that had once been sharp and were now patient in a way that broke something in Drenna every time she saw them. The creature was bonded to her father. The bond had not broken. That was the worst part. The lichen patterns on its back still pulsed faintly when her father moved, which he did, sometimes — a hand raised, a step taken to the latrine, the mechanical motions of a body that continued. The creature tracked these movements with an attention that had no object. It was waiting for someone who was still there. Still breathing. Still technically present.

Just — not.

Drenna set down the pack she'd brought. Dried fish. A root-flour mix that Maret could bake into bread. A new shirt, because his old ones were hanging off him and she couldn't look at that without her throat closing. She unpacked the supplies with movements that were quick and competent and entirely focused on the task, because the task was something she could do, and the other thing — the sitting with him, the being present beside a person who was present only in the most technical sense — was something she'd never learned to do without feeling like the floor had been removed from under her.

She put the dried fish in the cold-store. She stacked the flour. She folded the shirt and placed it on his bed.

Then she sat across from him.

He didn't look at her. His eyes were open but they received light the way a window receives light — it passed through, it illuminated nothing on the other side. She could see the ghost of his face in the shape of his face: the jaw that had been strong, the brow that had furrowed when he was thinking about a problem with the irrigation or a crack in the node's output or what to cook for the harvest meal. The architecture of the person was still there. The person was not.

"The node at Torven's community is failing," she said.

She talked to him. She always talked to him. Not because she expected a response. Because the alternative was silence, and silence in this room was a substance — thick, adhesive, the kind of quiet that stuck to your skin and followed you out the door and into the rest of your day.

"Eighteen months of decline. The eastern fields are dead. The water's gone flat — you'd know what that means. You always knew before the specialists did." She paused. Swallowed. "They sent a team. A real one, this time. The pattern reader is — he's good. He sensed the downstream compensation in three seconds from the carriage. I don't think he understands what he's looking at yet. But he's looking."

Her father's hand moved.

It was a small motion. The fingers of his right hand uncurled slightly, as if reaching for something on the table that wasn't there — a cup, a tool, a piece of bread to break. The movement lasted less than a second. Then the hand settled again, palm up, fingers curling back to their resting position. A reflex. A muscle memory. The body remembering a gesture the mind had abandoned.

Drenna stared at the hand. She stared at it for a long time.

Outside, the bond-creature made a sound — low, almost inaudible, a vibration more than a voice. It had felt the hand move. Through the bond, through the lichen, through whatever thread still connected it to the man who had raised it from a pup and named it and slept with it curled against his back for twenty years. It had felt the hand reach and stop. It was still waiting.

Drenna stood up. She touched her father's shoulder. The bone under her palm was too close to the surface. She left her hand there for three breaths — she counted — and then she picked up her empty pack and walked out.

Maret was on her porch, arms crossed. She was perhaps fifty, weathered, with the permanent expression of someone who had opinions about everything and shared approximately half of them.

"He ate yesterday," Maret said. "Most of the bowl."

"Thank you."

"The new shirt won't fit. He's smaller than last time."

"I know."

Maret looked at her. The look contained everything she didn't say — you should be here more, you should be here at all, you can't fix this from wherever you're going, this is your father and he is disappearing and you are letting him disappear because you have somewhere more important to be. All of it was in the look. None of it was wrong.

"I'll be back in a few days," Drenna said. "The deployment is close."

"The deployment is always close," Maret said. She went inside.

Drenna walked back. The forty minutes passed in what felt like ten. She didn't remember the stones or the dip or the silver-bark roots. She was thinking about the hand. The reach that wasn't a reach. The creature's sound. The thread that still connected them — still pulsed, still lived — while the man at the other end of it had gone so quiet that the pulse had nothing to carry.

She was thinking about the water that tasted like nothing. About how you stop noticing absence when it arrives slowly enough.

About how she had stopped noticing her father's absence and when exactly that had happened and what that made her.

· · ·

The team was awake when she returned. Breakfast was happening at the elder's table — a communal meal of boiled grain and dried fruit and a tea brewed from something local that tasted like warm earth. Drenna took a bowl and sat at the end of the table and ate with the focused efficiency of a person who understood that food was fuel and sentiment about food was a luxury.

The team's morning dynamics were interesting.

Jorath was arguing with Torven's daughter about the cost of additional supplies. Not unpleasantly — Jorath argued about money the way other people breathed, as a continuous and necessary function. The daughter was holding her own. Jorath looked almost impressed, which for Jorath meant his eyebrows moved a fraction of an inch.

Sorvyn sat with a cup of tea and said nothing. He'd finished eating before anyone else and had entered a state of restful observation that Drenna recognized from certain elders in her own community — the stillness of someone who had learned that watching was more useful than speaking and had decided to commit to it fully. He caught her eye once. Nodded. Returned to his tea. The nod said: I see you arrived before us and returned before breakfast and I have no questions about it because I was also once young and had places I needed to be at dawn. Or perhaps it said nothing at all. Sorvyn was difficult to read and she suspected he preferred it that way.

Caeleth was writing in his journal. He looked up when she sat down, then quickly away, then back. He had the fidgeting self-consciousness of someone who wanted to ask a question and was rehearsing it internally. She waited. He didn't ask. She ate her grain.

And Vaelen was at the node.

She could see him from the table — two hundred paces away, standing at the center of the shimmer with his eyes closed and his hands at his sides. Perfectly still. He'd been there since before she returned, according to Sorvyn's brief morning account — "He went out at first light. Hasn't moved." The elder had brought him tea an hour ago. The tea sat on a rock beside him, untouched, going cold.

Drenna watched him the way she watched everything: from a distance, with attention, building a model.

He was gifted. She'd confirmed that yesterday in the eastern fields. Three hours walking the dead Mirathyn rows, and she'd watched him work the way you watch a musician play an instrument you've only seen in books. His hands moving through the air, not touching anything, reading something invisible with a sensitivity that made her own considerable training feel blunt. At one point he'd stopped in the middle of a dead field, turned ninety degrees, and said, "The failure started here. This exact spot. Fourteen months ago." He'd pointed at a patch of dirt that looked identical to every other patch of dirt. Later, when she checked the community's planting records, he was right. Not approximately right. Right.

She'd asked him how he knew. He'd said, "The network remembers. It's like — reading a scar. The tissue tells you when the wound happened, how deep it went, which direction the blade was moving." He'd said this while walking, casually, as if describing how to read weather. No awareness that what he'd just done was extraordinary. No sense that the metaphor he'd chosen — scars, wounds, blades — revealed anything about how he experienced his own gift. He read suffering in the earth the way she read suffering in communities: expertly, intimately, from too much practice.

The waste still irritated her. Not his gift — his ignorance of its value. If she had his perception, she'd have built her entire argument around it by now. He had the most compelling evidence of systemic network failure she'd ever encountered, and he filed it in reports that nobody read. He deferred. He waited. He trusted the institution to recognize what he'd found and respond appropriately, and the institution had responded by sending him to another community to do it again.

She'd seen this pattern. At the Solcarin — the most prestigious one, the one whose name made practitioners in the field sit up straighter — she'd watched brilliant people defer to systems that didn't deserve their deference. She'd done it herself, for a while. Performed gratitude when she wanted to perform fury. Modulated her frontier accent until it was gone. Learned which version of her story to tell in which room — the resilient version for the patrons who funded her, the analytical version for the professors who assessed her, the humble version for the peers who needed to believe she was grateful rather than strategic.

She had been grateful. That was the thing nobody understood and she'd stopped trying to explain. She'd been grateful and strategic. Both. Simultaneously. All the time. The gratitude was real and the strategy was real and the inability of everyone around her to hold both truths at once was the loneliest thing about her education.

Vaelen's team had something her Solcarin never had. She'd noticed it on the carriage ride — no, before that, watching them unload, the way Jorath handed equipment to Sorvyn without looking and Sorvyn received it without looking and neither of them needed to look because their bodies had learned each other's rhythms. Trust. Not performed trust. Not institutional trust. The real kind, built from shared fear and shared meals and the specific intimacy of people who'd seen each other fail and stayed anyway.

She respected it. She observed it carefully. She did not understand it, not in the bone-deep way they understood it, because her experience of groups had always been this: you enter, you perform, you extract what you need, you leave. The group serves a function. The function is not belonging. Belonging is what happens to people who don't have somewhere else they need to be.

Drenna had somewhere else she needed to be. She'd had somewhere else she needed to be since she was twelve years old, sitting in a dwelling with a father who was disappearing, understanding with a clarity that frightened her that nobody was coming to fix this. That the institutions existed and the specialists existed and the reports were filed and the data was collected and none of it would bring her father back, because the system was not designed to bring anyone back. It was designed to document the loss. To measure the decline. To publish findings.

She was going to be the person who didn't just measure.

But first she needed the evidence. She needed deployments on her record, frontier communities in her field journal, data points that added up to an argument no one could dismiss. She needed this rotation the way a builder needs scaffolding — not as the structure, but as the thing you stand on while you build the structure.

Vaelen opened his eyes.

She saw it happen from two hundred paces — the subtle shift, the return. He stood still for another moment, processing whatever he'd read, and then he bent and picked up the cold tea and drank it in one long swallow. He turned toward the elder's table. His eyes found Drenna's across the distance, and even from here she could see the expression on his face: focused, troubled, wanting to talk. Not to Sorvyn. Not to Jorath. To her.

She understood why. She was the one who knew this land.

She put down her bowl and went to meet him.

· · ·

They walked the western fields together, and Drenna showed him what the specialists never saw.

Not the node readings. Not the soil data. Not the decline curve she'd charted in her journal. The other thing — the thing that lived in the spaces between data points, in the behavior of people rather than the behavior of networks.

"Torven's granddaughter used to play here," she said. They were standing at the edge of a field that was still producing, technically — the Mirathyn stalks upright, the leaves green, the root structures intact. On paper, this field was healthy. "Every afternoon. She'd run between the rows and the stalks would lean toward her. Mirathyn does that — responds to the energy of children. The movement, the noise. The stalks lean."

Vaelen looked at the field. "They're not leaning now."

"There are no children in this field. Haven't been for weeks. The girl stopped coming. Her parents didn't stop her — she stopped herself. She told her grandmother the field felt wrong. She couldn't explain it better than that. She's seven." Drenna looked at him. "The field reads as healthy in every assessment. The output is down eight percent, which is within seasonal variance. The specialist who came through last year walked this exact row and marked it as stable."

"The specialist wasn't a seven-year-old," Vaelen said.

"No. The specialist had training and instruments and protocols. The girl has bare feet and a bond with this land that started before she could walk." Drenna paused. "I'm not being sentimental. I'm telling you the instruments are measuring the wrong thing."

Vaelen was quiet. He'd stopped walking. His head was tilted again — the reading posture, the one that made his face go flat and his eyes go distant. But this time it was different. He wasn't reading the network. He was reading the field through what she'd told him, layering her observation over his own perception, trying to see what a seven-year-old saw and a specialist didn't.

"There's a gap," he said. "Between what the network is reporting and what's actually happening on the ground."

"Yes."

"The node reads as stressed but functional. The soil data shows decline but within parameters. The network's own diagnostic says this is recoverable. But the water has no taste and the children won't play in the fields and three families have already left."

"Yes."

"The network is lying."

Drenna felt something shift in her chest. Not surprise — she'd known this for weeks. Relief. The relief of hearing someone else say the thing she'd been carrying alone. "The network isn't lying. The network is reporting what it can measure. What's happening here is outside its parameters. The instruments are working. They're just measuring the wrong thing."

Vaelen looked at her. Not the warm look. Not the grin. The other thing — the focused, cold, slightly frightening version of him that she'd seen once before, when she'd mentioned the water. The version that took problems seriously enough to put the charm away.

"What do you think is happening?" he asked.

She'd prepared for this question. She'd been preparing for it since she was fourteen, since the first deployment came to her community and measured everything and changed nothing. She had data. She had cross-references. She had four communities' worth of observations logged in the journal under her arm.

But what she said was: "My father bonded with his creature when he was seventeen. They were together for twenty-three years. Three years ago, the network in our settlement weakened — slowly, the way it always does, the way it's doing here. The bond didn't break. It's still there. You can see the lichen. You can see the pulse. By every measure, the bond is intact."

She stopped. Swallowed.

"But my father is gone. He's sitting in his house forty minutes east of here with a bond that's still pulsing and a creature that's still waiting and he hasn't spoken a full sentence in two years. The bond is intact. The person isn't." She held his gaze. "That's what's happening. The network says it's functional. The people inside it are disappearing. And no one is measuring the people."

Vaelen didn't respond immediately. She watched him take the information — not just the data, but the cost of the data, the fact that she'd handed him something personal and precise and paid for — and hold it without deflecting into analysis. He didn't say I'm sorry. He didn't say that must be hard. He stood in a field that should have been leaning toward them and wasn't, and he let the silence sit.

Then he said: "Show me the other communities."

"What?"

"You said the instruments are measuring the wrong thing. You've been measuring the right thing — the people, the behavior, the gaps between data and reality. Show me what you've found. All of it. Not just this community."

Drenna opened her journal. Her hands were steady. She was a person whose hands were always steady, who had learned very young that the world did not have time for hands that shook, that shaking hands were a luxury for people whose fathers were still present and whose futures were still being decided by someone other than themselves.

She showed him everything.

· · ·

That night, while the team ate at the communal table and Jorath argued about supply logistics and Sorvyn drank his tea and Caeleth wrote his earnest, hopeful notes, Drenna sat on her bedroll in the guest quarters and opened the other document.

Not her field journal. The other one. The one she kept in a separate binding, without a label, in the inner pocket of her pack where nobody would think to look. Not because it was secret — if someone found it, they'd see only notes, references, half-formed arguments. But the notes had a shape. The references had a direction. And the arguments, half-formed as they were, pointed toward a conclusion that the Solvarei would not welcome and the Amarith would like even less.

She wrote by the light of a small lamp. Her handwriting was different in this document — tighter, faster, less careful. The field journal was a record. This was a weapon being built.

Community four. Same pattern. Node declining within parameters. Water loss of taste (4 weeks). Field output dropping but within seasonal variance. Specialist assessment: cyclical. Actual assessment: the network is measuring itself and finding itself adequate while the people connected to it are disappearing.

Cross-reference: Communities 1, 2, and 3 showed identical sequence. Water first. Then fields. Then behavioral changes — children withdrawing, bonds flattening, communal patterns degrading. In every case, institutional response came AFTER the community had already adapted to the decline. Response was assessment, not intervention. Assessment confirmed the instruments. Instruments confirmed the assessment. The loop is closed. The people are outside it.

The pattern reader (V.) confirmed today what I've observed across four sites: the network's self-reporting does not match ground reality. He used the word "gap." He's right. The gap is the story. The gap is what I'll build the argument around.

He asked to see my data from the other communities. I showed him. He didn't flinch. He didn't deflect. He asked the right questions. He's the first institutional practitioner I've worked with who treats community observation as data rather than anecdote.

I need to be careful. He's useful. I can't let useful become something else.

She paused. Read the last line. Crossed it out.

Not because it was wrong. Because it revealed too much, even to herself.

She put the document away. She lay on her bedroll. Outside, the settlement was quiet in the way that settlements in declining regions were quiet — not peaceful, not restful. The silence of a place that had once been louder and couldn't remember when the volume began to drop.

She thought about her father's hand. The reach that wasn't a reach. The creature's sound.

She thought about Vaelen standing in the field, his head tilted, reading something in the earth that nobody else could see. The way he'd said the network is lying — not dramatically, not with revelation. With the flat pragmatism of a man stating a problem he intended to solve.

She thought about the gap. The story she was building. The rooms where decisions were made, where she would stand one day with her frontier threadwork on her wrist and her elite Solcarin credential on her record and her voice — her real voice, the one she'd learned to modulate and calibrate and perform — finally saying the thing she'd known since she was twelve years old:

Nobody is coming. The system is not designed to save us. If we are going to be saved, we will have to do it ourselves.

She closed her eyes. Sleep came slowly. When it came, she dreamed of Mirathyn fields leaning toward a child who was no longer there.
