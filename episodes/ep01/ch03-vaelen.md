# Episode One — Chapter Three
## The Node
*Vaelen*

*First Draft*

---

The node woke him.

Not a sound. Nodes didn't make sounds, not in the way anyone who wasn't a pattern reader would understand the word. But something had shifted in the small hours — a change in the rhythm he felt through the floor of the wayhouse, through the stone foundation, through the Amarvel roots that ran beneath this settlement like veins beneath skin. He'd been asleep and then he wasn't, and the transition had the quality of a finger pressed against his collarbone. Not pain. Pressure. Attention demanded.

He lay still. The wayhouse was dark. Sorvyn breathing slowly by the door. Jorath's silence — Jorath was a silent sleeper, which had unnerved Vaelen on their first deployment together until he'd realized the man simply refused to waste energy even unconsciously. Caeleth's breathing was faster, shallow, the breathing of someone caught in a dream he wasn't enjoying. Vaelen thought about waking him and didn't. The boy needed sleep more than rescue from whatever his mind was doing to him.

Drenna's bed was empty. He'd noticed.

He'd noticed the way he noticed everything — automatically, without decision, the information arriving the way light arrives through an open window. She'd left before he did. She'd come back while he was at the node, her boots carrying forty minutes' worth of a different soil — darker red, more clay, the mineral content of ground that sat above a weaker section of the Amarvel. She'd been east. She hadn't told anyone.

He filed it. That was what he did with information — filed it, held it, let it sit until the pattern it belonged to made itself visible. Vaelen's mind had been doing this since he was old enough to walk. His mother had called it his quiet, the way he'd go still at the table and his eyes would move and she'd know he was reading something nobody else could see. At the academy they'd called it sensitivity. At the Solvarei assessment they'd called it extraordinary. He called it nothing. It was like calling breathing something. It was the thing he did.

The node pulsed again. Off-rhythm. He could feel it the way you feel a stone in your boot — not agonizing, but persistent, occupying attention that should be free for other things.

He got up. Dressed in the dark. His body knew the routine: boots, vest, the field knife he'd carried since his third deployment not because he expected to use it but because the weight of it on his hip was a small certainty in a profession built on uncertainty. He stepped over Sorvyn's outstretched legs — the old man didn't stir, but his breathing changed for one cycle, which meant he'd registered Vaelen's movement and decided it wasn't a threat — and went outside.

The cold hit first. Then the dark. Then the Amarvel.

It was always there, of course. The Amarvel was always there. But during the day, with the noise of people and wind and the settlement's small business, it was a background hum — a river you lived beside and stopped hearing. At night, in silence, the hum became the only thing. Vaelen stood on the wayhouse step and felt it rise through his boots and into the bones of his feet and up his legs into his hips and spine, a vibration so deep it was almost below feeling, like standing on the chest of something enormous that was breathing.

The Amarvel beneath Torven's community was old. Vaelen could tell the age the way a woodworker could tell the age of a beam — by density, by resonance. Old roots vibrated differently from young ones. They were slower. Deeper. They carried more information because they'd carried it longer, and the information had layered and compressed until it had its own texture, its own weight. Reading old Amarvel was like putting your hand flat against a wall and feeling, through the stone, the accumulated years of the house — every fire lit, every meal cooked, every child born, every body laid out for the dead. The Amarvel remembered. Not the way people remembered, with stories and distortion and the gentle erosion of time. The Amarvel remembered like stone remembers. Exactly. Permanently. Without opinion.

He walked to the node.

The shimmer was almost invisible in the dark — just a faint disturbance in the moonlight, as if the air above the concentration point were slightly thicker than the air around it. In a healthy system, the shimmer would pulse gold-green, steady as a heartbeat. This one pulsed, but the rhythm had a hitch in it — a missed beat every eight or nine cycles, like a drummer who kept losing the count. Vaelen had been tracking the hitch since yesterday. It was getting worse. Not faster. Wider. The pause between the eighth and ninth beat was lengthening, as if the Amarvel were gathering its strength for each cycle and needing more time to do it.

He stopped at the node's edge. Closed his eyes.

And the world changed.

Not visually. He wasn't seeing anything — his eyes were shut, his face turned toward the sky. But the Amarvel opened beneath him the way a conversation opens between two people who've been waiting to speak. He felt the roots first — the major trunks running north-south beneath the settlement, thick as old trees, carrying the slow deep pulse that was Amarwen's own rhythm transmitted across hundreds of miles of living tissue. The branches off the trunks, thinner, faster, spreading east and west toward the fields. The capillaries at the edges, hair-thin, fragile, reaching into the soil beneath the Mirathyn rows where they fed and were fed in turn.

Each layer had its own voice. The trunks were bass — slow, resonant, the kind of sound that lives in the floor rather than the air. The branches were midrange, complex, carrying traffic: nutrients moving in one direction, information moving in the other, the constant negotiation between what the community needed and what the Amarvel could provide. The capillaries were high and fast and anxious, the chatter of a frontier where resources were thinning and the edges were pulling back.

Vaelen listened. He listened the way you listen to a sick friend's breathing — not for the sound, but for what the sound was telling you about the body making it.

The trunks were strong. That was good. Whatever was wrong here, the main conduits were intact, still carrying Amarwen's pulse from the deep roots near the Hearthlands out to this frontier node. The signal was tired — Vaelen could feel the fatigue like a taste at the back of his mouth, metallic and thin — but it was arriving. The infrastructure was holding.

The branches were where the trouble started. The east-west network that served the Mirathyn fields was thinner than it should have been — not broken, not damaged, just... less. As if the Amarvel had been slowly pulling resources back from the edges, consolidating toward the trunks the way a cold body pulls blood from the fingers to keep the heart warm. Eighteen months of this. Gradual enough that no single day looked different from the last. But Vaelen could feel the accumulated absence — the places where the branches had been full and were now half-empty, the capillaries that had once reached the farthest field rows and now stopped short by ten, twenty, thirty meters.

The dead zone in the eastern fields wasn't a failure. It was a retreat. The Amarvel was pulling back, and the community was living on what remained, and what remained was enough — technically, on paper, by the instruments' measure — but it was enough the way a half-meal is enough. It stops the hunger without providing what the body needs.

He went deeper.

This was the part that cost him. The surface reading — the trunks, the branches, the general health of the local network — was easy. He could do it while walking, while talking, while eating smokebread one-handed on a shaking carriage. It was like glancing at the weather. But going deep meant letting the Amarvel's information replace his own senses, and that was a transaction with a price. The world above — the cold air, the moonlight, the sound of his own breathing — receded. The Amarvel's world replaced it. And the Amarvel's world was vast and slow and did not care about the small warm thing standing above it trying to listen.

He felt the downstream deficit Sorvyn had asked about. There — south-southwest, maybe forty miles along the main trunk. A draw on the network so steady and sustained that the Torven's node had been compensating for it without the community knowing. The trunk was carrying resources in a direction they shouldn't be flowing, like a river running uphill. Something down there was pulling. Something was taking more than its share, and the Amarvel, in its blind mechanical way, was providing — redistributing, balancing, keeping the signal alive by stealing from the edges to feed whatever was eating from the center.

Vaelen mapped it. The deficit had a shape — not circular, not random. Directional. A line running south-southwest from this node toward... where? He pushed further and the signal blurred. Too far. His range had limits, and at forty miles through stressed Amarvel, the detail dissolved into static. But the direction was clear. Something was pulling. Something was hungry. And the Amarvel was feeding it because the Amarvel fed everything that asked, because that was what the Amarvel was *for*, because the roots didn't distinguish between a community that needed sustenance and a deficit that would swallow everything it was given.

He came up. Gasped. The surface world flooded back — cold air, the shock of hearing his own heartbeat again after the Amarvel's slower rhythm. His hands were shaking. They always shook after a deep read. He closed them into fists, pressed them against his thighs, and waited for the trembling to stop.

It took longer than it used to.

He stood in the dark and thought about what Drenna had shown him. Four communities. The same sequence. The gap between what the Amarvel reported and what was happening to the people. She'd walked those communities, talked to those families, tracked the decline with a method that his training hadn't given him and couldn't replicate — the method of someone who'd grown up inside the thing she was studying. Her observation and his reading overlapped like two transparencies laid one on top of the other, and where they overlapped, the picture sharpened.

The Amarvel was retreating. It was pulling resources from the frontier edges toward the main trunks, and from the trunks toward somewhere south-southwest. The communities on the edges were being starved — slowly, within parameters, technically recoverable — while the Amarvel fed something deeper in the network that Vaelen couldn't see yet. The instruments measured the edges and found decline within seasonal variance. The instruments were correct. The instruments were also useless, because the problem wasn't the edges. The problem was what was pulling.

He needed to follow the trunk south-southwest. He needed to get closer to whatever was drawing the Amarvel's resources. But that was forty miles through thinning network, and he had a community here that needed help today, not next week.

Right. Today first.

---

Dawn came gray and windless. Vaelen had slept another two hours after the reading — not enough, but enough to make the headache manageable. He'd learned years ago that the body's protest after a deep read wasn't negotiable. You paid the cost or the cost took what it was owed without asking. A headache behind the left eye. A faint ringing that would fade by midday. The metallic taste that stayed longer.

He was eating boiled grain at the communal table — the grain was adequate, the water was nothing, he drank it without flinching because flinching accomplished nothing — when Sorvyn sat down across from him.

"You went out last night."

"The node's rhythm shifted. I wanted to track it."

"You look terrible."

"Thank you, Sorvyn."

"The deep reading." Not a question. Sorvyn had been with him long enough to recognize the signs — the shadows under the eyes darkening by a shade, the left hand cradling the cup too carefully, the way Vaelen held his head slightly still to keep the headache from shifting. "You could have waited until morning."

"The Amarvel's quieter at night. Less traffic. The deep structure's easier to read."

"The deep structure is always there. Your body isn't always up for the conversation." Sorvyn drank his tea. "What did you find?"

Vaelen looked at his grain. At the table. At the settlement waking up around them — Torven's daughter carrying water, a child feeding a small furred creature that was sitting on a fence post with its tail wrapped around the railing, a man sweeping the path between houses with the slow, even strokes of someone who'd been sweeping the same path every morning for decades. The community was alive. It was moving. It was doing the things communities do. And underneath all of it, the Amarvel was pulling back, one capillary at a time, like a tide going out so slowly that the fish didn't notice until they were gasping on dry sand.

"Something's drawing on the trunk line south-southwest. Forty miles or more. Steady pull, sustained. This node's been compensating — redistributing its own resources to feed the deficit. That's why the eastern fields died. The Amarvel chose the trunk over the edges."

"The Amarvel chose?"

"The Amarvel balanced. The way it always does — resources flow toward the greatest demand. The trunk's demand is greater because something downstream is taking. The edges starve. It's not malice. It's physics." He paused. "Or it's the closest thing the Amarvel has to physics."

Sorvyn was quiet for a moment. "Can you fix the balance?"

"I can redirect the local flow. Reinforce the branches, strengthen the capillary connections to the fields. Buy them time — a growing season, maybe two. But if the downstream pull doesn't stop, the Amarvel will redirect again. I'd be bailing water from a boat with a hole in it."

"Then bail. Give them the season. We'll figure out the hole."

Vaelen smiled. It was, he was aware, a good smile — the kind that made people feel steady. He'd been producing it since he was sixteen and discovered that the right expression at the right moment could change the temperature of a room. He didn't think about it anymore. The smile happened and it did its work and the fact that it was also genuine didn't make it less practiced. He could be glad and deliberate at the same time. Most people could. He was simply more aware of it than most.

"Get Caeleth," he said. "He's going to help me with the branch work. And find Drenna — tell her I need her at the eastern field edge by midmorning. She knows something about the soil out there that I need."

Sorvyn stood. "And Jorath?"

"Tell Jorath the community's grain store needs auditing and he should negotiate the resupply rates before the next trader comes through. He'll be happier than if I gave him a birthday."

Sorvyn almost smiled. It was the closest Vaelen had seen in days. Then the old man walked away with his hands clasped behind his back, steady pace, and Vaelen watched him go and thought: *he trusts me completely and I am not at all certain I deserve it*.

He finished his grain. The headache was fading. The metallic taste was not. He went to work.

---

The branch work was the closest thing Vaelen did to physical labor, and it was nothing like what anyone watching would expect.

He knelt at the junction where the main trunk line passed beneath the settlement's western edge — a spot he'd identified by feel, where the root structure was thickest and the branches diverged toward the fields. From the outside, he was a man kneeling in the dirt with his palms flat on the ground and his eyes closed. From the inside, he was holding a conversation.

The Amarvel didn't think. He'd made that mistake early in his training — projecting intent onto a system that had none. The Amarvel responded. It flowed where the signal was strongest, pulled back where the signal weakened, redistributed along gradients that were as mindless and as elegant as water finding its level. Vaelen's gift was that he could feel those gradients — and, with effort, shift them.

He found the branch that fed the western fields. Felt along it — here, where the flow was strong, and here, where it thinned, and here, at the capillary level, where the Mirathyn roots were reaching down into soil that the Amarvel had half-abandoned. The capillaries were still alive. They were just... quiet. Waiting for a signal that had stopped arriving.

Vaelen gave them the signal.

It wasn't magic — or rather, it was exactly magic, in the way that everything the Amarvel did was magic, but it felt more like coaxing than commanding. He leaned his awareness into the branch, pressed against the gradient, and created a small bright insistence: *here. Flow here. This matters.* Not in words. In the language the Amarvel understood, which was the language of need — pressure, direction, the warmth of living roots reaching for sustenance.

The branch resisted. The Amarvel had been pulling back from this section for months, and the gradient had settled into a pattern that wanted to be maintained. Changing it was like pushing a boulder uphill — not impossible, but the boulder had opinions about where it preferred to sit.

"Vaelen?"

Caeleth. Vaelen held the read with one part of his mind — the trained part, the part that could maintain a connection the way a juggler maintains three balls while having a conversation — and opened his eyes with the other.

"Kneel here." He indicated a spot two meters to his left. "Put your palms flat. Close your eyes."

Caeleth knelt. His hands pressed the earth. His eyes closed with the exaggerated concentration of someone who was terrified of doing it wrong.

"What do you feel?"

A pause. "Cold. The ground is cold."

"Below that."

A longer pause. Caeleth's brow furrowed. His fingers pressed harder, as if pressure would help. It wouldn't — the Amarvel didn't respond to force — but Vaelen didn't correct him. The boy needed to find his own way in.

"Something... humming? Very low. Like — like standing near a wall that has bees behind it. You can't hear them exactly. But you know they're there."

Vaelen felt something ease in his chest. A hum. Bees behind a wall. It wasn't how he'd describe it — for Vaelen the Amarvel was more like a river than bees, more like current than sound — but it was right. The boy was feeling the root trunk. His sensitivity was real, if undeveloped — rough where Vaelen's was precise, blunt where his was sharp, but present. The seed was there.

"Good. That's the main trunk. Now — can you feel where it branches? There should be a point somewhere between us where the hum splits. One direction keeps going north-south. The other turns toward the fields."

Caeleth was quiet. His face was working — the effort visible, the wanting visible, the desperate desire to be good at this visible in every line of him. Vaelen recognized it. He'd been that boy once. Twenty years ago, kneeling in a different field, pressing his hands against the ground, wanting so badly to feel what his instructor was describing that his jaw ached from clenching.

"I think..." Caeleth's hands shifted slightly. "There. It's — it's like the hum changes direction. Bends."

"That's the junction. You're on it." Vaelen kept his voice level. Casual. As if finding a trunk-branch junction on your second deployment was ordinary. It wasn't. The boy's sensitivity was better than half the practitioners Vaelen had worked with, and those practitioners had years of training. "Now I'm going to push more flow into that branch. You're going to feel it — a change in the vibration, like turning up the volume on the bees. Your job is to tell me when it reaches the field edge. Can you feel that far?"

"I don't know."

"Neither did I, the first time. Try."

Vaelen closed his eyes again and leaned into the gradient. The boulder-on-a-hill resistance was still there, but he'd been working it all morning and the pattern was loosening. He pushed. The branch brightened — not visibly, not in any way Caeleth or anyone watching would see, but in the language of the Amarvel, the flow increased. Resources moving west toward the fields. Capillaries waking. The thin silent threads reaching into the soil beginning to hum again, faintly, like a sleeping instrument being lifted from its case.

"It's moving," Caeleth said. His voice had changed. The anxiety was gone, replaced by something Vaelen knew very well — the wonder. The first time you felt the Amarvel respond to your presence, the first time you understood that you were inside a living thing so large it made oceans look shallow, and it had noticed you. "I can feel it — it's spreading, like — like water filling channels. The bees are getting louder toward the fields. It's—"

He stopped. His eyes opened. His face was strange — half awe, half something Vaelen couldn't read.

"What?"

"It's beautiful," Caeleth said. He sounded embarrassed by the word. He shouldn't have been. It was the right word. The only word.

"It is," Vaelen said. And for a moment — a brief, unguarded moment that he didn't choose and couldn't have replicated — his voice carried something that wasn't the warmth he deployed or the steadiness he maintained or the competence he projected. It carried the thing underneath all of that: the raw, helpless love for the thing he could feel beneath his hands. The Amarvel. The living network. The vast breathing fact of it. He'd loved it since the first time he'd felt it, the way some people love the ocean — not because it was kind, not because it cared, but because it was so much bigger than he was that standing inside it made every small and broken thing about him irrelevant.

Then the moment passed. He was Vaelen again. The control returned like a door closing. Smooth. Invisible.

"Keep feeling the edge," he said. "Tell me when the flow reaches the last field row."

Caeleth closed his eyes and went back in. Vaelen watched him for a second — the concentration, the wonder, the transparent wanting — and thought: *I was you. I remember being you. I remember the part that came after.*

He turned back to the work.

---

By midmorning the western branches were running stronger than they had in months. Vaelen could feel the difference in the soil — a warmth that hadn't been there, a responsiveness in the capillaries, the Mirathyn roots reaching deeper because the Amarvel was offering something worth reaching for. It wouldn't hold forever. The downstream pull was still there, still drawing, and within a season the gradient would reassert itself and the branches would thin again. But a season was a season. A growing cycle. Food on tables. Something instead of nothing.

He found Drenna at the eastern field edge, as he'd asked. She was crouched in the dirt, her fingers in the soil, her journal closed beside her. Not writing. Looking. She had the expression of someone reading a sentence she'd read before and finding a word she'd missed.

"The soil's warmer," she said when he reached her. "Since this morning. It wasn't warm yesterday."

"The branch work. I reinforced the western flow. The eastern fields are further from the trunk — I'll need to work them separately this afternoon. But the western boost should have raised the ground temperature across the whole settlement by a fraction."

She stood. Brushed the dirt from her hands. "You did that. From the junction."

"That's the job."

"That's not a normal deployment outcome. I've been on three rotations. None of the practitioners I've worked with could affect the ground temperature from a trunk junction."

Vaelen didn't answer immediately. She was looking at him with the directness that was becoming familiar — the way she looked at everything, taking it in whole, deciding what it was worth. It was uncomfortable, being looked at like that. Most people didn't look at him at all — they looked at the warmth, the grin, the version of him that arrived in the room first and did most of the talking. Drenna looked past that. She'd been looking past it since she'd said *you're the pattern reader* on the first day.

"The eastern fields," he said. "You told me the last specialist called the decline cyclical. Seasonal variance. But you said the girl stopped playing here because it felt wrong. I need to know: how long ago did the girl stop?"

"Seven weeks. Before the eastern fields died visibly. Before the yield data showed anything."

"Seven weeks." He knelt. Put his palms flat on the dead field soil. Even through the deep reading's aftereffects, he could feel it — the capillaries here were gone. Not retreated. Gone. The Amarvel had withdrawn from this section completely, the way a body abandons a frostbitten toe. The soil was alive in the ordinary way — bacteria, minerals, the chemistry of dirt — but the Amarvel's presence was zero. Not a hum. Not even silence. An absence.

"The girl felt it seven weeks ago," he said. "The instruments wouldn't have caught it for another three. The yield data another two after that. She was five weeks ahead of the instruments."

"Because the instruments measure the Amarvel. The girl was measuring something else."

Vaelen looked up at her. "What?"

"I don't know." She said it simply. Without frustration. A problem she hadn't solved yet but intended to. "The specialists measure the Amarvel — root density, flow rates, capillary reach. You measure the Amarvel, with more sensitivity than anyone I've met. But the girl wasn't measuring the Amarvel. She was standing barefoot in the dirt and she felt something change before the Amarvel's own readings showed any change. Whatever she felt, it's upstream of the Amarvel. It's the thing that makes the Amarvel retreat."

Vaelen sat back on his heels. The headache was returning. The metallic taste was strong. He'd been in the Amarvel's space too much in the last eighteen hours and his body was informing him, in its calm persistent way, that the conversation was becoming costly.

But Drenna was right. He could feel it — not with his training, not with the sensitivity that the Solvarei had tested and measured and called extraordinary. With something older. Something that wasn't in any curriculum because nobody had thought to teach it, because nobody had a name for the thing that a seven-year-old girl could feel through her bare feet and a trained pattern reader couldn't see in the Amarvel's own data.

The gap. The thing Drenna had been tracking across four communities. The disconnect between the network's self-reporting and the reality on the ground. It wasn't just a measurement problem. It was a category problem. The instruments — including him — were measuring the Amarvel. But the Amarvel wasn't the thing that was failing. Something was failing the Amarvel. Something upstream. Something that the roots carried but didn't originate, the way a river carries a poison that began miles from the water.

"I need to come back tonight," he said. "The deep read. I need to go further this time — past the local network, past the trunk line, down toward whatever's pulling from the south-southwest."

"You look like the last deep read nearly broke you."

"Nearly is the operative word."

"Vaelen." She said his name the way she said everything — directly, with a weight that stopped him from deflecting. "Whatever you're sensing at forty miles is also the thing that a seven-year-old felt through her feet. You don't have to go further. You have to go different. Deeper doesn't mean more distance. It means a different way of listening."

He stared at her. She was twenty-one years old. She had three months of Solvarei rotation under her belt and a field journal full of observations that no trained practitioner had thought to make. And she'd just told him something about his own gift that nobody had ever said to him before — not his instructors, not his assessors, not the Solvarei practitioners who'd worked alongside him for years.

He wanted to argue. He wanted to explain that his method was rigorous, tested, the product of a decade of refinement. He wanted to say that distance *was* the way you found things in the Amarvel — you followed the roots, you traced the gradients, you pushed until the signal resolved.

Instead he thought about the girl in the field. Standing barefoot. Not reaching further. Not pushing deeper. Just... present. Open. Feeling something change before the Amarvel's own systems registered the change.

"I don't know how to do that," he said. It was, he realized, the most honest thing he'd said in months. Possibly years. The admission landed in the air between them and sat there, unadorned, the way Sorvyn's declarations sat — plain, without performance.

Drenna looked at him for a long moment. Then she knelt beside him and put her hands flat on the dead soil of the eastern fields. "Neither do I," she said. "So we figure it out."

---

He went back to the node at midnight.

The settlement was asleep. The wayhouse silent. He hadn't told Sorvyn — the old man would have insisted on staying awake, watching, being present in case Vaelen came back from the deep read badly, and Vaelen didn't want to be watched. There was something about the deep read that was private — not because it was secret but because the person he became inside the Amarvel was not the person anyone knew. Not the warmth. Not the grin. Not the competent practitioner who made everyone feel steady. The person inside the Amarvel was small. Overwhelmed. A single nervous system trying to hold a conversation with something that spanned a continent.

He knelt at the node. Closed his eyes.

And didn't push.

Drenna's words. *Deeper doesn't mean more distance. It means a different way of listening.* He didn't know what that meant. But he knew that his method — extending along the roots, following the gradients, pushing toward the source — had given him distance and not clarity. He'd felt the pull at forty miles and it had been a blur. More distance would give him more blur.

So he stopped trying to travel. He stopped following the roots south-southwest. He stopped pushing.

He sat inside the node and listened.

The Amarvel's rhythm surrounded him. The familiar layers — trunk bass, branch midrange, capillary chatter. The tired pulse. The missed beats. The downstream pull, steady and patient and hungry. He let it all wash over him without reaching for any of it. Not reading. Not analyzing. Present.

Minutes passed. Maybe longer. The cold crept through his trousers and into his knees. His headache pulsed in time with the node. The metallic taste was back. He breathed. He waited.

And then he felt it.

Not in the Amarvel. Not through the roots. Not in any layer of the network he'd been trained to read. He felt it in the space between the layers — in the silence between the trunk's bass pulses, in the gaps between the branch traffic, in the pauses where the capillaries went quiet before cycling again. There was something in the silence. Not a signal. Not an absence. A *texture*. As if the silence itself had changed quality — the way a room feels different when someone has just left it, the air still holding the shape of their presence.

Something had been here. Or something was here, so large and so slow that his senses couldn't distinguish it from the background the way a fish can't distinguish water from nothing. He was swimming in it. He'd been swimming in it since they arrived. The wrongness — the muted colors, the tasteless water, the child's smile that didn't travel — wasn't a symptom of the Amarvel's retreat. It was the thing the Amarvel was retreating *from*.

His left hand closed into a fist. He couldn't stop it.

The silence between the Amarvel's pulses had a feel. He tried to hold onto it — to examine it, to give it shape — and it slipped away, the way a dream dissolves when you try to look at it directly. It was there. It was not there. It was the texture of the space where something living had been replaced by something that was very, very good at pretending to be living. A copy so precise that the instruments couldn't tell the difference. A simulation so faithful that the Amarvel's own systems processed it as real.

But the girl in the field had known. Her bare feet in the dirt, seven weeks ahead of every instrument. She'd felt the pretending. She'd felt the difference between a living field and a field that was doing a perfect impression of being alive.

Vaelen opened his eyes.

The moon had moved. He didn't know how long he'd been under. His hands were shaking — not the normal post-read tremor but something deeper, a vibration that lived in the bones rather than the muscles. His breath came in short pulls, too fast, and he made himself slow it by counting. Three in. Hold. Three out. The wayhouse. The settlement. The pale wood of the houses in the moonlight. Real things. Solid things. Things that were what they were and didn't pretend to be anything else.

He thought about Torven's grandmother. The quiet that lasted three years in one valley and came back on its own. A node that failed and recovered. An old story about an old wound.

This wasn't an old wound. This was something that had never happened before — or if it had, nobody who'd experienced it had survived to describe it in a way that made it into the old stories. This was the Amarvel retreating from something it couldn't process, and the something filling the space that the Amarvel left behind with a silence that looked, from the outside, exactly like normal.

He stood. His knees cracked. The cold had settled into his joints and wasn't leaving. He walked back to the wayhouse with his fists clenched and the taste of metal thick on his tongue and the knowledge, new and certain and terrible, that the grandmother's stories were about a different world. That the old ways of understanding were a map drawn for a country that no longer existed. That what he'd felt in the silence between the pulses was not a failing node, not a cyclical decline, not a problem a team could fix with branch work and good intentions.

It was something else. Something new. Something patient.

He didn't sleep. He sat on his bed in the dark wayhouse and listened to his team breathe — Sorvyn steady, Jorath silent, Caeleth turning in dreams — and he held his left hand in his right and tried to stop the shaking and couldn't.

In the eastern fields, where the Amarvel had pulled back to nothing, the Mirathyn stalks stood in the moonlight. Upright. Leaves green. To anyone walking past in the dark, they would have looked healthy. They would have looked fine. They would have looked, from the outside, exactly like they were supposed to look.

Almost exactly.
