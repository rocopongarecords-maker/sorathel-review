# Sorathel Writing Rules
*Each rule exists because we broke it. Each is a scar from a specific error.*

## 1. Show, Never Interpret
NEVER explain what a gesture, look, or silence means. No "the look said:" followed by interpretation. Describe the physical action. Stop. Trust the reader.

## 2. No Thematic Vocabulary in Prose
NEVER use: "perform," "institution," "authenticity," "identity," "structure," "scaffolding," "framework." These are analytical terms for planning, not character thoughts. Characters think in concrete, sensory terms.

## 3. Characters Contradict Themselves
Every POV character must show at least one moment per chapter that contradicts their dominant trait. Drenna unstrategic. Vaelen ungraceful. Sorvyn doubting. Jorath generous.

## 4. Specific Emotions, Not Expected Ones
Before writing an emotional beat: is this how THIS person would feel, or how MOST people would feel? Drenna doesn't feel shame about her father — she carries him as proof of resilience.

## 5. Same Trait, New Expression
A character trait should never be expressed the same way twice. Jorath can't resist a problem — show it through a route he's mapped, a fix he sketches at dinner, a challenge he takes on for no reason, a moment of surprising resourcefulness. Money is ONE trait, not the defining one. Not the same bit repeated.

## 6. Professional Infrastructure Exists
Practitioners on deployment have protocols, infrastructure, perks. They don't sleep in the client's house. Think like a consultant, not a guest.

## 7. Characters Don't Have Reader Knowledge
Characters can only know what their experience and culture gives them. The systemic Ethura pattern is unprecedented — no character has our vocabulary for it.

## 8. No Jacket-Blurb Sentences
If a sentence could work as a book review quote, it's summarizing instead of showing. Cut it. Let the reader write that sentence in their own mind.

## 9. Pre-Chapter Checklist
Before writing each chapter, review: (1) outline for agreed beats, (2) Story Bible for continuity, (3) these Writing Rules, (4) Anomaly Bible if the chapter touches the Ethura, node decline, network anomalies, or Hushing, (5) discussion notes for specific details.

## 10. Problem Solvers, Not Bureaucrats
These characters are field practitioners. They DO things. If a character's main activity in a scene is writing or filing, something is wrong. Show them in the dirt.

## 11. No Analytical Vocabulary in Prose
Words like "frameworks," "perform," "institution," "structure," "scaffolding," "unidimensional" belong to planning, not character voices.

## 12. Drenna Has No Political Document
NEVER write Drenna with a secret document, separate binding, unlabeled notebook, or any physical prop representing her ambition. Her political arc lives in behavior. Zombie idea — do not resurrect.

## 13. Drenna Always Shows Both
Every Drenna scene must show compassion AND ambition simultaneously. She feels AND files. She grieves AND assesses what the grief is worth. Neither cancels the other. She is Tabata Amaral.

## GRRM Additions

## 14. Texture Scenes
Include at least one scene per episode that exists for world texture, not plot. A meal. A walk. A creature being tended. The reader needs to LIVE in Sorathel, not just visit during crises.

## 15. Libertarian Costs
When showing voluntary systems working, also show the costs. Freedom without structure means some people fall through. The frontier is structurally resilient but individually dangerous. Show both.

## 16. Magic Sensory Vocabulary
The Amarvel should be described with expanding sensory language — not the same metaphors repeated. Bass/midrange/chatter is established. Find new textures: temperature, taste, pressure, the feeling of standing near something vast.

## 17. Surface Behaviors Over Interior Analysis
Show what characters DO, not what they think about doing. If Vaelen is controlling his expression, describe the micro-movements — don't write "he controlled his expression." The reader should see the effort, not be told about it.

## 19. Don't Narrate the Mechanism
Never explain WHY a character is doing the thing while they're doing it. If Vaelen smiles at someone, don't follow it with a paragraph about how the smile is a tool he learned at sixteen. Show the smile. Show what it does to the room. The reader assembles the psychology. The moment you narrate the emotional machinery, you've replaced the reader's insight with yours. Cut every sentence that reads like a character study of the person whose head we're inside.

## 20. Populate the World
Every chapter needs at least one person the reader hasn't met — not a named character with an arc, but a FACE. Someone who says one line, or does one thing, or has one detail that makes them stick. A handler who holds a bird a certain way. A vendor who remembers the team from last time. The world is full of people. If a scene only contains the main cast, the world feels like a stage set.

## 21. Feed the Reader
When characters eat, let them EAT. The taste, the texture, the noise of chewing, the argument about seasoning. Shared meals are where relationships become real. Don't skip the meal to get to the conversation — the meal IS the conversation. Martin understood: food grounds fantasy in the body. A reader who's tasted Sorvyn's tuber lives in Sorathel.

## 22. Earn the Silence
If a chapter ends on a heavy image or a thematic echo, it must be SHORT. One line. Two at most. The longer a closing goes, the more it tells the reader what to feel. End on a concrete image and stop. No summative paragraphs. No "and he carried the weight of..." closings. The chapter did the work or it didn't. Trust it.

## 23. Humor Through Collision
Comedy comes from character incompatibility, not from information delivery. Jorath calculating fares is exposition. Jorath calculating fares while Sorvyn is trying to describe a recipe and they're talking past each other — that's a scene. Humor needs two energies in the same space, colliding. One character being funny alone is a bit. Two characters being themselves in proximity is comedy.

## 18. The Ethura Never Attacks — It Fills
The Ethura is the ABSENCE of Amarwen experienced as a presence. It does not invade, mimic, simulate, chase, or retreat the Amarvel. When Amarwen withdraws, the Ethura is what fills the space she left. The shape of life persists — the mold remains — but the animating force is gone. Characters sense this as wrongness, hollowness, weight in the silence. Never write the Ethura as an active agent. Always read `bible/anomaly-bible.md` before writing any scene involving the Ethura, node decline, network anomalies, or Hushing.

## 24. Earn Every Simile — HARD LIMIT
One simile per scene break, maximum. Three per chapter, absolute ceiling. A simile is ANY comparison using "like," "as if," "as though," "the way [X] does [Y]," or "the way you'd [X]." Count them. If over limit, replace with direct description. The pattern "the way he did everything" is a simile — cut it, name the specific behavior. "Smelled like X" is a simile — cut it, describe the smell directly. "The way a room feels when..." is a simile — cut it, describe the room. After drafting, grep for "like ", "as if", "as though", "the way" and count. If over 3, cut until under. No exceptions. Name things directly. A simile should SHOCK — if it doesn't stop the reader, it's filler.

## 25. Build the World the Reader Sees
Every new location needs physical description — architecture, light, smell, sound, the texture of the street under boots. Every new person needs a face — one or two details that stick, that the reader carries. The reader can't love a world they can't picture. Don't skip the city to get to the conversation — the city IS the story. A street has width, has vendors, has noise. A room has walls, has light from a specific direction, has the smell of what was cooked there last. A person has a jaw, has hands, has the way they stand when they think nobody's watching. Ground every scene in the physical world first. Then let the characters move through it.
